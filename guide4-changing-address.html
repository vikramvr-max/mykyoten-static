Why Address Changes Break Things More Than You'd Expect In most countries, changing your address means updating your mail forwarding and telling a few companies. In Japan, your registered address determines: Which city hall manages your resident record Which health insurance office handles your enrollment Which pension office tracks your contributions Which tax office collects your residence tax Where official government notices get sent Which ward or city provides your garbage bags (yes, really) Your address is not just where you sleep. It's an administrative key that connects you to specific government offices and systems. When you move and don't update properly, these systems start pointing to the wrong place. Your health insurance gets sent to your old address. Your tax bills go missing. Your new city hall has no record of you existing. The Two Types of Moves (The Difference Matters) Type 1: Moving within the same municipality (same city or ward) This is called tenkyo todoke (転居届) - a "moving notification." Type 2: Moving to a different municipality (different city or ward) This requires two steps: tenshutsu todoke (転出届) - "move-out notification" at your old city hall, and tennyu todoke (転入届) - "move-in notification" at your new city hall. Type 2 moves are significantly more complicated because you're transferring your entire resident record from one government to another. Don't forget the separate immigration address notification - and be aware of the 14-day reporting requirements . Moving Within the Same Municipality (The Easy Version) If you're moving within the same city or ward, the process is relatively straightforward. The Physical Process Step-by-Step Move to your new apartment Within 14 days, go to your local city hall Go to the resident registration counter (usually called 住民登録 or 市民課) Tell them you moved within the municipality Fill out a short form with your old address and new address Hand over your residence card They update the address on the back of your residence card by hand You're done The whole process takes 15-30 minutes depending on how busy the office is. You need to bring: Your residence card (required) Something showing your new address (lease contract, move-in confirmation from your landlord) Your My Number Card if you have one (optional but recommended) What Gets Updated Automatically Your resident record updates immediately Your health insurance record updates automatically (but you might receive a new insurance card in the mail with the updated address) Your My Number system updates automatically Your registered seal certificate (if you have one) updates automatically What You Still Need to Update Manually Your bank account address (go to your bank or update online) Your employer's records (tell HR your new address) Your phone contract (most carriers let you update online) Any credit cards or loans Online shopping accounts Postal forwarding service "Your address in Japan isn't just a location. It's the key that unlocks which government office manages your records, which insurance plan you're enrolled in, and where your official mail goes." Moving to a Different Municipality (The Complicated Version) This is where people mess up. Moving to a different city or ward means you're transferring your resident record from one local government to another. This is a two-step process and the timing matters. Step 1: Move-Out Notification at Your Old City Hall Before you move (or within 14 days after), you need to go to your old city hall and submit a move-out notification (tenshutsu todoke). Go to your old city hall Go to the resident registration counter Tell them you're moving out of the municipality Fill out the move-out form with your current address and your planned new address They'll give you a tenshutsu shomeisho (転出証明書) - a "certificate of moving out" This certificate is critical. You need it to complete registration at your new city hall. You need to bring: Your residence card Your My Number Card (if you have one) Your health insurance card (they'll collect this because your old municipality is no longer your insurance administrator) Timing note: You can submit this move-out notification up to 14 days before your move date or within 14 days after. Most people do it a few days before moving or on moving day itself. Step 2: Move-In Notification at Your New City Hall Within 14 days of moving into your new address, you must go to your new city hall and complete move-in registration (tennyu todoke). Go to your new city hall Go to the resident registration counter Tell them you're moving in from another municipality Hand them the tenshutsu shomeisho certificate from your old city hall Fill out the move-in form with your new address They update your residence card with the new address They automatically enroll you in the new municipality's health insurance You're done (at city hall) You need to bring: Your residence card The tenshutsu shomeisho certificate from your old city hall (required) Your My Number Card (if you have one) Proof of new address (lease contract) The new city hall will issue you a new health insurance card within 1-2 weeks. Until it arrives, you can still see a doctor, but you may need to explain the situation. This is not a suggestion. It's a legal requirement under the Basic Resident Registration Act. Miss this deadline and you can be fined up to ¥50,000 (though in practice, fines for first-time violations are rare unless you ignore it for months). The real consequences are worse than fines: Your health insurance might lapse or get backdated incorrectly Official notices (tax bills, visa renewal reminders) get sent to your old address Your new municipality has no record of you existing, so you can't access local services Banks and employers get confused when your registered address doesn't match their records Just do it within 14 days. Set a reminder on your phone for day 10 if you need to. What Updates Automatically and What Doesn't This is the part that confuses everyone. Updates Automatically (No Action Needed) Your resident record in the national database Your My Number system record Your health insurance enrollment (but you receive a new card in the mail) Your pension record (transfers between offices) Your residence tax jurisdiction (your new city starts collecting) Does NOT Update Automatically (You Must Do This Manually) Banks: You need to go to your bank branch or update online. Bring your updated residence card. Some banks like Japan Post Bank let you update online, others require in-person visits. Employers: Tell your HR department your new address immediately. They need it for tax withholding and year-end tax adjustment. Phone contracts: Docomo, AU, Softbank all have online address update forms. Takes 5 minutes. Credit cards: Most Japanese credit card companies let you update your address through their app or website. Utilities: If you're moving to a new apartment, you're setting up new accounts anyway. If you're moving within the same building (rare), notify the utility companies. Immigration: If you move, you're supposed to notify immigration within 14 days. This is separate from the city hall notification. You can do this online through the immigration website or by mailing a notification form. Many people skip this and immigration doesn't actively enforce it, but technically it's required. "Change your address wrong - or late - and you'll spend months trying to untangle the mess." Edge Case Scenarios That Cause Problems Scenario 1: What If I Move on a Weekend or Holiday? City halls are closed on weekends and national holidays. You cannot register your address change on these days. The workaround: The 14-day deadline counts calendar days, not business days, so weekends and holidays count against you. If you move on a Saturday, your 14-day window includes that Saturday and Sunday. You need to go to city hall sometime in the following 12 business days (roughly). Practical tip: If you're planning a move, try to move on a weekday or early in a weekend so you have flexibility to visit city hall during business hours within the deadline. Scenario 2: What If I Forgot to Get the Certificate from My Old City Hall? If you moved to a different municipality and forgot to get the tenshutsu shomeisho certificate from your old city hall before moving, you have options: Option 1: Go back to your old city hall in person and get the certificate. Inconvenient if you moved far away. Option 2: Request the certificate by mail. Most city halls allow this. You need to send a request form (available on their website), a copy of your residence card, and a return envelope with postage. This takes 1-2 weeks. Option 3 (sometimes works): Some city halls can issue the certificate remotely if your new city hall requests it directly. Ask at your new city hall if they can do this. Not all offices cooperate, but it's worth asking. Scenario 3: What If My Landlord Won't Give Me a Move-In Confirmation Document? Most city halls want to see proof of your new address when you register. This is usually your lease contract or a move-in confirmation letter from your landlord. If your landlord is unresponsive or you're in a short-term rental situation without formal paperwork, you can use: A utility bill in your name at the new address (but this usually takes a month to arrive) A letter from your real estate agent confirming the move-in In some cases, city halls will accept your verbal confirmation if you can show other documentation like a key receipt Different city halls have different flexibility on this. If you're stuck, explain your situation to the city hall staff and ask what alternatives they accept. Scenario 4: What Happens to My Mail During the Transition? Japan Post has a mail forwarding service (yubin no tensō). You can register for this at any post office or online. Fill out a form with your old address and new address, show ID, and Japan Post will forward your mail from the old address to the new address for one year. This is free and takes about a week to activate. The catch: Mail forwarding doesn't solve the problem of systems having the wrong address on file. It's a temporary solution. You still need to update your address with banks, employers, and government offices. ⚠️ WARNING: The Health Insurance Transition Gap When you move to a different municipality, there's often a 1-2 week gap between when your old health insurance card is collected and when your new health insurance card arrives. If you need medical care during this gap, you have two options: Go to the doctor and pay full price (100% cost), then claim reimbursement from your new city hall once you receive your insurance card. Go to your new city hall and request a temporary insurance certificate. Most offices can issue this on the spot if you explain you need medical care urgently. Don't avoid medical care just because your insurance card is in transition. The system has workarounds. How Address Changes Affect Daily Life (The Stuff Nobody Tells You) Garbage Collection Rules Change Different municipalities have different garbage collection schedules and rules. Some cities require you to buy specific garbage bags from designated stores. Some allow any clear bag. When you move to a new city, check the local garbage collection rules. Your new city hall usually has English pamphlets explaining the system. Frugal tip: In some cities, you can buy official garbage bags at the city hall for cheaper than convenience stores or supermarkets. Your Local Ward Office Changes If you move to a new ward or city, you now deal with a different ward office for any future administrative tasks (address updates, certificate requests, etc.). Find out where your new ward office is located and what their business hours are. Save the address and phone number. Library Cards and Community Services Your library card from your old city won't work in your new city. You need to register for a new library card. The same goes for community sports facilities, public pools, cultural centers, and any other municipal services. These are tied to your residence in that specific municipality. Childcare and Schools If you have kids, moving to a different municipality means re-enrolling in a new school district or applying for childcare in the new city. This requires separate paperwork beyond just the address change registration. Contact the new city's education board or childcare division as early as possible. Common Mistakes People Make Mistake 1: Assuming the post office mail forwarding service means they don't need to update their address at city hall. Mail forwarding is temporary. City hall registration is permanent and legally required. Mistake 2: Only updating their address at city hall and forgetting to tell their bank. Your bank won't know you moved unless you tell them. Important mail (credit card renewals, bank statements) will go to your old address. Mistake 3: Moving and waiting a month to register the address change because they're "still settling in." The 14-day deadline starts on your move date, not when you feel ready. Mistake 4: Thinking that because they live in the same building (but a different unit), they don't need to update anything. Even moving from unit 101 to unit 202 in the same building requires an address change notification at city hall. The Residence Tax Transition (This Confuses Everyone) Residence tax in Japan is assessed based on where you lived on January 1st of each year. The tax is collected by that municipality in the following year. Example: You lived in Shibuya on January 1, 2024 In June 2024, you move to Osaka In June 2025, you'll still pay residence tax to Shibuya (for the 2024 tax year) In June 2026, you'll pay residence tax to Osaka (for the 2025 tax year) This means you might be paying residence tax to a city you no longer live in. This is normal and expected. Your employer handles the withholding, so you don't need to do anything except understand why your tax bill is coming from your old city.
