The day you lose your job in Japan, a countdown begins. Most foreigners do not know the exact number of days they have before immigration can begin questioning their status, and missing the deadline quietly goes on your record. Here is what actually changes, what you must do, and in what order. ---

What Do You Have to Report to Immigration, and When?

The reporting obligation is immediate. Under the Immigration Control and Refugee Recognition Act, foreign nationals on work-based visas are required to notify the Immigration Services Agency within **14 days** of losing employment. This is not optional and it is not informal. You can report online through the Immigration Services Agency portal or in person at your regional immigration office. The form is called the **"Notification of Loss of Employment"** (契約機関との契約終了届出). You submit your residence card number, the name and address of your former employer, and the date employment ended. Why does this exist? Immigration uses this data to track whether visa holders remain in activities permitted by their status. Without it, they have no way to identify holders who have quietly slipped into unemployment and remained in Japan with no legal activity basis. **What happens if you miss it?** A failure to report within 14 days is a violation of Article 19-16 of the Immigration Act. You will not be arrested immediately, but the violation is logged against your immigration record. This matters enormously if you later apply for a visa renewal, change of status, or permanent residency. Based on reports from foreign residents, officers reviewing PR applications have flagged missed reporting incidents years after they occurred. See the full breakdown of what this reporting covers in the [immigration reporting compliance guide](https://mykyoten.com/guides/immigration-reporting-compliance-rules-japan). > **Warning Box** > Missing the 14-day reporting window does not trigger immediate deportation, but it creates a compliance record that follows you. Immigration officers have discretionary power during renewal and PR screening. A logged violation from a missed report can shift a borderline renewal into a refusal. File it late if you have to, but file it. ---

Does Your Visa Expire the Day You're Fired?

No. Your visa stamp and residence card remain valid until their printed expiry date regardless of your employment status. But the expiry date is not the real risk. **The 3-month activity gap rule is.** Under Japanese immigration practice, maintaining a status of residence requires that you actually engage in the activities your visa permits. If immigration determines that you have not engaged in permitted activities for **three consecutive months or more**, they can move to revoke your status under Article 22-4 of the Immigration Act, even if your card has not expired. This is the distinction most people miss entirely. **A concrete example:** A Canadian IT consultant on an Engineer/Specialist in Humanities/International Services visa is laid off on March 1. His residence card expires August 31. He assumes he has until August to sort things out. By June 1, three months of zero qualifying activity have passed. Immigration now has legal grounds to initiate revocation proceedings, two months before his card even expires. The practical enforcement reality is that immigration does not proactively audit every unemployed visa holder in real time. But if you come to their attention through any means (a renewal application, a police matter, a tip), the three-month gap is on record and the outcome changes. What Happens to Your Health Insurance on the Day You Leave?

Your company's shakai hoken (社会保険) coverage ends on the **last day of the month in which your employment ends** in most cases, though the exact cutoff depends on your final working day and company procedures. Confirm this date in writing with your HR department. From that point, you have **14 days** to enroll in Kokumin Kenko Hoken (国民健康保険, National Health Insurance) at your local city or ward office. Why 14 days? Because NHI enrollment is technically mandatory from the day your shakai hoken lapses. The 14-day window is the grace period for paperwork, not a coverage gap. **Calculating your first-year premium:** NHI premiums are based on the previous year's income. If you earned 5,000,000 yen in the prior year, your annual NHI premium in the first year could run between 300,000 and 500,000 yen depending on your municipality, paid in installments. The second year recalculates based on your actual income during unemployment, which will be lower. **The retroactive billing risk:** If you delay enrollment past the 14-day window and then show up at city hall months later, they will bill you retroactively from the day your company coverage ended. There is no cap on this backbilling in most municipalities. A five-month delay on 5,000,000 yen prior income could mean an unexpected lump-sum demand of 150,000 yen or more at enrollment. If you receive a health insurance notice and cannot read it, use the [Health Insurance Notice Decoder](https://mykyoten.com/tools/decode/health-insurance-notice) to parse what it says before taking action. The full insurance transition process is also covered in the [health insurance guide](https://mykyoten.com/guides/health-insurance-japan). > **Step-by-Step Checklist: Health Insurance Transition** > 1. Get your shakai hoken loss certificate (健康保険資格喪失証明書) from your employer in writing > 2. Bring it, your residence card, and My Number card to city hall > 3. Complete the NHI enrollment form at the citizen services counter > 4. Confirm your premium schedule before you leave the counter > 5. Return your company health insurance card by the deadline your former employer specifies > 6. Keep the NHI card you receive; it activates from enrollment date ---

Can Foreigners Actually Claim Unemployment Benefits in Japan?

Yes, if you have a valid work visa and have paid into employment insurance (雇用保険, koyou hoken) for the required period. Register at your nearest **Hello Work** (ハローワーク) office. Bring your residence card, employment insurance card (if you have it), your離職票 (rishoku-hyo, separation certificate from your employer), and your bank account details. **The waiting period difference matters significantly.** If you were involuntarily terminated (layoff, company closure, contract non-renewal), you can begin receiving benefits after a **7-day waiting period**. If you resigned voluntarily, there is an additional **2-to-3-month restriction period** before benefits begin, on top of the 7-day wait. **Worked yen example:** A Filipino customer service worker on an Engineer/Specialist in Humanities/International Services visa earned an average of 280,000 yen per month over the past six months. Her daily wage base is 280,000 yen x 6 / 180 = approximately 9,333 yen per day. Her benefit rate at that income level falls in the 50-80% range under the sliding scale. At 60%, she would receive roughly **5,600 yen per day**. The total benefit period depends on years of employment insurance contribution, ranging from 90 to 330 days. **Edge case 1:** You were on a fixed-term contract that was not renewed. This is treated as involuntary separation in most cases, meaning you avoid the 2-to-3-month restriction. Document the non-renewal in writing before you leave the company. **Edge case 2:** You are on a Specified Skilled Worker visa. You are eligible for Hello Work registration and unemployment benefits under the same rules as other work visa holders, provided you paid into koyou hoken. Your visa category does not disqualify you. Note that if you are receiving unemployment benefits, you are still legally required to be actively job-searching. Continued presence in Japan with no qualifying activity and no Hello Work registration weakens any status argument. > **Reality Check** > Hello Work staff quality and English availability varies by office. Based on common experience among foreign residents, larger urban offices in Tokyo, Osaka, and Nagoya tend to have at least one English-speaking staff member or a dedicated foreign national window. Smaller offices may not. Bring a Japanese-speaking friend or the printout of your rishoku-hyo with the key fields circled. The paperwork itself is manageable; the spoken consultation is where language becomes the friction point. ---

You have three realistic paths. Each has a hard clock attached. **Path 1: Find a new employer within the activity window.** This is the preferred outcome for most people. You have the three-month activity gap window to find a new employer, and they will file a new contract notification on your behalf. The [Visa Work Checker](https://mykyoten.com/tools/visa-work-checker) can help you confirm whether a prospective new role falls within your current visa category before you accept an offer. If the new role requires a different visa category, you will need a change of status, which takes time. Start that process before the three months expire, not after. **Path 2: Change to Designated Activities (Job-Searching) Status.** Since 2019, Japan has offered a designated activities visa for foreign graduates and for certain visa holders actively seeking new employment. Requirements include proof of genuine job-searching activity, valid status, and often a sponsor letter. The fee for the change of status application is 4,000 yen. Processing time runs 2 to 6 weeks in most regional offices. This status does not permit employment while searching, only job-hunting activities. It is a holding pattern, not a solution, but it legally extends your permitted stay. Read more about the [visa renewal vs change of status decision](https://mykyoten.com/guides/visa-renewal-vs-change-status-japan). **Path 3: Leave Japan.** If the timeline is collapsing and no offer is coming, a clean departure is better than an overstay or a forced revocation. If you have been paying into the pension system for six months or more and are a non-Japanese national leaving Japan, you may be eligible for a lump-sum pension withdrawal. That process and the 20% tax withholding on the refund are covered in the [pension refund tax guide](https://mykyoten.com/guides/pension-refund-tax-20-percent-japan). Use the [Leaving Japan Calculator](https://mykyoten.com/tools/leaving-japan-calculator) to map out the financial and administrative steps before your departure date. **Your next step:** File your employment loss notification with immigration today if you have not already done so, then call your city hall to confirm the exact date your company health insurance lapses before you calculate your NHI enrollment deadline. ---

**If I find a new job within two months, do I still need to report the job loss to immigration?** Yes. The reporting obligation for job loss is separate from the reporting obligation for new employment. Both notifications are required. Skipping the loss notification because you found new work quickly still creates a compliance gap on your record. File both, in sequence. **Can my visa be revoked immediately when I lose my job?** No, not immediately. The three-month activity gap threshold must be met first, and even then revocation requires a formal administrative process. That said, being out of qualifying activity for three months creates legal grounds for revocation, and a renewal application filed at that point faces serious scrutiny. **What if my employer goes bankrupt and I never receive a rishoku-hyo?** You can still register at Hello Work. Report the situation to the Hello Work officer and they will advise on alternative documentation. The Ministry of Health, Labour and Welfare has provisions for situations where employers have folded and cannot issue standard separation documents. --- If you are unsure how your current immigration record looks after a job loss or reporting delay, the [Immigration Risk Checker](https://mykyoten.com/tools/immigration-risk-checker) lets you input your visa type, employment gap, and reporting history to identify where your situation sits relative to standard compliance thresholds.
