Why Can a Japanese Bank Legally Refuse You?

Japanese banks operate under the Act on Prevention of Transfer of Criminal Proceeds and the Foreign Exchange and Foreign Trade Act. These laws require banks to verify your identity, your legal status in Japan, and your residential stability before opening an account. Basically, a bank that cannot confirm you are legally residing here and plan to stay can refuse you without explanation.

The three most common legal triggers for refusal are:

**Residence period under 6 months.** Many major banks, including MUFG and Mizuho, have internal policies requiring at least 6 months of registered residence in Japan before approving a new account. This is not a written law. It is internal risk policy. The distinction matters because it means the threshold varies by branch and by how your application is presented.

**Visa type restrictions.** Short-stay visas (tourist, 90-day stamps) are an immediate rejection trigger at nearly every traditional bank. Even some working visa holders on Specified Skilled Worker visas have reported initial difficulties based on reports from foreign residents, though this is not universal. How Does the Application Process Actually Work?

**At Japan Post Bank (ゆうちょ銀行):**

Step 1: Go to any Japan Post branch, not just a post office counter. Bring your residence card, passport, and My Number document.

Step 2: Take a number and wait. Tell the teller you want to open a futsuu yokin (普通預金) account. Say: 「口座を開設したいのですが」

Step 3: Fill out the paper application form. You will be asked for your name in Roman letters and katakana, your address, phone number, and occupation.

Step 4: Your inkan or signature is applied. The teller processes the paperwork. Your cash card arrives by post to your registered address within approximately 7 to 10 business days. There is no fee to open the account.

The process is similar but significantly stricter. These banks often require proof of employment and will ask about your purpose for opening the account.
