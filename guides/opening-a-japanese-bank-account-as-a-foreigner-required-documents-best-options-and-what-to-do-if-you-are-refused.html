Why Can a Japanese Bank Legally Refuse You?

Japanese banks operate under the Act on Prevention of Transfer of Criminal Proceeds and the Foreign Exchange and Foreign Trade Act. These laws require banks to verify your identity, your legal status in Japan, and your residential stability before opening an account. Basically, a bank that cannot confirm you are legally residing here and plan to stay can refuse you without explanation. The three most common legal triggers for refusal are: **Residence period under 6 months.** Many major banks, including MUFG and Mizuho, have internal policies requiring at least 6 months of registered residence in Japan before approving a new account. This is not a written law. It is internal risk policy. The distinction matters because it means the threshold varies by branch and by how your application is presented. **Visa type restrictions.** Short-stay visas (tourist, 90-day stamps) are an immediate rejection trigger at nearly every traditional bank. Even some working visa holders on Specified Skilled Worker visas have reported initial difficulties based on reports from foreign residents, though this is not universal. You can check whether your visa type creates complications using the [Visa Work Checker](https://mykyoten.com/tools/visa-work-checker). **Missing address registration.** If you have not completed jūmin tōroku (住民登録) at your local city hall, the bank cannot verify your address and will refuse you. This is non-negotiable. If you have not done this step yet, the [moving to Japan checklist](https://mykyoten.com/guides/moving-to-japan-checklist) covers the exact sequence. The most foreigner-friendly options in 2024-2026 based on common experience are Japan Post Bank (ゆうちょ銀行), Sony Bank, and Rakuten Bank. SBI Shinsei Bank is also frequently cited by foreign residents as accessible. ---

How Does the Application Process Actually Work?

**At Japan Post Bank (ゆうちょ銀行):** Step 1: Go to any Japan Post branch, not just a post office counter. Bring your residence card, passport, and My Number document. Step 2: Take a number and wait. Tell the teller you want to open a futsuu yokin (普通預金) account. Say: 「口座を開設したいのですが」 Step 3: Fill out the paper application form. You will be asked for your name in Roman letters and katakana, your address, phone number, and occupation. Step 4: Your inkan or signature is applied. The teller processes the paperwork. Your cash card arrives by post to your registered address within approximately 7 to 10 business days. There is no fee to open the account. **At MUFG or Mizuho:** The process is similar but significantly stricter. These banks often require proof of employment and will ask about your purpose for opening the account. Expect a longer form, a possible waiting period while the application is reviewed internally (sometimes 2 to 4 weeks), and a higher chance of being asked to return with additional documents. The 6-month residency expectation is most strictly applied here. ---

**Can I open a Japanese bank account with only a tourist visa?** No. Tourist visas and short-term stay stamps do not grant you legal residency status in Japan. Every major bank and most online banks require a valid residence card, which is only issued to holders of medium or long-term visas. There are no legitimate workarounds to this. **Does Japan Post Bank really have no minimum residency period?** Japan Post Bank's publicly available guidelines do not state a minimum registered residence period, unlike major city banks. Based on common experience among new arrivals, it is the most accessible option in the first weeks after arrival. That said, individual branches have some discretion, and bringing strong documentation always helps. **Will not having a bank account affect my visa renewal?** It can, indirectly. Immigration officers reviewing your renewal may look at financial stability. If you have no domestic bank account and no evidence of regular salary receipt, that raises questions about your economic integration. Having a verified account with salary deposits strengthens your renewal file.
