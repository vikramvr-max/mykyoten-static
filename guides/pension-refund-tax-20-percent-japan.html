You must meet all of these criteria: - Not be a Japanese citizen (dual citizens are disqualified) - Have paid into the system for 6+ months - No longer have a registered address in Japan - Never qualified for Japanese pension benefits - Apply within 2 years of your last insured date The "insured status end date" is typically the day you stopped working for a Japanese employer or the last day you were registered as a resident in Japan. The 2-year clock starts from this date, or from when you deregistered your address, whichever is later. At 120 months of contributions, you are permanently disqualified from the lump-sum withdrawal. You now qualify for Japan's old-age pension at age 65. Workers on 9-year, 11-month contracts who were unaware of the 120-month threshold lose access to the lump sum entirely. The Japan Pension Service counts partial months. Check your enrollment history at the nearest pension office before making employment decisions in your ninth year. Learn about Japan's pension system before deciding.

The lump-sum withdrawal is not a full refund. You recover approximately 30-50% of your employee contributions. The employer's matching 50% stays in the pension fund. For National Pension enrollees: Refund = (Final year monthly premium) × 0.5 × stepped multiplier based on months. For 2025, the monthly premium is ¥17,510 (2025 fiscal year). No withholding tax on National Pension refunds. You receive 100%. For Employees' Pension enrollees: The calculation uses "Average Standard Monthly Remuneration," which is not your actual average salary. Three salary tier examples: ¥3,000,000 annual income → Average standard monthly remuneration ¥250,000 → Lump sum before tax ¥1,372,500 → After 20.42% (current withholding rate) withholding ¥1,092,210 (41.7% recovery rate) ¥6,000,000 annual income → Lump sum before tax ¥2,745,000 → After withholding ¥2,183,901 ¥10,000,000 annual income → Lump sum before tax ¥4,574,998 → After withholding ¥3,640,745 There is an upper limit to standard monthly remuneration (currently ¥650,000). High earners hit this cap and recover less than 30% of actual cash paid into the system.

Japan withholds 20.42% (current withholding rate) as income tax on Employees' Pension withdrawals. For National Pension, there's no withholding. Getting that 20% back is not automatic. 1. Appoint a tax representative (納税管理人) in Japan 2. Have them file a tax return on your behalf 3. Wait for the refund to be deposited into their Japanese bank account 4. Arrange for them to transfer it to you The tax office requires a physical representative with a Japanese address. This is not negotiable. You cannot file remotely. If you don't have a trusted friend willing to take this on, you'll need to hire a professional. Licensed options include tax scriveners (行政書士) or tax accountants (税理士). Professional fees range from ¥30,000 to ¥80,000. For context: on a ¥1.6 million lump sum, the 20.42% (current withholding rate) withholding is ¥326,720. If you pay a professional ¥50,000 to recover it, you net ¥276,720. Still worth it. If you skip the tax refund entirely, you forfeit ¥326,720.

D-30: Identify your tax representative. D-21: Visit your municipal office to confirm the exact date your residence registration will be deleted. Avoid city hall paperwork errors. Follow the address change process carefully. D-Day: Exit Japan. Ensure your passport receives an exit stamp. D+3 to D+7: Download and complete the lump-sum withdrawal claim form from the Japan Pension Service website. Attach: passport copy (photo page + all visa pages + entry/exit stamps), residence deletion certificate, pension handbook or number notice, bank account verification document. Send via tracked international courier to Japan Pension Service, Foreign Services Group. D+120 to D+150: Receive wire transfer (80% of calculated lump sum) and payment notice. D+150 to D+180: Send original notice to your tax representative. D+300 to D+330: Confirm receipt of 20% refund from your tax representative. Total timeline: 10-11 months if no complications.

The account holder name on your bank account must match your passport character-for-character. If your passport says "Robert J. Smith" but your bank account says "Robert Smith," the pension office will reject the application. A German national submitted her application with her bank account under "Anna-Maria Schmidt" (hyphen). Her passport showed "Anna Maria Schmidt" (space, no hyphen). Resolution required a notarized letter from her bank, apostilled translation into Japanese, and re-submission. Total delay: 4 months.

International wire transfers rarely go directly from sender to recipient. They route through intermediary banks, each taking a cut. Typical routing: Japan Pension Service → MUFG Bank → Correspondent bank in your country → Your local bank. Each intermediary deducts $15-50 per transaction. SWIFT code precision matters. Verify your SWIFT code directly with your bank. Do not rely on Google or third-party websites. Digital banks are problematic: Wise does not have a traditional SWIFT code. You cannot receive a pension refund directly to a Wise account. Revolut JPY incoming wires are not supported in all countries. Monzo, N26, and Chime typically do not support incoming international wires in foreign currencies at all. The Japan Pension Service transfers funds in JPY only. Your bank converts at the prevailing exchange rate on the day funds are received. You have zero control over this rate.

Missing the 2-year deadline: The statute of limitations is absolute. Set a calendar reminder for 18 months after leaving Japan. Forgetting the move-out notification: If you're still registered as a resident, you can't claim the refund. See our leaving Japan exit guide for the full checklist. Employer never enrolled you: Check your payslips. If pension premiums (厚生年金保険料) were deducted, you were enrolled. If they deducted but didn't remit, that's fraud. Contact the pension office with payslip copies. Lost pension handbook: Request a reissue while still in Japan. Transfer payment failures: Can fail if bank account closed, SWIFT code incorrect, or name mismatch. Returning to Japan after claiming: Your enrollment history is erased. If you return and resume employment, you start from zero. However, you can claim multiple lump sums. The system resets each time you leave.

Claim the lump sum if: - You contributed less than 10 years in Japan - You're from a country without a totalization agreement - You don't expect to claim retirement benefits in your home country - You need immediate liquidity - You're young and capital now is more valuable than future pension benefits Keep contributions if: - You're close to 10 years and want to reach old-age pension eligibility - You plan to work more years in your home country and need totalized periods to qualify - You're nearing retirement age - Your home country pension would significantly benefit from totalized Japanese years If planning permanent residency, consider keeping contributions. If you claim the lump sum, you forfeit your Japanese contribution history. Those years won't count toward totalization later. You get money now, but lose the ability to use those years for your home country pension eligibility. This decision also affects your visa status when leaving.
