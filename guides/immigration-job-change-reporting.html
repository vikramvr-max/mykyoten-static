Many foreign residents assume immigration automatically knows when they change jobs. It makes sense, right? Your new employer submits paperwork to the government. Tax records get updated. You register your new workplace at city hall. It feels logical that immigration would be notified somewhere along the way as part of this process. After all, the government already seems to know so much about you. Why wouldn't they know about something as significant as a job change?

Here's the reality that catches a lot of people off guard: immigration does not automatically receive job change information. Employer records, city hall systems, and immigration databases all operate independently. They don't talk to each other the way you might expect. Updating one does not trigger an update in the others. Immigration maintains its own separate records, and they expect foreigners to submit specific notifications themselves. It's not an oversight or a flaw in the system. It's just how the reporting structure works.

This misunderstanding often stays hidden for months. Everything seems fine until you go to renew your visa or undergo some kind of status check. That's when immigration reviews your file and notices that a required notification was never filed. From your perspective, this feels completely sudden and unfair. You did everything you thought you were supposed to do. You registered at city hall. Your employer handled the tax paperwork. You assumed the system had it covered. But immigration sees it differently. They see an unreported change, and now you're scrambling to explain why.

Immigration expects proactive reporting of certain employment changes. This applies even when the new job feels basically the same as your old one. Maybe you switched companies but stayed in the same industry doing similar work. Maybe your job title barely changed. From your perspective, it might feel like a minor shift. But immigration doesn't assess changes based on how similar they feel to you. They focus on accurate, up-to-date records and whether you followed the proper notification procedures.

The rule is pretty straightforward: if your employment situation changes in a way that affects your visa status or requires notification under immigration law, you need to report it yourself. Don't wait for someone else to do it for you, because they won't.

Here's the thing about reporting gaps. They rarely cause immediate trouble. Nobody shows up at your door the day after you start a new job demanding to know why you haven't reported it yet. Instead, these gaps sit quietly in your file. They only surface later when immigration reviews your records for renewal or another reason. And the longer the gap exists, the harder it becomes to explain cleanly. A two-week delay might get a pass with a simple apology. A six-month or one-year delay looks like negligence or intentional avoidance.

After any job change, take a moment to confirm whether immigration reporting is required - see the full immigration reporting rules . If you're unsure, checking early is always safer than assuming everything is fine. Contact immigration directly, consult with an immigration specialist, or review the official guidelines. And if you realize you've already missed a deadline, late reporting is still better than no reporting at all. Fixing the problem now, even if it's overdue, shows good faith.
