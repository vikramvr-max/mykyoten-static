That envelope sitting on your kitchen counter, the one with 住民税 納税通知書 printed on it, is not junk mail. It is a legally binding tax demand from your local municipality. Ignoring it can trigger late fees, a collections notice, and a red flag on your residency record that follows you to your next visa renewal.

So What Actually Is This Notice, and Why Did It Show Up in June?

Resident tax (住民税, *juminzei*) is a local income tax collected by your city, ward, or town government. It is not the same as national income tax. It is calculated based on your previous year's income and billed the following year, which is exactly why so many foreigners get blindsided in their second year of living in Japan. You earned money in Year 1. In June of Year 2, the bill arrives.

The notice is formally called the 納税通知書 (*nōzei tsūchisho*). It is issued by your municipal office, not by the national tax authority. Every resident registered at a Japanese address as of January 1st of the billing year is legally subject to it, regardless of nationality.

Here is the part nobody tells you: if you arrived in Japan mid-year and had low or zero income in that first partial year, your first June notice might show a very small amount or nothing at all. Then the second June hits and the real bill appears. This phenomenon is documented in detail in the [resident tax second year shock guide](https://mykyoten.com/guides/microguide-20-resident-tax-second-year-shock), and it catches people off guard constantly.

What Every Line of the Notice Actually Means

The notice contains several blocks of information. Breaking them down:

**Tax amount (税額):** The total you owe for the year, split across four instalments.

**Payment due dates (納期限):** Typically the last business day of June, August, October, and January. These are hard deadlines, not suggestions.

**Municipal tax (市町村民税) vs. prefectural tax (道府県民税):** The bill combines both. You do not pay them separately. The split is roughly 6% to the municipality and 4% to the prefecture of your income, but the notice handles this internally.

**Blank or zero sections:** If part of the notice shows zero, it usually means that component does not apply to you. It does not mean the whole bill is cancelled.

> **[Warning Box]** > If the total amount on your notice looks impossibly high, do not assume it is an error and ignore it. A single missed review window can lock in a penalty. Call your ward or city office within 30 days of the notice date to request a review. Bring your residence card, My Number, and any income documentation you have.

**Convenience store payment** is the most common method. The notice includes a barcode slip for each instalment. Take the relevant slip to any 7-Eleven, FamilyMart, Lawson, or similar store and hand it to the cashier. They scan it, you pay cash, done. Keep the receipt. Maximum single payment amount accepted at convenience stores is generally ¥300,000. If your instalment exceeds that, you will need to use a bank.

**Bank or post office counter:** Present the slip at any bank or Japan Post. Most banks accept tax slips even if you do not hold an account there, though based on common experience reported by foreign residents, some smaller regional banks may decline non-account holders.

**Direct debit (口座振替):** You register your bank account with the municipal office. Future payments are automatically debited on the due date. This requires a visit to city hall with your bankbook and registered seal (or signature, depending on the municipality), done before the June deadline for that year's bill. Once set up, it removes the risk of forgetting.

**Online payment via PayPay or LINE Pay:** Many municipalities now support smartphone payment. Look for a QR code on your payment slip. Scan it through the PayPay or LINE Pay app and pay directly. Not every city offers this. Check your municipality's website or ask at the counter.

A Filipino nurse working on a Specified Skilled Worker (特定技能) visa earned ¥3,200,000 in 2024. In June 2025, her resident tax notice arrives showing a total annual liability of approximately ¥152,000, divided into four instalments of roughly ¥38,000 each. Due dates: June 30, August 31, October 31, January 31. She pays the first instalment at a FamilyMart using the barcode slip on June 28. Done in under three minutes.

**Wrong amount:** This happens when your income was misreported, you had deductions not reflected, or you changed employers mid-year. Go to your city or ward's tax division (市民税課 or 住民税課) in person. Bring your tax return (確定申告書) if you filed one, your most recent pay slips, and your residence card. If you need help reading those pay slips first, the [Payslip Decoder](https://mykyoten.com/tools/payslip-decoder) can help you identify the relevant income figures before you walk in.

**Notice sent to old address:** This is a serious problem. The municipality sends to the address registered in the resident registry as of January 1st. If you moved and did not update your juminhyo, the notice went to the wrong place. The legal obligation to pay still stands from the original due date. Update your address immediately at city hall and contact the tax division of your old municipality. Read the [address change reporting guide](https://mykyoten.com/guides/address-change-reporting) before you go.

**Belief you are exempt:** Exemption applies to people below the minimum income threshold, generally around ¥350,000 to ¥450,000 per year depending on the municipality and dependents. If you believe you qualify, you must formally apply for exemption. It is not automatic.

> **[Reality Check]** > The municipal office is not your enemy here. Based on common experience among foreign residents, most city hall tax staff will work with you on corrections if you come in with documentation and do not wait until after the deadline. Coming in on day two versus day sixty makes a significant difference in how the conversation goes.

**What if you left Japan before June and the notice followed you overseas?** The tax liability does not disappear when you leave. If you were registered as a resident on January 1st of the billing year, you owe the full year's tax. You need to have appointed a tax representative (納税管理人) before leaving, or the municipality will pursue collection. This also affects any future re-entry and visa application. The [Leaving Japan Calculator](https://mykyoten.com/tools/leaving-japan-calculator) covers the financial obligations tied to departure timing.

**What if your employer was handling resident tax through salary deduction (特別徴収) and then you quit?** When employment ends, any unpaid instalments revert to the individual collection method (普通徴収). You will receive new payment slips directly. This switch sometimes causes confusion because the amounts and due dates change slightly.

Missing a Payment Deadline: This Is Where People Mess Up

Japan's late payment penalty on resident tax is not trivial. The current statutory rate runs at **8.7% per year for the first two months** past the due date, escalating to **14.6% per year** thereafter, as set by Japanese tax law. On a ¥38,000 instalment, that is roughly ¥277 per month at the base rate, climbing steeply if months pass.

1. **Day 1 past deadline:** Late penalty begins accruing automatically. 2. **Approximately 1 month late:** Reminder notice (督促状) arrives by mail. 3. **Extended non-payment:** The municipality issues a demand order and can begin asset investigation. 4. **Seizure stage:** Bank accounts, salary, or other assets can be legally seized under Japanese tax collection law. This is not a threat, it happens.

The visa consequence is the one that hits hardest. Immigration reviews tax payment compliance during visa renewal and especially during permanent residency applications. Unpaid resident tax, documented delinquency, or a collections record are treated as evidence of poor civic compliance. If you are working toward PR, this matters enormously. **Can my employer pay resident tax on my behalf?** Yes. Employees can request that resident tax be withheld directly from salary through a system called 特別徴収. Your employer submits a form to the municipality. If you are already receiving a direct notice, ask your HR department whether this switch is possible for next year.

**I just arrived in Japan this year. Will I get a notice in June?** If you had no Japan-sourced income in the prior calendar year, you likely owe nothing this coming June. Your first real bill typically arrives the June after your first full year of working. The amount can feel like a shock if you were not expecting it.

**Does paying resident tax affect my visa renewal or PR application?** Yes, directly. Immigration officers routinely request tax payment certificates (納税証明書) during visa renewals and PR screening. Consistent, on-time payment is treated as evidence of compliance and social integration. Gaps or delinquencies create complications that are difficult to explain away.
