Your residence card and your visa status are two separate things. This distinction trips up thousands of foreigners every year. You can have a valid visa period stamped in your passport and still be treated as an illegal overstayer if your residence card has expired. The card itself is your legal proof of residence in Japan. Let it lapse, and you are technically in violation of the Immigration Control and Refugee Recognition Act, regardless of what your passport says.

That is not a minor paperwork issue. It can affect your ability to rent an apartment, open a bank account, travel internationally, and, in serious cases, lead to detention proceedings.

The rule depends on your situation.

**Long-term residents** (those who have been in Japan and have a residence card already) must apply for renewal **within 3 months before the expiry date** printed on their card. Not 6 months. Not 1 month. The window opens exactly at the 3-month mark.

**Mid-period renewals** happen when your circumstances change before the card expires. If you change visa categories, such as moving from Student to Engineer/Specialist in Humanities/International Services, you must apply for a change of status rather than a standard renewal. These are different procedures. Confusing them is one of the most common mistakes foreigners make. [This guide on visa renewal vs. change of status explains the difference in detail.](https://mykyoten.com/guides/visa-renewal-vs-change-status-japan)

**What happens if you miss the deadline?** If your card expires and you have not applied, you are in overstay territory. The Immigration Services Agency (ISA) can issue a fine of up to ¥200,000 under Article 71 of the Immigration Control Act. Deportation proceedings are also legally possible in repeated or willful cases. Practically speaking, reports from foreign residents suggest that first-time, short overstays where the person self-reports are sometimes handled with a penalty fine and a corrective procedure rather than immediate deportation, but this is never guaranteed and you should never rely on it.

Apply early. Open that 3-month window and use it.

Here is where people get burned. The ISA will reject your application if documents are missing, wrong format, or outdated. Rejections do not reset your deadline. You lose that appointment slot and have to start over.

> **Warning Box** > The ISA does not always tell you which specific document caused a rejection. Based on common experience reported by foreign residents, you may receive a general deficiency notice without detailed explanation. Bring originals AND copies of everything unless explicitly told otherwise.

**Documents required for ALL applicants:** - Residence card (original, mandatory) - Passport (original, mandatory) - Application form (Form for Extension of Period of Stay, downloadable from the ISA website, filled in Japanese or English) - One photograph: 4cm x 3cm, taken within 3 months, white background, no glasses, no hat - Juminhyo (住民票): issued within 3 months, original. Obtain this at your city hall or ward office. Cost: typically ¥200 to ¥400.

**Additional documents by visa category:**

| Visa Category | Key Additional Documents | Originals Required? | |---|---|---| | Work (Engineer, Humanities, etc.) | Certificate of employment, recent payslips (3 months), tax certificate (nozei shomeisho), company registration | Employment cert: original | | Student | Certificate of enrollment, transcript or attendance record | Enrollment cert: original | | Spouse of Japanese National | Marriage certificate (Japanese and foreign), spouse's juminhyo, spouse's income proof or tax records | Marriage cert: original + certified translation | | Dependent | Sponsor's income certificate, relationship proof, sponsor's residence card copy | Income cert: original | | Specified Skilled Worker | Skills evaluation certificate, Japanese language test results, employer contract | Evaluation cert: original |

If you are a work visa holder and need help interpreting what your payslips say about your social insurance enrollment status, use the [Payslip Decoder](https://mykyoten.com/tools/payslip-decoder) before your appointment. Immigration officers can and do check whether your insurance and pension contributions are current.

**Common rejection triggers:** - Juminhyo older than 3 months - Photographs with shadows, glasses, or incorrect dimensions - Tax certificate from the wrong fiscal year - Employer certificate signed by someone without signing authority - Missing certified translation for foreign-language documents

Here is the part nobody tells you: the ISA system has changed significantly since the launch of online applications. You now have two main paths.

**Path 1: In-person at the Immigration Services Agency**

Step 1: Locate your nearest ISA office. There are regional offices in major cities and branch offices in smaller prefectures. Tokyo, Osaka, Nagoya, Sapporo, Fukuoka, and Sendai have main offices. Find your jurisdiction on the ISA official website.

Step 2: Book an appointment online at the ISA portal (nyuukokukanri.moj.go.jp). Walk-in slots exist but waiting times without an appointment are often 2 to 4 hours based on common experience. With an appointment, typical wait is 20 to 40 minutes.

Step 3: Arrive with all documents in the order the form requests them. Bring a number ticket from the queue machine at the entrance.

Step 4: Submit at the counter. The officer reviews your documents on the spot. If something is deficient, you will be told to supplement. If accepted, you receive a receipt slip (受付票, uketsuke-hyou).

Step 5: Wait for your new card. Processing is typically 2 to 4 weeks for standard cases. Complex cases (first renewals, status changes, criminal record flags) can take 6 to 8 weeks.

**Path 2: Online Application (e-VISA system)**

The ISA online application system allows certain applicants to submit electronically. As of 2026, eligibility is still limited and primarily covers specific visa categories and employer-sponsored applicants. You still need to collect physical originals and may need to visit the office to submit biometrics or pick up the new card.

> **Reality Check** > The online system is functional but not frictionless. Reports from foreign residents indicate that document upload errors, browser compatibility issues with Safari on older macOS, and session timeouts during long uploads are recurring problems. Use Chrome or Edge, upload documents in PDF format under 2MB each, and save your application ID immediately after submission.

The government fee is **¥4,000**, paid via a revenue stamp (収入印紙, shunyu inshi). You purchase this at a post office or at a stamp vending machine inside the ISA office. Do not affix the stamp to the form before the officer instructs you to. Some offices have you affix it only after approval.

**No government fee exemptions** exist for standard renewals based on publicly available ISA guidelines. Special humanitarian categories may differ. Consult a certified immigration lawyer if you believe you qualify.

A realistic total out-of-pocket cost for a self-managed renewal is **¥5,000 to ¥7,000**. Add translation or legal support and you are looking at ¥25,000 to ¥90,000.

**Concrete example:** A Filipino nurse on a Specified Skilled Worker (SSW) visa renewing in Osaka in March 2026 spent ¥4,000 for the revenue stamp, ¥350 for her juminhyo, ¥350 for her tax certificate, and ¥6,000 for a certified translation of her Philippine civil registry document confirming her legal name. Total: ¥10,700. Her appointment was booked 11 days in advance, and her new card arrived by mail 19 days after submission.

What to Do While You Are Waiting for the New Card

You will not leave the ISA empty-handed. The receipt slip you receive at submission is a legally recognized interim document. It proves you have applied and that your status is under review. Carry it with your passport at all times while waiting.

**Can you travel internationally while waiting?** Yes, but carefully. You need a re-entry permit or you must use the special re-entry permission (within 1 year of departure). When you return to Japan, you will use the receipt slip alongside your passport at immigration. The officer will note your pending renewal. Your new card will typically be ready for pickup or mailed to you shortly after. [Check the immigration reporting compliance rules](https://mykyoten.com/guides/immigration-reporting-compliance-rules-japan) before any international travel during this period.

**Edge Case 1:** Your employer closes down while your renewal application is pending. This is a serious compliance issue. You are required to report a change in employer or employment termination to the ISA within 14 days under Article 19-16 of the Immigration Control Act. Failure to report can be treated as a material misrepresentation on your original application. Consult a lawyer immediately if this happens. You can check your current risk profile using the [Immigration Risk Checker](https://mykyoten.com/tools/immigration-risk-checker).

**Edge Case 2:** Your card expires while your application is still processing. This happens. As long as you submitted before the expiry date and hold a valid receipt slip, you are legally covered. The receipt slip extends your provisional status. Carry it. Show it when asked by police, landlords, or employers.

**Tracking your application:** The ISA website has an online status check tool. You will need your application receipt number. Status typically updates from "under review" to "ready" within 2 to 4 weeks. You will also receive a postcard notification at your registered address when the card is ready for pickup or mailing.

If your application status does not update after 6 weeks, call the ISA inquiry line directly. Do not wait and hope.

Your next step: gather your juminhyo and tax certificate first, since these have the shortest validity windows and are most commonly the reason renewals stall. Book your ISA appointment before you have every document in hand, then collect remaining materials in the days before your appointment date.

**Can I renew my residence card if I changed jobs recently?** Yes, but you must also report the job change to the ISA separately, typically within 14 days of the change. A renewal application does not substitute for job change reporting. Bring your new employer certificate to the renewal. See [job change reporting deadlines](https://mykyoten.com/guides/job-change-reporting-deadline) for details.

**What if my name on the residence card does not match my passport exactly?** This can happen after passport renewal or due to romanization differences. Bring both documents and raise this at the counter. The officer may issue a corrected card or note the discrepancy. Do not ignore it. Name mismatches can cause problems at airport immigration and during background checks.

**Is it possible to renew a residence card if I am currently unemployed?** It depends on your visa category. Some visa types require active employment as a condition of status. If you are between jobs, your renewal may face additional scrutiny or be denied. The [Immigration Risk Checker](https://mykyoten.com/tools/immigration-risk-checker) can help you assess your specific situation before you apply.

Before you submit your renewal application, run your situation through the [Immigration Risk Checker](https://mykyoten.com/tools/immigration-risk-checker). It reviews factors like employment continuity, tax compliance, insurance enrollment, and reporting history, all of which immigration officers examine during renewal reviews. It takes a few minutes and can surface issues you did not know existed before they become problems at the counter.
