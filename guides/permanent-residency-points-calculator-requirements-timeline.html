Japan PR Points Calculator 2026: Miss 1 Point, Lose ¥8,000

Japan HSP permanent residency requires 70 points for the 3-year fast track or 80 points for 1 year. Salary carries up to 50 points. Document validity expires in 3 to 6 months.

Getting permanent residency in Japan requires hitting exact point thresholds or waiting the full decade. **Miss by even one point and you waste months of preparation plus ¥8,000 in non-refundable application fees.** The Immigration Bureau designed the points system to fast-track skilled foreign workers who contribute significantly to Japan's economy. If you qualify for 70+ points, you can apply after three years instead of ten. Hit 80+ points and you only need one year of residence.

How many points do I actually need and when can I apply?

The points system evaluates three main categories: academic background, professional experience, and annual salary. Each category has specific point values that Immigration Officers verify against your submitted documents. **Education points range from 0 to 25.** A bachelor's degree earns 10 points, master's degree gets 15 points, and PhD receives 20 points. If your degree is from a Japanese university, add 5 bonus points. The catch is that Immigration requires official transcripts and degree verification - not just diploma copies. **Age brackets determine another 0 to 15 points.** Under 30 years old gets maximum 15 points. Ages 30-34 earn 10 points, 35-39 get 5 points, and 40+ receive zero age points. This creates urgency for older applicants who must compensate with higher salaries or advanced degrees. **Annual salary carries the heaviest weight, up to 50 points.** The minimum threshold starts at ¥3 million yearly for any points. Jump to ¥4 million and earn 10 points. Each salary tier increases points: ¥5 million (20 points), ¥6 million (25 points), ¥7 million (30 points), ¥8 million (35 points), ¥9 million (40 points), and ¥10+ million (50 points). [**Reality Check**] Here is the part nobody tells you: Immigration calculates salary points based on your **total annual income** including bonuses, not just base salary. A German IT engineer earning ¥6.5 million base salary plus ¥1.5 million in annual bonuses hits the ¥8 million tier for 35 salary points. But if bonuses vary yearly, use your lowest guaranteed amount for point calculations.

Should I take the 10-year route or gamble on points?

The traditional 10-year route requires continuous legal residence with stable employment and tax compliance. No point calculations needed, but you must demonstrate financial stability and integration into Japanese society. Points-based applications let you apply after just 1-3 years depending on your score. **70-79 points qualifies after 3 years of residence. 80+ points reduces the wait to just 1 year.** However, you must maintain those point levels continuously until your application gets approved. For detailed guidance on optimizing your [permanent residency strategy](https://mykyoten.com/guides/permanent-residency-strategy), consider the specific visa pathway requirements. Most applicants underestimate the documentation burden for points-based applications. You need official transcripts, employer salary certificates, Japanese language test certificates, and professional licensing documents. Each piece must be current and properly authenticated. The 10-year route accepts simpler documentation like residence certificates, tax payment records, and employment contracts. **If your points hover near the minimum threshold or your salary fluctuates, the traditional route offers more certainty.** Consider a Filipino nurse on a Specified Skilled Worker visa earning ¥4.2 million annually. With a bachelor's degree (10 points), under-30 age bracket (15 points), and salary tier (10 points), they reach exactly 35 points - far below the 70-point minimum. The 10-year route becomes their only viable option unless they pursue additional qualifications. [**Warning Box**] **Point inflation trap:** Many applicants calculate points based on expected salary increases or bonuses that haven't materialized yet. Immigration verifies your actual income through tax records and employer certificates. Overestimating points leads to automatic rejection and wasted fees.

What documents do I need and how long are they valid?

Document validity periods vary significantly, and mistiming your collection can force expensive re-applications. **Most official certificates expire within 3-6 months of issue.** **From your home country:** Original degree certificates with apostille authentication, official transcripts, and criminal background checks. These must be translated into Japanese by certified translators. Criminal background checks typically expire after 6 months. **From Japanese institutions:** Certificate of Legal Residence (在留証明書) valid for 3 months, tax payment certificates covering the required residence period, and employment contracts or salary certificates from your current employer dated within 3 months. **Language certifications:** JLPT (Japanese Language Proficiency Test) certificates never expire, but other language tests like J-TEST have 2-year validity periods. Immigration accepts N1 or N2 levels for language bonus points. **Professional licenses:** Medical licenses, engineering certifications, or teaching credentials must be current and properly translated. Some professional bodies require annual renewals that affect your point calculations. The biggest mistake is collecting documents too early. **Start gathering home country documents first since they take longest to authenticate and translate.** Then collect Japanese documents in reverse order of expiration dates. [**Step-by-Step Checklist**] ✓ Order official transcripts and degree certificates (6-8 weeks delivery) ✓ Request apostille authentication from your home country (2-4 weeks) ✓ Get criminal background check apostilled (timing varies by country) ✓ Find certified translator for document translation (1-2 weeks, ¥15,000-30,000) ✓ Obtain Certificate of Legal Residence from city hall (same day, ¥300) ✓ Request employer salary certificate (1-2 weeks) ✓ Gather tax payment certificates for required years (same day at city hall)

How long does the actual application process take?

Immigration quotes 4-8 months for permanent residency decisions, but the timeline depends heavily on your preparation and document quality. **Well-prepared applications typically receive decisions within 4-6 months. Incomplete or questionable applications stretch toward the 8-month maximum.** **Months 1-2: Document review phase.** Immigration officers verify each document against their database systems. They cross-check your tax records, employment history, and residence stability. This phase runs smoothly if your documents are complete and authentic. **Month 3-4: Background investigation.** Immigration contacts your employer to verify employment details and salary information. They may request additional documentation if anything appears inconsistent. **This is where most delays occur - when employers are slow to respond or provide conflicting information.** **Months 4-6: Final decision processing.** Senior immigration officers review the complete file and make approval or rejection decisions. Approvals get processed faster than rejections because rejections require detailed written explanations. The system favors applicants who demonstrate long-term commitment to Japan. Having the same employer throughout your residence period, maintaining the same address, and showing consistent tax compliance all accelerate processing times.

What mistakes kill applications before they reach an officer?

**Salary documentation inconsistencies** cause more rejections than any other factor. Your employer certificate, tax records, and payslip records must show identical annual income figures. Even small discrepancies trigger automatic reviews that can extend processing times. **Address history gaps** create red flags for immigration officers. If you moved apartments and forgot to update your residence registration within 14 days, that violation appears in their system. Multiple address reporting violations suggest poor compliance with Japanese administrative requirements. **Employment status mismatches** between your current visa category and actual job duties cause immediate rejections. An Engineer visa holder working primarily in sales or marketing roles violates their visa conditions, disqualifying them from permanent residency regardless of points. Here is where people mess up: **submitting expired documents thinking immigration won't notice.** Every document gets verified against official databases. Expired certificates waste your application fee and restart the entire timeline. Tax payment delays or missing payments automatically disqualify your application. **Even if you eventually paid all taxes owed, late payments appear on your tax compliance record.** Immigration views tax compliance as evidence of your reliability as a permanent resident.
