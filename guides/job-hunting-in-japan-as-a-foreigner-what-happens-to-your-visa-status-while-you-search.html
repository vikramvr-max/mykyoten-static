Changing jobs in Japan feels like a career move. For a foreigner on a work visa, it is also an immigration event. The gap between your last day at one employer and your first day at the next is not neutral time. It is a documented period that immigration officers will scrutinize at your next renewal, and potentially at your permanent residency application. Get this wrong, and the gap follows you.

What Are the Two Notifications You Must File When You Change Jobs?

Japanese immigration law requires two formal notifications when you change employers. Neither is optional. Both are enforced under the Immigration Control and Refugee Recognition Act, and missing either one is a violation that goes on your record. **Notification 1: Leaving your current employer.** Within 14 days of your last working day, you must notify the Immigration Services Agency that you have left your job. You can do this online via the Immigration Bureau's e-Notification system at the [Immigration Services Agency portal](https://www.isa.go.jp), or by mailing a paper form (届出書) to your regional immigration office. The online method is faster and generates a submission record you should save. **Notification 2: Joining your new employer.** Within 14 days of your first working day at the new company, you file another notification reporting the new employer's name, address, and your job title. Same system, same process. Why does this exist? Immigration tracks your activities by employer. Your visa status is granted partly on the basis of where and how you work. The notifications let the agency verify that your actual activities still match your visa category. Without them, immigration has no visibility into your employment situation. Failing to notify carries a fine of up to 200,000 yen per violation. Based on common experience reported by foreign residents, this is rarely enforced as an isolated penalty, but a missing notification discovered during a renewal review will almost certainly trigger additional scrutiny and documentation requests. How Long Can You Actually Stay in Japan Between Jobs?

Your visa does not expire when your employment ends. That is one of the most common misunderstandings. Your residence status remains valid until the expiry date printed on your residence card. If your card says it expires on March 31, 2027, you can legally remain in Japan until that date regardless of your employment situation. **But here is the catch.** Immigration regulations contain a provision that allows authorities to revoke your status if you have not engaged in the activities your visa covers for a continuous period of three months or more. This is the "3-month gap rule," and it is separate from your card's expiry date. So the timeline that matters is not your card expiry. It is the length of your unemployment gap. > **Warning Box** > If your job search extends beyond three months and you have not filed for an alternative status or taken any documented steps (such as applying for the designated activities visa), you are in a revocable-status zone. Immigration can, at their discretion, treat this as grounds for cancellation of your residence status. The risk is not automatic, but it is real and documented in the Act. See [can immigration revoke your visa](https://mykyoten.com/guides/can-immigration-revoke-visa) for the full legal context. Consider a concrete scenario: a Brazilian software engineer on an Engineer/Specialist in Humanities/International Services visa leaves his company on July 1. His residence card expires January 2028. His job search runs long. By October 1, he has hit the three-month threshold without employment in his visa category. His card still shows a valid date, but immigration now has legal grounds to review his status if they choose to check. At his next renewal, this gap will appear in his notification history and may require a written explanation.

Does Your New Job Actually Match Your Current Visa?

This is the question most people skip, and it causes the most serious problems. Every work visa in Japan is tied to a category of activity. An Engineer/Specialist in Humanities/International Services visa covers technical, translation, legal, and certain business roles. An Instructor visa covers teaching. A Skilled Labor visa covers specific trades. If your new role falls outside the activity scope of your current visa, **you cannot legally start that job** without first completing a formal change of status application. Examples of job changes that require a status change before Day 1: - An IT engineer (Engineer visa) moving to a full-time sales role with no technical component - A university instructor (Instructor visa) moving to a corporate HR position - A cook with a Skilled Labor visa taking a role as a restaurant manager with no cooking duties The change of status application is filed at your regional immigration office. The standard processing fee is 4,000 yen (as of the last published schedule). Processing typically takes 2 to 3 months, though complex cases or periods of high application volume can extend this. You cannot start the new role until the Certificate of Status Change is issued. What If Your Job Search Takes Longer Than Three Months?

You have options. The key is acting before the three-month mark, not after. Japan offers a designated activities visa for mid-career job seekers in specific circumstances. This is not widely publicized, and based on reports from foreign residents, many people discover it too late. **Who qualifies:** You must be a former holder of a substantive work visa (not a dependent or student visa), actively seeking employment in a role that matches your previous visa category, and able to demonstrate genuine job search activity. **Documents typically required:** - Application for change of status to Designated Activities (job-seeking) - Explanation letter describing your job search activities - Evidence of job search: interview records, recruitment agency correspondence, job application records - Bank statements showing sufficient funds to support yourself - Proof of health insurance coverage - Your current residence card and passport **Duration granted:** Generally 6 months, sometimes renewable once for an additional 6 months. This is not a guaranteed outcome. Immigration officers evaluate each case individually. The application should be filed at your regional immigration office in person, or in some cases by proxy through a licensed immigration lawyer (gyoseishoshi or bengoshi). Filing early, before you hit the three-month threshold, is strongly recommended. **What if you let it lapse past three months without applying?** At that point you are dependent on discretionary leniency during your next renewal. That leniency is not something any guide can promise you.

What Practical Steps Do You Need to Handle During the Transition?

The immigration notifications get the most attention, but the administrative work does not stop there. **Address change:** If you move during your job search, you must notify your ward or city office within 14 days of moving. This is a separate obligation from the immigration notification. Failure to report an address change is its own violation. See the [address change reporting guide](https://mykyoten.com/guides/address-change-reporting) for the exact steps. **Health insurance:** When you leave your employer, you lose coverage under company-sponsored shakai hoken (社会保険). You have two options: join kokumin kenko hoken (National Health Insurance) through your ward office, or continue your previous employer's coverage voluntarily under the COBRA-equivalent called ninini keizoku. National Health Insurance enrollment should happen within 14 days of losing previous coverage. Premiums vary by municipality and income, but a single person with moderate prior income should expect monthly premiums in the range of 15,000 to 30,000 yen. For help reading insurance documents, use the [Health Insurance Notice Decoder](https://mykyoten.com/tools/decode/health-insurance-notice). **Shakai hoken at your new employer:** Your new employer is legally required to enroll you in shakai hoken within 5 days of your start date. Confirm this in writing before you sign. Some smaller companies delay enrollment, which affects your pension record and health coverage. Verify the enrollment date on your first payslip. The [Payslip Decoder](https://mykyoten.com/tools/payslip-decoder) can help you confirm what deductions are appearing and whether enrollment looks correct. **Before you sign the offer letter:** Verify that the job title and role description on the contract align with your visa category. Get the employer's company registration number (法人番号) and confirm they are registered as a legitimate entity. **Does my employer have to notify immigration when I quit, or is that my responsibility?** The notification obligation sits with you, not your employer. Some employers file a separate notification of contract termination under a different regulatory framework, but that does not substitute for your personal departure notification. File it yourself within 14 days regardless of what your employer tells you they have done. **Will a three-month unemployment gap automatically hurt my permanent residency application?** Not automatically, but it will require explanation. Immigration officers reviewing PR applications examine notification history and employment continuity. A documented gap with a clear explanation, such as a filed designated activities visa application, is treated very differently from an unexplained gap. See the [permanent residency guide](https://mykyoten.com/guides/permanent-residency-japan) for how employment history is evaluated. **Can I do freelance work or contract shifts while I job search on my current visa?** Only if your current visa category permits that type of work. Most standard work visas restrict you to the activities of your sponsoring employer. Freelance work typically requires a specific permission or a different visa category. Engaging in unauthorized work during a gap, even brief paid consulting, is a violation. Confirm your situation with an immigration attorney before accepting any paid work. --- If you are currently between jobs or about to leave an employer, use the [Immigration Risk Checker](https://mykyoten.com/tools/immigration-risk-checker) to assess your specific situation.
