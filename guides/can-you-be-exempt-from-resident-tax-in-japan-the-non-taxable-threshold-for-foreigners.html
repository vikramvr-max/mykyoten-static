What Does "Non-Taxable" Actually Mean in Japan

Resident tax (住民税) in Japan has two components. The first is the **per-capita levy** (均等割, *kintou-wari*), a flat fee charged regardless of income, typically around ¥5,000 to ¥6,000 per year depending on the municipality. The second is the **income-proportional levy** (所得割, *shotoku-wari*), which is calculated as approximately 10% of your taxable income.

Non-taxable status (非課税, *hikazei*) can apply to one or both.

Basically, there are two tiers. If your income falls below a certain threshold, you may be exempt from the income-proportional levy only. If it falls below a lower threshold, you may be exempt from both components entirely, meaning zero resident tax owed. These thresholds are not national standards locked to a single number. **Each municipality sets them within a range established by national law**, which is why the exact cutoff varies slightly depending on where you live.

The threshold for full exemption (both levies) for a single person with no dependants is generally **¥1,000,000 in taxable income** for most major municipalities, though some set it marginally higher. This is not your gross salary. This is your income after applying the basic deductions, including the employment income deduction (給与所得控除).

Here is the part nobody tells you: being registered at the municipal office and having a low income does not automatically trigger non-taxable status. **You still need to file**, or the municipality will assume you owe the full amount.
