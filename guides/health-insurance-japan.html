How health insurance works for foreigners in Japan. National Health Insurance enrollment, costs, and what is covered.

Most people get confused because they think they can delay enrollment until they "need" it or until they feel settled. By the time they realize this is wrong, they're looking at backdated premium bills for months they didn't even know they were enrolled.

The system is actually straightforward once you understand which type of insurance applies to you and when enrollment happens.

This guide applies after you've registered your address at city hall . Once that registration is complete, you're in the health insurance system whether you realize it or not.

It doesn't matter if you're employed, self-employed, a student, or unemployed. Everyone who registers an address in Japan must have health insurance. Health insurance is part of the broader pension and social insurance system - check your arrival timeline for enrollment deadlines.

How Japan Structures Health Insurance (Two Main Paths)

Japan has universal health coverage, but it's not a single unified system. Instead, there are two main insurance schemes, and which one applies to you depends on your employment situation.

Path 1: Employee Health Insurance (健康保険 - Kenkō Hoken) - If you work for a company that enrolls you in employee health insurance, your employer handles everything. This applies to most full-time employees and some part-time workers who meet certain hour thresholds.

Path 2: National Health Insurance (国民健康保険 - Kokumin Kenkō Hoken) - If you're not covered by employer insurance, you're automatically enrolled in National Health Insurance (NHI) through your city hall. This includes students, freelancers, unemployed people, and part-time workers whose employers don't provide insurance.

You don't get to choose between these two. Your employment situation determines which system you're in.

Employer-Provided Health Insurance: How It Works

If your employer provides health insurance, they enroll you through the company's payroll system. This usually happens within your first month of employment.

Your employer deducts health insurance premiums from your paycheck

You receive a health insurance card (保険証 - hokenshō) in the mail within 2-3 weeks

The card shows your employer's insurance provider name (like Kyokai Kenpo, company-specific mutual aid associations, etc.)

What you pay: Premiums are based on your salary and split between you and your employer (roughly 50/50). The exact percentage varies slightly by insurance provider but averages around 10% of your salary total, with you paying 5%.

For example, if your monthly salary is ¥300,000, your health insurance deduction is roughly ¥15,000 per month.

National Health Insurance (NHI): How It Works

If you're not covered by employer insurance, you're automatically enrolled in National Health Insurance when you register your address at city hall. Make sure you're familiar with city hall paperwork requirements before your visit.

Let me repeat that: automatically enrolled.

You don't apply for it. You don't choose to enroll. The moment you register your address, city hall enrolls you in NHI. This happens whether you realize it or not.

You receive a health insurance card in the mail 1-3 weeks after registering your address

You receive a premium bill (usually quarterly) from your city office

The card shows your municipality name and the National Health Insurance logo

What you pay: NHI premiums are calculated based on your income from the previous year, your age, and your municipality. The calculation is complex, but here's a rough estimate:

If you just arrived in Japan with no prior Japanese income: ¥2,000-¥4,000 per month (low income rate)

If you have Japanese income history: ¥10,000-¥30,000+ per month depending on earnings

Students often pay the minimum rate: ¥2,000-¥3,000 per month

Premium bills arrive every 2-3 months and can be paid at convenience stores, banks, or via automatic bank transfer.

When it starts: Your enrollment starts from your address registration date, not from when you receive the card. If you register your address on April 15, your coverage starts April 15, even if your card doesn't arrive until May.

The Timing Problem That Costs People Money

Here's where people mess up: they register their address at city hall but don't realize they're enrolled in NHI immediately. They assume enrollment happens when they receive the card or when they explicitly "apply."

Months go by. No card arrives (maybe it got lost in the mail, maybe they registered a temporary address and moved). They're not paying premiums because they never received a bill.

Then they either need medical care and discover they're enrolled, or they receive a massive backdated bill for 6+ months of premiums.

Japan doesn't care that you didn't know. Your enrollment date is your address registration date. You owe premiums from that day forward, whether you received the card or not.

What Health Insurance Actually Covers

Japanese health insurance covers 70% of most medical costs. You pay the remaining 30% at the point of care.

Doctor visits and consultations

Diagnostic tests (blood tests, X-rays, MRIs, etc.)

Most dental work (cleanings, fillings, basic procedures)

Some advanced dental work (implants, whitening)

Certain experimental treatments

Glasses and contact lenses (though eye exams are covered)

Normal pregnancy and childbirth (but complications are covered, and there are separate childbirth subsidies)

Regular doctor visit: ¥1,500-¥3,000 (you pay 30% of ¥5,000-¥10,000 total)

Prescription medication: ¥500-¥2,000 depending on what's prescribed

Hospital stay (per day): ¥3,000-¥9,000 depending on room type

Without insurance, these costs are 3-4x higher.

What If You Don't Have Your Card Yet?

If you need medical care before your insurance card arrives, you have two options:

Option 1: Pay full price (100%) at the clinic, keep the receipt, and file for reimbursement later at your city hall or insurance provider. Bring your insurance card once you receive it plus the receipts. They'll refund you 70% of what you paid.

Option 2: Tell the clinic you're waiting for your insurance card. Many clinics can look up your enrollment in the national database if you provide your name, date of birth, and address. They'll charge you the 30% co-pay and bill the insurance system directly.

Option 2 is easier but not all clinics do this. Smaller clinics might insist on option 1.

Edge Case Scenarios That Confuse People

What If I Have a Job With Insurance But There's a Gap Before I Start?

If you arrive in Japan on March 1, register your address, but your job doesn't start until April 1, you're automatically enrolled in NHI for the month of March. Once your employment starts April 1, your employer enrolls you in employee health insurance and you're removed from NHI.

You'll receive an NHI premium bill for March only. Pay it. Don't ignore it thinking "but I have a job now." The systems track this and will keep billing you until you pay.

When you leave a job that provided health insurance, your coverage ends on your last day of employment. If there's any gap before your new job starts, you're automatically re-enrolled in NHI for that gap period.

Your old employer should give you a "certificate of loss of qualification" (資格喪失証明書 - shikaku sōshitsu shōmeisho). Take this to city hall to register for NHI if there's a gap. If you start your new job within a few days and the new employer provides insurance, you might skip NHI entirely.

If you switch jobs with no gap (last day Friday, start new job Monday), your new employer should handle the transition directly.

What If I'm a Student Working Part-Time?

If you're a student, you're on NHI unless your part-time job meets the thresholds for employer insurance enrollment (usually 20+ hours per week, earning over ¥88,000 per month, and meeting other conditions).

Most student part-time jobs don't meet these thresholds, so students stay on NHI and pay the low student rate (around ¥2,000-¥3,000 per month).

If your part-time employer does enroll you in their insurance, great. Your NHI automatically ends and you switch to employer insurance.

⚠️ WARNING: The Backdated Premium Trap

If you registered your address months ago but never received your insurance card or premium bill (maybe because you moved addresses, or mail got lost, or you registered a temporary address), you still owe premiums from your original registration date.

When the city hall finally catches up with you, they'll send a bill for all the months you weren't paying. This can be ¥20,000-¥100,000+ depending on how long it's been.

You can sometimes negotiate a payment plan, but you cannot escape the debt. "I didn't know I was enrolled" is not a valid excuse in Japan's system.

Register your address properly, update it when you move, and make sure you're receiving mail from city hall.

How to Pay Your NHI Premiums (And Save Money)

NHI premium bills arrive by mail every 2-3 months. Each bill covers multiple months (usually 2-4 months at a time).

Convenience store: Take the bill to any convenience store and pay cash. No fee.

Bank transfer: Pay at any bank. Small fee (¥100-¥200) depending on the bank.

Automatic withdrawal: Set up automatic withdrawal from your Japanese bank account. No fee, and some municipalities offer a small discount (¥50-¥100 per bill) for using this method.

Credit card: Some municipalities accept credit card payments online. Check your city website.

Frugal tip: Setting up automatic withdrawal saves you the hassle of remembering to pay and might get you a small discount. Go to your city hall with your bankbook and seal to set it up.
