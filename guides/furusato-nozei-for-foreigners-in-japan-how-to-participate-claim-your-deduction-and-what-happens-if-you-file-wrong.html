Furusato Nozei is a donation-based tax redirection system. You donate to a Japanese municipality, receive a regional gift, and that donation amount gets deducted from your resident tax bill the following year. On paper, it sounds like free goods for a small admin fee. In practice, foreigners get tripped up at the very first step: the deduction only exists if you actually owe resident tax. Donate without confirming your liability, and you pay full price for the gift with no recovery. ---

Are You Actually Eligible, or Are You About to Pay Full Price?

Eligibility for Furusato Nozei is not tied to nationality. It is tied to resident tax liability. Any foreigner registered as a resident in Japan who owes resident tax the following year can participate. The structural trap: **resident tax in Japan is assessed on the previous year's income, paid the year after.** If you arrived in Japan in 2024, your 2025 resident tax bill is calculated on 2024 income. If you arrived mid-2024 and earned a partial year's income, your bill may be very small. If you arrived in January 2025 and are donating in 2025, your 2026 resident tax bill is based on 2025 income -- and it does not arrive until June 2026. Donate heavily in 2025 expecting a deduction, and it will apply against that bill. That part works. The problem is year-one residents who donated in their first calendar year thinking the deduction applies immediately. It does not. **Year-one residents in Japan almost never benefit** because they have no prior-year resident tax bill to offset. The deduction is applied forward to next year's bill, not backward. **Worked example:** A Brazilian software engineer on an Engineer/Specialist in Humanities/International Services visa earned ¥4,000,000 gross in the previous calendar year. Using the standard simplified formula, the approximate deductible limit is calculated as: > (Previous year income - deductible expenses) x 0.2 + ¥2,000 For a salaried employee earning ¥4,000,000, after applying the employment income deduction (approximately ¥1,240,000), taxable income is roughly ¥2,760,000. The approximate Furusato Nozei deductible ceiling lands around **¥41,000 to ¥47,000** depending on family composition and other deductions. Donating beyond that ceiling means the excess is not deducted. You simply paid it. Use your gensen choushu hyo (withholding slip) from your employer to confirm last year's income. You submit a declaration form to each municipality you donated to, and the deduction is processed automatically against your resident tax. The deadline to apply for one-stop when donating is **January 10 of the year following your donation**. You must select the one-stop option at the time of donation, before December 31 of the donation year, and the physical form must arrive at the municipality by January 10. ---

How Do You Actually Complete a Donation?

> **[Step-by-Step Checklist]** > 1. Confirm your estimated deductible limit using last year's income > 2. Log into your chosen platform and locate the municipality/gift > 3. Enter your name and address **exactly as they appear on your juminhyo** > 4. Set your delivery address (can differ from registered address) > 5. Select the one-stop special declaration option if you are not filing kakutei shinkoku > 6. Complete payment > 7. Watch for the one-stop declaration form to arrive by mail (typically 1 to 3 weeks) > 8. Fill in the form, attach a copy of your My Number card (front and back) or residence card plus My Number notification > 9. Post the form to the municipality before January 10 The address step is where foreigners make a concrete, costly mistake. **Enter your address as it appears on your juminhyo, not your business card, not your company address.** The tax certificate that proves your deduction is issued to the address on file. If you enter a company address, the certificate goes there, your ward office cannot match it to your tax record, and the deduction fails to apply cleanly. Fixing it requires contacting the municipality directly and requesting reissuance -- a process that can take several weeks and is not guaranteed before the filing deadline. If you are unsure what your juminhyo says, or you recently moved and need to verify, see the guide on [changing your address in Japan](https://mykyoten.com/guides/changing-address-japan) and cross-check against your current registration. ---

One-Stop vs. Tax Return: Which Method Applies to You?

These are not interchangeable options you freely choose between. The rules determine which applies. **One-stop special declaration** is available if: you donated to five or fewer municipalities in the calendar year, and you are not required to file a kakutei shinkoku (annual tax return) for any other reason. **Kakutei shinkoku is required** if: you have freelance or side income above ¥200,000, you are self-employed, you have medical expense deductions to claim, you changed jobs during the year with a gap in withholding, or you earned income from overseas. > **[Warning Box]** > **The one-stop method is voided if you also file a tax return.** This catches foreigners off guard. A common scenario: a German engineer on an Engineer visa used the one-stop option in November. In February, his accountant files a kakutei shinkoku to claim a housing loan deduction. Filing the tax return automatically cancels the one-stop declarations he submitted. The municipalities have already processed them. His ward office now has conflicting records. The fix: when filing kakutei shinkoku, you must manually include all Furusato Nozei donations in the return as itemized deductions. The one-stop forms become irrelevant. If you know in advance that you will file a tax return, do not use one-stop at all -- list the donations directly in your kakutei shinkoku. If you discover the conflict after filing, contact your ward office tax counter immediately with both the one-stop receipt letters and your tax return filing confirmation. They can issue a corrective assessment (kouseizei), but the window is narrow. ---

**Over-donating past your limit** does not trigger a penalty. It simply means the excess donation receives no deduction. If your ceiling was ¥45,000 and you donated ¥80,000, you pay ¥35,000 with no tax benefit. There is no refund mechanism and no correction procedure. The money is gone as a donation. **The 30 percent product value rule:** Municipalities are required to limit the market value of gifts to 30 percent of the donation amount. This is a regulatory cap that has been tightened. Gifts that appear to exceed this threshold are flagged by the Ministry of Internal Affairs and Communications, and municipalities offering non-compliant gifts can be removed from certified platforms. If you donated to a municipality later removed from the list, reports from foreign residents indicate the donation still counts for tax purposes, but the gift may be cancelled or substituted. **Missing the one-stop January 10 deadline:** If your declaration form arrives at the municipality on January 11 or later, the one-stop option is void. Your deduction does not transfer automatically to your resident tax. You have one path remaining: file a kakutei shinkoku by March 15 and claim the donation as a deduction there. If you miss both deadlines, the deduction is permanently lost for that donation. **What if you already received the gift and then realize the deduction failed?** The gift is not returnable. The municipality received a legitimate donation. You have no recourse for the gift value. Your only remaining action is the kakutei shinkoku route described above if the March 15 deadline has not passed. Your next step: pull last year's withholding slip, confirm your estimated deductible ceiling using the formula above, and cross-check your registered address on your juminhyo before donating anything. Those two verifications take fifteen minutes and prevent every significant mistake described in this guide. ---

**Can a foreigner on a Working Holiday visa use Furusato Nozei?** Yes, if you are registered as a resident and will owe resident tax. Working Holiday visa holders who work and earn income in Japan are subject to resident tax the following year. The same eligibility logic applies: confirm your tax liability before donating. Year-one participants with short stays often see minimal benefit due to low or zero resident tax exposure. **Do I need a My Number card to participate?** You need a My Number to submit the one-stop declaration form. The form requires your My Number and a copy of an identity document. You can use your My Number notification letter plus your residence card as an alternative to the My Number card itself. If you have not yet obtained your My Number card, see the [My Number Card guide](https://mykyoten.com/guides/my-number-card-japan) for the application process. **If I leave Japan before the resident tax bill arrives, do I lose the deduction?** Potentially. If you leave Japan permanently, your resident tax for the year is settled at departure or collected from a tax representative. If the Furusato Nozei deduction has already been processed into your resident tax calculation, it reduces the amount owed. If you leave before the assessment is finalized and do not appoint a tax representative, collection becomes complicated.
