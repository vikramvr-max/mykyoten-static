Most foreigners in Japan sign their lease renewal documents the same day they receive them. The envelope arrives, it looks routine, the agent says "just sign here," and that is that. But buried in those pages can be rent increases you are not legally required to accept, new restrictions on pets or subletting that did not exist before, and guarantor fee structures that quietly doubled since your original contract. This guide tells you what to look for before you sign anything.

When Does Renewal Actually Happen, and What Triggers It?

Standard residential leases in Japan run for two years. Renewal is not automatic in the way most foreigners assume.

Under the **Borrowing Land and House Lease Act (借地借家法, Shakuchi Shakka Ho)**, a landlord who wants to change the contract terms at renewal must notify you **between 1 and 6 months before the lease expiry date**. If no notice arrives within that window, your lease continues under **jidou koshin (自動更新)**, meaning automatic renewal, with exactly the same terms as before.

Here is the part nobody tells you: if your lease renews automatically under jidou koshin, there is a legitimate legal argument that you do not owe the **koshinryo (更新料)**, the renewal fee. The renewal fee is typically one month's rent and is standard in Tokyo and some other urban areas. But it is a contractual obligation, not a statutory one. If the contract requires it only upon "formal renewal" and your lease rolls over automatically, review the exact wording before paying. Based on common experience reported by foreign residents and tenant advocacy groups, agents often invoice the fee anyway without clarifying this distinction.

A concrete example: if your lease expires on March 31 and you receive no renewal notice before October 1 of the prior year, the landlord has missed the legal window for mandated notice. The contract continues. Any attempt to change terms at that point requires your agreement.

**What if your landlord sends the renewal notice late, say three weeks before expiry?** That notice is technically outside the statutory window. You are not obligated to accept new terms on that timeline. Respond in writing, confirm receipt, and state that you require additional time to review. Do not let urgency pressure you into signing same-day.

What Three Clauses Do You Actually Need to Read?

Landlords can request a rent increase at renewal. They cannot impose one unilaterally.

Under **Land and House Lease Act Article 32 (借地借家法第32条)**, a tenant has the right to contest any rent increase that is not justified by changed circumstances such as rising property taxes, significant neighborhood market shifts, or inflation. If you and the landlord cannot agree, you continue paying your current rent while the dispute is processed. This is a genuine legal protection, not a technicality.

Look for this language in the renewal documents:

> 賃料を金○○円に改定する (Chintai wo kin XX en ni kaitei suru): "Rent is revised to XX yen"

If you see this in the renewal contract rather than in a separate negotiation letter, do not assume it is agreed. That clause requires your signature to become binding.

### 2. Changes to Guarantor Requirements

Many renewals now include a switch from personal guarantors (kojin hoshonin) to mandatory institutional guarantors (hoshogaisha). The contract may add language like:

> 保証会社の利用を必須とする (Hoshogaisha no riyo wo hissu to suru): "Use of a guarantee company is mandatory"

This was not in many original contracts signed before 2020. Landlords added it as standard practice following the Civil Code amendments of 2020 that capped personal guarantor liability. Basically, landlords shifted financial risk to institutional guarantors, and tenants now carry the annual cost.

Watch for additions to the **tokuyaku jiko (特約事項)** section. This is where landlords insert new pet bans, subletting prohibitions, smoking restrictions, or co-habitant approval requirements. If these conditions were not in your original contract, **you are not legally required to accept them at renewal** simply because they appear in the new document. Signing means consenting.

> [!WARNING BOX] > **Do not sign a renewal contract the same day you receive it.** Agents are trained to normalize urgency. You have the legal right to take several days to review the document. Anything presented as "just a formality" deserves a second read. Specifically check the tokuyaku jiko section on the final page, which is where new restrictions are buried.

How Does the Guarantor System Actually Work for Foreigners?

Personal guarantors (typically a Japanese national willing to co-sign your lease) have become nearly impossible for most foreigners to provide. The 2020 Civil Code amendments now require personal guarantors to have their maximum liability amount stated explicitly in the contract, which made many Japanese individuals reluctant to serve in this role.

The result: **hoshogaisha (保証会社)**, institutional rent guarantee companies, are now the standard. They charge between 0.5 and 1 month's rent at contract signing or renewal, plus an annual management fee of around 1,000 to 10,000 yen depending on the company.

For a 90,000 yen/month apartment in Tokyo, expect to pay approximately 45,000 to 90,000 yen to the guarantee company at renewal, on top of the koshinryo if applicable. That means a renewal can cost you 135,000 to 180,000 yen before you have changed anything.

**What if you cannot afford the guarantee company fee?** Some companies offer installment options. Others work with your employer to split the cost. Based on reports from foreign residents, companies like CASA, Jiid, and Lagleas are commonly used and some have English-language support lines. Ask your real estate agent which company is used and request their fee schedule in writing before renewal.

What Can a Landlord Legally Not Do at Renewal?

This is where people mess up. They assume that because a landlord has written something into the renewal document, it must be legal or enforceable.

It is not automatically either.

Under the Shakuchi Shakka Ho, a landlord **cannot**:

- Force you to accept new contract terms you have not agreed to - Raise rent without written notice and legally sufficient justification - Refuse to renew your lease solely because of your nationality

That last point is worth stating clearly. Discriminatory refusal to renew a lease on the basis of nationality violates Japanese civil law protections and is actionable. Practically, proving intent is difficult, but the legal protection exists. If a landlord cites your visa type as a renewal concern, that is different from citing nationality, and the situation is more ambiguous. If you are on a stable long-term visa and your [immigration compliance is current](https://mykyoten.com/guides/immigration-reporting-compliance-rules-japan), there is no legitimate visa-based grounds to deny renewal.

A Brazilian IT contractor on a Business Manager visa received a renewal notice in 2024 stating that because his visa was expiring in eight months, the landlord wanted the lease changed to a fixed-term contract (teiki shakka keiyaku). He had not been told this at signing. A fixed-term lease does not renew by default and puts all control at expiry in the landlord's hands. He contacted the Real Estate Dispute Resolution Centre, clarified that no such change was agreed in the original contract, and continued under his original standard contract terms.

How Do You Actually Negotiate a Renewal?

### Step-by-Step Renewal Negotiation

**Step 1: Acknowledge receipt in writing.** When the renewal notice arrives, reply by email or certified mail (haitatsushomeiyubin) confirming you received it and that you are reviewing the terms. Do this within five business days.

**Step 2: List every change from your original contract.** Compare the renewal document line by line against your original. Flag rent changes, guarantor changes, and anything new in the tokuyaku jiko section.

**Step 3: Respond to each point in writing.** Do not negotiate verbally. Write a short letter or email addressing each change. For rent increases, state that you are exercising your right to contest under Article 32 of the Shakuchi Shakka Ho if the increase is not supported by market evidence.

**Step 4: Request supporting documentation.** If the landlord claims the rent increase is justified by market rates, ask for written comparables. They are not legally required to provide them, but the request signals that you know your rights.

**Step 5: Attempt direct negotiation.** Many landlords will reduce or waive the koshinryo if you ask. Based on common experience, long-term tenants with no payment history issues have a reasonable position to negotiate. Agents earn a commission on koshinryo, so direct contact with the landlord is more effective than going through the agency.

**Step 6: Escalate if needed.** If the landlord is unresponsive or applying pressure, contact the **Real Estate Dispute Resolution Centre (fudosan kanren sodo shori iinkai, 不動産関連紛争処理委員会)**. Each prefecture has one. Tokyo's is operated through the Tokyo Metropolitan Government. There is no filing fee for initial consultation. They do not litigate but can mediate and clarify legal standing.

What Documents Do You Need and What Will This Actually Cost?

Required renewal documents typically include: an updated income certificate (所得証明書), an employment verification letter, guarantor income verification if applicable, a copy of your residence card, and bank account verification.

Income certificates must be current within 3 months of the renewal date. Get these from your city hall using your [My Number card](https://mykyoten.com/guides/my-number-card-japan) or by requesting one from your employer's HR department.

**Typical renewal costs breakdown:**

| Fee Type | Amount | Required | |---|---|---| | Renewal fee (koshinryo) | 1-2 months rent | Yes | | Administrative fee | ¥30,000-¥50,000 | Usually | | Guarantee company renewal | ¥10,000-¥20,000 | If applicable | | Fire insurance renewal | ¥15,000-¥25,000 | Yes | | Key money adjustment | ¥0-¥50,000 | Sometimes |

A Filipino nurse on a Specified Skilled Worker visa paid ¥180,000 total for her renewal on a ¥65,000/month apartment: ¥65,000 renewal fee, ¥35,000 administrative costs, ¥15,000 guarantee company fee, ¥20,000 insurance, plus ¥45,000 for a tatami replacement required by updated contract terms.

Employment verification letters need specific information: your current salary, employment start date, contract type (permanent or fixed-term), and supervisor contact information. Template letters from HR often miss the salary details management companies actually require.

**Document submission timing matters.** Submit everything at least 30 days before your lease expiration. Late submissions can result in month-to-month extensions at a higher monthly rent while processing completes.

Keep physical or digital copies of:

- Your original lease contract - Every renewal contract, signed and unsigned versions - All written communications with the landlord or agent - Receipts for all payments including guarantor fees and koshinryo - Any notices received by mail with postmarks intact

Honestly, the lease renewal process in Japan is not designed to harm you. But it is designed around the assumption that tenants will not push back. Most do not. The moment you respond in writing and cite the Shakuchi Shakka Ho, the conversation changes.

Your next step: Pull out your original lease contract today, check the expiry date, and count back six months. If you are inside that window right now and have not received a written renewal notice, you are already in a position of legal strength. Respond in writing before you do anything else.

**Can my landlord refuse to renew my lease because I am a foreigner?** Nationality alone is not a legally valid basis for non-renewal under Japanese civil law. Landlords must have substantial cause, such as rent arrears or property damage, to refuse renewal. If you suspect nationality-based discrimination, consult the Real Estate Dispute Resolution Centre in your prefecture.

**Do I have to pay the renewal fee (koshinryo) every two years?** Only if your contract requires it and the renewal is a formal bilateral renewal. If the lease renewed automatically under jidou koshin because neither party initiated a formal renewal, review your contract wording carefully. The obligation depends on specific contract language, not on the passage of time alone.

**What happens if I refuse to sign the renewal document?** Your existing lease continues under its current terms until either party takes formal legal action. A landlord cannot terminate your tenancy simply because you refused to sign a renewal with changed terms. You remain a legal tenant. Refusal to accept new conditions is not the same as refusing to renew.
