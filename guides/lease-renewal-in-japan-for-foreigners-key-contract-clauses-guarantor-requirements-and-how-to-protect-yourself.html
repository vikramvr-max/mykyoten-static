When Does Renewal Actually Happen, and What Triggers It?

Standard residential leases in Japan run for two years. Renewal is not automatic in the way most foreigners assume. Under the **Borrowing Land and House Lease Act (借地借家法, Shakuchi Shakka Ho)**, a landlord who wants to change the contract terms at renewal must notify you **between 1 and 6 months before the lease expiry date**. If no notice arrives within that window, your lease continues under **jidou koshin (自動更新)** — automatic renewal — with exactly the same terms as before. What Three Clauses Do You Actually Need to Read?

### 1. Rent Increase Clauses Landlords can request a rent increase at renewal. They cannot impose one unilaterally. Under **Land and House Lease Act Article 32 (借地借家法第32条)**, a tenant has the right to contest any rent increase that is not justified by changed circumstances such as rising property taxes, significant neighborhood market shifts, or inflation. If you and the landlord cannot agree, you continue paying your current rent while the dispute is processed. This is a genuine legal protection, not a technicality. How Does the Guarantor System Actually Work for Foreigners?

Personal guarantors — typically a Japanese national willing to co-sign your lease — have become nearly impossible for most foreigners to provide. The 2020 Civil Code amendments now require personal guarantors to have their maximum liability amount stated explicitly in the contract, which made many Japanese individuals reluctant to serve in this role. What Can a Landlord Legally Not Do at Renewal?

This is where people mess up. They assume that because a landlord has written something into the renewal document, it must be legal or enforceable. It is not automatically either. Under the Shakuchi Shakka Ho, a landlord **cannot**: - Force you to accept new contract terms you have not agreed to - Raise rent without written notice and legally sufficient justification - Refuse to renew your lease solely because of your nationality That last point is worth stating clearly. Discriminatory refusal to renew a lease on the basis of nationality violates Japanese civil law protections and is actionable. Practically, proving intent is difficult, but the legal protection exists. If a landlord cites your visa type as a renewal concern, that is different from citing nationality, and the situation is more ambiguous. Write a short letter or email addressing each change.
