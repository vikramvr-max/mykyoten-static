Foreign workers in Japan are disproportionately targeted for unpaid or excessive overtime because employers assume, often correctly, that they will not know their rights or fear visa consequences if they speak up. This guide exists to close that gap.

What Are the Actual Legal Limits on Overtime in Japan?

The revised Labour Standards Act, which came into full force in 2019 and applies today, sets a hard cap of **45 hours of overtime per month and 360 hours per year** as the standard limit. This is not a guideline. It is the legal default.

The catch is, many companies use a **36-kyotei (三六協定)** (a written labour-management agreement named after Article 36 of the Labour Standards Act) to extend those limits. With a valid 36-kyotei, an employer can legally require additional overtime. But even then, the absolute ceiling is **100 hours in a single month**, and no more than **80 hours per month averaged across any two-to-six month period**.

Exceeding 100 hours in a single month is not a grey area. It is a **criminal violation by the employer**, punishable by up to six months imprisonment or a fine of up to ¥300,000 per violation. You, as the worker, have no legal obligation to work those hours. Honestly, many foreign workers do not know this and simply comply. That compliance does not make the overtime legal.

Does Your Company Actually Have a Valid 36-Kyotei Agreement?

Here is the part nobody tells you: a 36-kyotei is only valid if it was filed with and accepted by the **Labour Standards Inspection Office (労働基準監督署, Rodo Kijun Kantokusho)** before the overtime was assigned. A company that simply hands you an employment contract saying "overtime may be required" has not met this requirement.

1. Ask your HR department or direct manager in writing (email is fine) for a copy of the current 36-kyotei agreement 2. Check the document for: the specific overtime limits stated, the date of the agreement, signatures from both an employer representative and a worker representative, and the stamp from the Labour Standards Inspection Office confirming receipt 3. If they cannot produce one, or produce a document without the inspection office stamp, your company may be operating without a valid agreement

You can also verify directly. Visit or call the Labour Standards Inspection Office that has jurisdiction over your company's location. They maintain filed agreements and can confirm whether one exists. You do not need your employer's permission to do this.

**If no valid agreement exists:** any overtime beyond the standard 8-hour workday is already a violation, regardless of how much your contract says otherwise.

> **Warning Box** > Reports from foreign residents suggest employers sometimes show workers an internal policy document and describe it as a 36-kyotei. These are not the same thing. A genuine 36-kyotei has a government stamp. If what you are seeing is a PDF with only your company's letterhead, it does not count.

How Is Overtime Pay Actually Calculated, and How Do You Spot Underpayment?

Japanese law specifies minimum overtime premium rates:

- **Standard overtime** (beyond 8 hours/day or 40 hours/week): base hourly rate **+ 25%** - **Monthly overtime exceeding 60 hours**: base hourly rate **+ 50%** - **Late-night work** (between 10pm and 5am): base hourly rate **+ 35%** - **Late-night overtime**: these rates stack, so late-night work beyond 60 hours/month triggers a **+ 85%** premium

**To find your base hourly rate from a monthly salary:**

Divide your monthly salary by the number of prescribed working hours in that month. For a standard 40-hour week, this is roughly 173 hours per month (2,080 hours per year divided by 12).

**Concrete example:** A Filipino nurse on a Specified Skilled Worker visa earns ¥250,000 per month. Her base hourly rate is approximately ¥1,445 (¥250,000 ÷ 173). For standard overtime, she must be paid at least ¥1,806 per hour (¥1,445 x 1.25). If her payslip shows a flat ¥1,000 per overtime hour, she is being underpaid by at least ¥806 per hour of overtime worked.

> **Reality Check** > Based on common experience among foreign workers in Japan, the most frequent underpayment method is not outright refusal to pay overtime. It is including overtime pay inside the base salary as an "all-inclusive" or "fixed overtime" (固定残業代, kotei zangyo dai) arrangement. This is legal only if the agreement explicitly states the number of hours it covers and the amount, and only up to the legal caps. If your contract has a fixed overtime clause but does not specify a number of hours, that clause may be unenforceable.

Do These Laws Actually Apply to You as a Foreign National?

**Labour Standards Act Article 3** explicitly prohibits employers from discriminating in working conditions based on nationality. This means the same overtime limits, premium rates, and filing protections apply whether you hold an Engineer/Specialist in Humanities/International Services visa, a Specified Skilled Worker visa, a Highly Skilled Professional visa, or any other work-authorised status.

The threat you are most likely to hear is: "If you complain, it could affect your visa." This is either a misunderstanding or deliberate intimidation. Filing a labour complaint does not trigger immigration review. The Labour Standards Inspection Office and the Immigration Bureau operate independently. One does not notify the other when a labour complaint is filed.

Your visa status is tied to your employment category and conduct, not to whether you exercised a legal right. If anything, a pattern of being denied legal wages is documented employer misconduct, not worker misconduct. If you want to understand how your specific visa type intersects with employment protections, the guide on [work visas and employment stability](https://mykyoten.com/guides/work-visas-employment-stability-japan) covers this in detail.

**What if your employer threatens non-renewal of your visa sponsorship as retaliation for a complaint?** This is a separate legal issue. Retaliatory dismissal or non-renewal tied to a labour complaint can itself be challenged through the labour tribunal system. Document the threat in writing if it occurs.

How Do You Actually File a Complaint: Step by Step

**Step 1: Gather your evidence before you do anything else**

You need: payslips (全 months available), your employment contract, any time records you have access to (IC card logs, email timestamps, shift schedules), and any written communications about overtime expectations.

**Step 2: Contact the Labour Consultation Hotline first if you want to talk it through**

The number is **0120-811-610**, available in multiple languages including English, Chinese, and others depending on the regional service. It is free. Call this before walking into an office if you are unsure of your situation.

**Step 3: Visit the Labour Standards Inspection Office with jurisdiction over your employer's location**

Find the correct office via the Ministry of Health, Labour and Welfare website using your company's address. Bring all documents from Step 1. You can file anonymously or under your name. Anonymous complaints limit the remedies available to you personally, but they trigger an inspection that can benefit other workers at the same company.

**Step 4: File a formal complaint (申告, shinkoku)**

The inspector will review your materials. Inspections typically begin within a few weeks for straightforward wage claims. Complex cases involving multiple workers or disputed records take longer.

You can recover: **unpaid overtime back pay for up to three years**, the applicable premium rates, and in egregious cases courts have awarded additional damages. Back pay awards are paid by the employer directly following either an administrative order or a civil judgment.

> **Step-by-Step Checklist** > - [ ] Collect all payslips (minimum last 3 months, ideally 3 years) > - [ ] Pull your employment contract and locate any overtime or fixed overtime clauses > - [ ] Request a copy of the 36-kyotei from your employer in writing > - [ ] Calculate your correct overtime rate using the base hourly rate method above > - [ ] Identify specific payslip lines showing underpayment or no overtime payment > - [ ] Call 0120-811-610 to confirm your situation and get language-appropriate guidance > - [ ] Locate the correct Labour Standards Inspection Office for your employer's address > - [ ] File your complaint with documentation in hand

**What if you have already left the company?** You can still file. The three-year statute of limitations on wage claims runs from the date each wage payment was due, not from your last day of employment. A German engineer on an Engineer/Specialist visa who left a company in 2024 can still file claims for unpaid overtime going back to 2022 as of 2025.

**What if your employer retaliates by firing you before you file?** Termination of a foreign national does have immigration implications. Are There Different Overtime Rules for Management-Track Foreigners?

**Yes, but probably not the way your company explained it.**

The "management exemption" (管理監督者) only applies to employees with genuine managerial authority. You must have the power to hire and fire subordinates, set their work schedules, and make independent business decisions affecting company direction.

Having a fancy title like "Senior Manager" or "Director" means nothing legally. Many foreign workers get misclassified as exempt managers to avoid overtime payments.

**Real management exemption requires:** - Authority over other employees' working conditions - Power to make hiring and firing decisions - Discretion in your own working hours and methods - Compensation reflecting your managerial responsibilities

If you punch a timecard, follow set working hours, or need permission for basic decisions, you are not legally a manager regardless of your title.

> **Reality Check** > Some companies use "fixed overtime" systems claiming to pay for predetermined overtime hours within your base salary. This is legal only if the contract clearly separates base pay from overtime pay portions. Many contracts written in Japanese obscure these distinctions, making the arrangements illegal under labour standards.

**What if you are genuinely management-level?** Even exempt managers receive legal protections. You still get premium pay for holiday work and late-night hours. Companies cannot require unlimited unpaid work from anyone.

**Can my employer reduce my base salary to offset overtime costs?** No. Your base salary cannot be retroactively reduced to absorb overtime premiums. The overtime premium must be calculated on the original base rate. Any clause in your contract attempting to bundle all future overtime into a lower base salary without specifying hours covered is likely unenforceable and worth flagging to an inspector.

**If I work part-time on a Student visa, do these overtime rules apply to me?** The same premium rates apply once you exceed 8 hours in a day or 40 hours in a week. Student visa holders are also restricted to 28 hours of work per week in total. Exceeding that limit is an immigration compliance issue separate from the labour law issue. Both can apply simultaneously.

**Does a company need a 36-kyotei if the overtime is voluntary?** Under Japanese law, "voluntary" overtime at an employer's request or benefit is still regulated overtime. If your employer asks you to stay late, the 36-kyotei requirement and premium pay obligations apply regardless of how the request is framed.
