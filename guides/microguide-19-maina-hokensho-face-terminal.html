How the maina hokensho check-in terminals work at Japanese clinics, why the face scanner fails 20 percent of the time, and what to do when it does.

As of January 2026, almost every clinic and hospital in Japan has installed these maina hokensho check-in terminals (マイナ保険証受付機). The government subsidized the rollout hard in 2024-2025, so even tiny neighborhood clinics have them now. They're usually boxy kiosks near the reception desk with a card reader slot, a camera at eye level, and a touchscreen.

Here's the deal: The old pink paper health insurance cards are gone. The terminals are designed to work only with your Residence Card (在留カード) (the merged residence/My Number card). If you show up without your card or refuse to use the terminal, you'll either get charged full price upfront (100% of the bill) or get sent to the manual reception desk where the staff will sigh heavily and spend 10 minutes inputting your info by hand.

Step 1: Find the terminal Walk into the clinic. Look for a machine that's about waist-height with a sign saying "マイナ保険証受付" or "本人確認" (identity verification). It'll usually have a card slot on top and a screen showing instructions in Japanese and (sometimes) English.

Step 2: Insert your Residence Card (在留カード) Slide your card face-up into the slot. The machine will beep and the screen will switch to a menu. If this is your first time using this specific clinic's terminal, it'll say something like "初回利用です" (first-time use).

Step 3: Set your 4-digit PIN (if you haven't already) If you haven't set a PIN for your card's health insurance function, the machine will prompt you to do it now. Enter 4 numbers. Write this down immediately. People forget their PIN within a week and then get stuck at the terminal looking stupid.

The vibe: The receptionist won't help you set your PIN-it's a privacy thing. You're on your own. If you mess up and enter the wrong PIN three times, the machine locks and you'll need to go to city hall to reset it (takes 30-60 minutes).

The machine compares your live face to the photo stored in your My Number database (the one from your Residence Card (在留カード) application). If it matches, you'll hear a cheerful "ピンポン!" (ping-pong!) sound and the screen says "確認できました" (confirmed).

Step 5: Confirm your insurance details The screen shows your name, insurance type (national or company), and co-pay rate (usually 30%). Tap "確認" (confirm). Your card pops out. Done. The receptionist will call your name when the doctor's ready.

Here's a weird quirk: If you use the manual paper check-in instead of the terminal (because you forgot your card or refused to scan), some clinics charge an extra ¥10-30 administrative fee per visit. It's buried in the bill as "書面発行手数料" (paper issuance fee).

The terminal check-in is free. So if you're going to the doctor regularly (chronic condition, pregnancy check-ups, etc.), using the terminal saves you ¥100-300 per year. Not life-changing, but it adds up.

Also: Some clinics give a ¥20 discount if you use the terminal because it reduces their admin workload. Look for signs at reception saying "マイナ保険証利用で割引" (discount for using maina hokensho).

Children under 15 can't have their own Residence Card (在留カード) (they're dependents on their parent's insurance). So how do you check them in?

Option 1: Use the terminal with your card, then tell the receptionist you're here for your child. They'll manually add your kid's info.

Option 2: Some newer terminals have a "dependent check-in" option (被扶養者受付). Insert your card, tap this button, and enter your child's name and birthdate.

The vibe: Most clinics still default to option 1 because the dependent feature is buggy. Just scan your own card and let the receptionist handle the rest.
