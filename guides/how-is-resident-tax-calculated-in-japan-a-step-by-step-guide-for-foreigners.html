Not knowing how your resident tax is calculated means you cannot plan your budget, cannot catch errors on your notice, and may pay thousands of yen more than you legally owe.

Resident tax hits differently when you have no idea where the number came from. Most foreigners just pay the bill. That is a mistake.

Resident tax in Japan has two separate components, and most people only think about one of them.

The first is the **income-proportional levy (所得割)**. This is roughly 10% of your taxable income, split between your prefecture (4%) and your municipality (6%). The rate is uniform across Japan by national law, though some municipalities have minor surcharges.

The second is the **per-capita flat levy (均等割)**. This is a fixed annual charge that every resident pays regardless of income, typically around ¥5,000 per year total. Some municipalities charge slightly more. Tokyo's special wards, for example, have charged ¥1,500 for the prefecture component and ¥3,500 for the ward component, but verify this with your specific ward office because amounts can differ.

Here is the part nobody tells you: **resident tax is calculated on last year's income and billed the following year**. If you arrived in Japan mid-year and started working, your first bill can arrive 12 to 16 months after you started earning. Then the second year hits and people treat it like a surprise attack. It is not a surprise if you understand the timeline. For a deeper look at why that second-year bill feels so brutal, read the [resident tax second year shock guide](https://mykyoten.com/guides/microguide-20-resident-tax-second-year-shock).

How Do You Actually Calculate It, Step by Step?

The calculation follows a fixed sequence. Every step reduces your taxable base.

**Step 1: Start with gross employment income.** This is your total salary before any deductions. For a company employee, it is what appears on your gensen choshu ticket (源泉徴収票) in the "支払金額" box.

**Step 2: Apply the employment income deduction (給与所得控除).** This is a government-mandated deduction that accounts for work-related expenses. You do not need receipts. It is applied automatically based on a sliding scale. For gross income of ¥3,500,000, the deduction is ¥1,020,000 (as of current rules). This gives you employment income of ¥2,480,000.

**Step 3: Subtract personal deductions.** These include: - Basic deduction (基礎控除): ¥430,000 for most taxpayers - Social insurance deduction: the full amount you paid in national health insurance or employee health insurance premiums, plus national pension contributions - Spouse, dependent, and disability deductions if applicable

**Step 4: Arrive at taxable income.** Taxable income = Employment income minus all personal deductions.

**Step 5: Apply the 10% rate, then add the flat levy.**

> **[Step-by-Step Checklist]** > - [ ] Locate your gensen choshu ticket from your employer > - [ ] Confirm the "支払金額" (gross salary) figure > - [ ] Look up your employment income deduction on the NTA table > - [ ] Add up all social insurance premiums you paid that year > - [ ] Subtract basic deduction (¥430,000) > - [ ] Multiply taxable income by 10% > - [ ] Add flat levy (approximately ¥5,000) > - [ ] Compare result to your municipality's tax notice

Which Deductions Do Foreigners Commonly Miss?

Honestly, this is where people mess up most often.

**National health insurance premiums paid out of pocket.** If you are self-employed or between jobs and paying NHI directly, every yen you paid is deductible. But it only gets applied if you reported it, either through year-end adjustment at your company or through a resident tax filing (住民税申告) at the municipal office by March 15.

**National pension contributions.** If you pay kokumin nenkin directly (¥16,980 per month in 2025), those are fully deductible. Again, only if declared. Your employer handles this automatically if you are on shakai hoken, but freelancers and those between jobs need to declare it themselves. The [pension and social insurance guide](https://mykyoten.com/guides/japanese-pension-social-insurance-foreigners) covers this in detail.

**Spouse and dependent deductions.** If your spouse lives in Japan and earns under ¥1,030,000, you likely qualify for a spouse deduction. Many foreign residents with dependants living abroad can also claim dependent deductions under specific conditions, but this requires documentation including remittance records.

> **[Reality Check]** > Based on reports from foreign residents, many people who arrived mid-year and paid NHI premiums for several months before switching to employer insurance never declare those NHI payments. That can easily mean ¥100,000 to ¥200,000 in undeducted premiums, which translates to ¥10,000 to ¥20,000 in resident tax overpaid. Multiply that over several years and the number becomes significant.

**What if you left a job mid-year and nobody did your year-end adjustment?** You need to file a final income tax return (確定申告) between February 16 and March 15 for the prior year. The municipality then uses that data to calculate your resident tax. If you skip it, deductions disappear and your bill inflates.

**What if you were enrolled in both NHI for part of the year and shakai hoken for the rest?** You can deduct both. Add up all premiums paid across both systems for that calendar year and declare the total. The catch is, you need payment receipts or your nenkin / NHI payment history from each insurer.

How Do You Verify Your Tax Notice and Fix Errors?

Your municipality mails out resident tax notices (住民税決定通知書) every June. Most people glance at the total and ignore the rest. Do not do that.

The notice shows taxable income, each deduction applied, and the resulting tax. If any deduction is missing, you can see it directly. Use the [Resident Tax Notice Decoder](https://mykyoten.com/tools/decode/resident-tax-notice) to walk through each field on your notice in plain language.

Many municipalities also have online tax simulators on their official websites. Search for your city's name plus "住民税 試算" and you will usually find one. Input your income and deductions and compare the result to your notice.

> **[Warning Box]** > The deadline to file a correction request (更正の請求 or 修正申告) for resident tax is generally **5 years** from the original filing date for claiming additional refunds. However, in practice, the municipal office can process corrections going back several years if you bring documentation. Do not assume it is too late without asking. Bring your gensen choshu ticket, social insurance payment receipts, and your original tax notice. Go to the market civil tax (市民税) counter, not the general inquiry window.

If you believe your notice contains an error, you can file a written objection (異議申立て) within **3 months** of receiving the notice. Missing that window means you lose the right to formally challenge that specific year's assessment.

Your next step: Pull out your most recent resident tax notice (or wait for June if it has not arrived yet), locate your gensen choshu ticket from the same income year, and run the calculation in this guide manually. **Do I pay resident tax if I only lived in Japan for part of the year?** Resident tax is assessed based on whether you were registered as a resident on January 1 of the tax year. If you were registered on January 1, you owe the full year's resident tax, even if you leave Japan in February. If you arrived after January 1, you typically owe nothing for that assessment year.

**What happens if I do not pay resident tax?** Unpaid resident tax accrues late fees (延滞金) at approximately 2.4% to 8.7% annually depending on how late the payment is. Continued non-payment can lead to wage garnishment or asset seizure. It also creates problems for permanent residency applications, which require a clean tax payment record. See the [permanent residency guide](https://mykyoten.com/guides/permanent-residency-japan) for how this affects your PR eligibility.

**My employer said they handle everything. Do I need to do anything?** For company employees, year-end adjustment (年末調整) covers most deductions automatically. But if you paid NHI, national pension out of pocket, or have dependants your employer does not know about, you need to file a resident tax declaration yourself at the municipal office by March 15. Employer processing does not capture everything.
