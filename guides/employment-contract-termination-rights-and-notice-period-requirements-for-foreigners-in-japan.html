Getting terminated from your job in Japan as a foreigner hits differently than back home.

You are not just losing income. Your visa status hangs in the balance, and Japanese labor law has specific requirements your employer must follow regardless of your nationality.

**The stakes are higher when your legal residency depends on employment.**

What are the minimum notice period requirements my employer must give me?

Japanese Labor Standards Act requires **30 days advance notice** for contract termination, or payment equivalent to 30 days of your average daily wage if terminated immediately.

This is not a suggestion. It is legally mandated.

Your employer cannot simply tell you "today is your last day" without paying dismissal allowance (kaisho teate). The calculation works like this: your total salary from the past three months divided by total working days in that period, multiplied by 30.

For example, if you earned ¥900,000 over 60 working days in the past three months, your daily wage is ¥15,000. **Immediate termination requires ¥450,000 dismissal allowance.**

Many foreign workers do not know this right exists. Companies sometimes bank on this ignorance.

[Warning Box] **Common Employer Violation:** Some companies claim "probationary period" employees (typically first 14 days) are exempt from notice requirements. This is false. After 14 days of employment, 30-day notice rules apply to all workers including foreigners.

The catch is enforcement. Labor Standards Inspection Office (rodoukijun kantokusho) handles violations, but you must file a complaint within two years. Companies rarely face criminal penalties, but they will be ordered to pay what they owe you.

Here is the part nobody tells you: **even if your contract says shorter notice periods, Japanese law supersedes contract terms.** Your employment contract cannot waive these statutory protections.

Can my employer fire me without cause, and what protections do I have?

Japanese employment law provides stronger job security than most countries, even for foreigners.

**Employers need "objectively reasonable grounds" for dismissal.** Simply saying "we do not need you anymore" is insufficient under the Civil Code Article 628.

Valid termination grounds include: - Serious misconduct or criminal behavior - Extended inability to perform job duties - Economic necessity requiring workforce reduction (with strict procedural requirements) - Fundamental breach of employment obligations

Economic dismissals (整理解雇) require employers to prove four conditions: genuine business necessity, efforts to avoid dismissal, fair selection criteria, and adequate consultation with affected employees.

A British marketing specialist on a Humanities visa discovered this when his company tried terminating him to "restructure." The company had not attempted salary cuts, reduced hours, or reassignment to other departments first. He successfully challenged the dismissal through local labor bureau mediation.

[Reality Check] **Unwritten Reality:** Many foreign employees accept "voluntary resignation" (自己都合退職) to avoid confrontation, not realizing this classification affects unemployment benefits and reference letters. Company pressure to resign "voluntarily" instead of formal dismissal is common but legally questionable if done coercively.

Wrongful termination disputes typically resolve through: 1. **Labor bureau mediation** (free, 2-3 months) 2. **Labor tribunal** (¥20,000-50,000, 3-6 months) 3. **Civil court** (expensive, 1-2 years)

Most cases settle during mediation. Companies prefer avoiding formal proceedings that create official records of labor law violations.

How does job termination affect my visa status and what must I report?

Losing your job does not immediately invalidate your residence card, but it starts a compliance countdown.

**You have 14 days to report employment termination** to immigration authorities using the [notification system](https://mykyoten.com/tools/immigration-risk-checker). Missing this deadline can result in fines up to ¥200,000 or impact future visa renewals and permanent residency applications.

Your work visa remains valid until its expiration date, giving you time to find new employment. However, extended unemployment without job-seeking activity can be grounds for visa revocation.

[Step-by-Step Checklist] **Immigration Reporting Process:** 1. Log into immigration online notification system within 14 days 2. Select "contracting organization change" notification 3. Input termination date and reason 4. Submit notification confirmation number record 5. Begin documenting job search activities (save application records, interview confirmations)

The tricky part: immigration expects "reasonable efforts" to find new employment. Exactly what constitutes reasonable effort is undefined, but **maintaining detailed job search records** protects you during visa renewal.

If your visa expires before finding new employment, you can apply for a **Designated Activities visa for job searching** (最大6ヶ月). This requires proof of job-hunting activities and sufficient financial resources.

Honestly, this is where people mess up. They assume having savings means they can take extended breaks between jobs. Immigration views long unemployment periods skeptically, especially for work visa holders.

What documents must my employer provide when terminating my contract?

Your employer has legal obligations to provide specific documentation upon termination.

**Required documents include:**

**Dismissal notice (kaisho tsuchi)** stating termination date, reason, and any dismissal allowance calculation. This document is crucial for unemployment benefits and potential legal challenges.

**Final payslip breakdown** showing salary calculation, deductions, and any outstanding payments. Use the [Payslip Decoder](https://mykyoten.com/tools/payslip-decoder) to verify all calculations are correct, especially if dismissal allowance is included.

**Tax withholding certificate (gensen choshuhyo)** for the current tax year. You need this for tax filing and providing to your next employer.

**Employment certificate (zaishoku shomeisho)** if requested, stating employment period and job duties. Some companies resist providing this, but you are entitled to request it.

Social insurance paperwork varies by company, but they should process your health insurance and pension withdrawals and provide relevant documentation.

The reality is enforcement depends on your willingness to pursue it. Many foreign workers accept incomplete documentation to avoid conflict, but this can cause problems with unemployment benefits, tax filing, and your next job application.

What immediate steps should I take to protect my rights and legal status?

**Document everything immediately.**

Take photos of your computer screen showing termination emails, record meeting details if terminated verbally, and save any company communications about the dismissal.

Request all required documentation in writing. Email your HR department listing specific documents you need and when you expect to receive them. Keep records of these requests.

Contact your local Hello Work office within 7 days if you plan to claim unemployment benefits. Bring your dismissal notice, employment certificate, and residence card. Benefits typically range from 50-80% of your previous salary for 90-330 days depending on employment history and age.

**Financial preparation matters.** Calculate your runway including any dismissal allowance, final salary, and potential unemployment benefits. Immigration wants proof you can support yourself during job searching.

Begin job hunting immediately, even if you plan to take time off. Document applications, interviews, and networking activities. This documentation protects you if immigration questions your job-seeking efforts.

Consider whether to challenge the dismissal. If termination seems unfair or procedural requirements were not met, consult with your local labor standards office within days, not weeks. Evidence becomes harder to gather over time.

**What if my employer refuses to provide dismissal allowance?** File a complaint with the Labor Standards Inspection Office. Bring employment contract, recent payslips, and documentation of the termination. They can order payment and investigate other labor law violations.

**What if I am on a dependent visa and my spouse loses their job?** The dependent visa holder is not directly required to report, but if the primary visa holder's status becomes invalid, it affects your status too. Monitor their compliance with reporting requirements and job search progress.

Your next step: Report your employment termination to immigration within 14 days using their online notification system, then immediately request all required documentation from your employer in writing to ensure you have everything needed for unemployment benefits and your job search.

**Q: Can my employer terminate me during paid leave or sick leave?** A: Termination during legally mandated leave periods faces higher scrutiny and often requires special approval from labor standards offices. The timing alone can be evidence of wrongful dismissal, especially if related to the reason for leave.

**Q: Do I need to return my residence card if I lose my job?** A: No, keep your residence card. It remains valid until its expiration date. You only surrender it when leaving Japan permanently or if immigration revokes your status for compliance violations.

**Q: How long can I stay unemployed on a work visa?** A: There is no specific time limit, but extended unemployment without job-seeking efforts can lead to visa revocation. Most immigration officers expect active job hunting within 1-2 months of termination.
