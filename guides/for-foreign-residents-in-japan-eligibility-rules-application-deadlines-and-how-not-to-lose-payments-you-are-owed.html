Foreign families in Japan are leaving up to ¥180,000 per year unclaimed. Not because the money does not exist. Because nobody told them 児童手当 (jidou teate) applies to them too, and because the application window is brutally short. Miss it, and those months are gone forever.

Do Foreign Residents Actually Qualify for 児童手当?

Basically, yes. If you are registered on the **jumin kisai** (住民基本台帳), meaning you have a valid residence registration at your local city hall, you are eligible to apply. This includes most mid-to-long term visa holders: Engineer/Specialist in Humanities, Skilled Labor, Specified Skilled Worker, Spouse of Japanese National, Permanent Resident, and others.

- You hold a valid **zairyu card** with a status of residence that permits medium or long-term stay - You are **registered in the juminhyo** (住民票) and your child is listed on it - Your child is under 18 years old and living in Japan (the child does not need to be a Japanese national) - You are the primary earner or the parent with higher income handles the claim

**Short-stay visa holders are excluded.** Tourist visa, temporary visitor, or diplomatic status do not qualify. The system is tied to proper resident registration, not just physical presence.

The shotoku seigen (所得制限) was technically abolished under the October 2024 reform. Prior to that reform, high-income households received a reduced tokubetsu kyufu (特別給付) of ¥5,000 per child per month. From the 2024 reform forward, **the income cap was removed entirely**, meaning all qualifying families receive full payments regardless of household income. This was one of the most significant structural changes to the system in years.

Here is the part nobody tells you. If one parent lives outside Japan, the parent residing in Japan applies and receives the allowance. The system looks at the **seikeiji** (生計維持者), the person providing financial support for the child. If both parents are overseas and the child lives with a guardian in Japan, that guardian can apply. If neither parent is registered in Japan, eligibility collapses. The child must be living with at least one registered person who has accepted legal guardianship.

If your spouse is on a [dependent visa](https://mykyoten.com/guides/dependent-visa-rules) and lives in Japan, they are part of your household registration and the application is straightforward.

| Child Age / Order | Monthly Amount (from Oct 2024) | |---|---| | Under 3 years old | ¥15,000 | | Age 3 to 12 (1st and 2nd child) | ¥10,000 | | Age 3 to 18 (3rd child onward) | ¥30,000 | | Age 12 to 18 (1st and 2nd child) | ¥10,000 |

The **¥30,000 rule for the third child** was introduced with the October 2024 reform and represents a substantial increase. A family with three children where the youngest is under 3 is now collecting ¥15,000 + ¥10,000 + ¥30,000 = ¥55,000 per month. That is ¥660,000 per year.

Payments are made three times per year, typically in **February, June, and October**, covering the preceding four-month period each time.

> **Warning Box:** The 2024 reform also extended eligibility from middle school age (under 15) to high school age (under 18). If you have a child aged 15 to 17 who was previously off the system, you needed to apply through your municipality. If you have not done that yet, check immediately. Retroactive payment is not guaranteed.

This is where people mess up. The application must be submitted **within 15 days of the triggering event**. The triggering events are:

- Child is born - You move into a new municipality - You obtain a status that makes you newly eligible

If you apply on **day 14**, your payment is calculated from the month of the triggering event. If you apply on **day 16**, payment starts from the **following month**. One day can cost you one full month of payments.

**Numeric example:** A Brazilian software engineer on an Engineer/Specialist in Humanities visa has a baby born on March 3rd. He applies on March 14th (day 11). His allowance starts from March, and he collects ¥15,000 for that month in the February payment cycle. If he had applied on March 20th instead, March drops out permanently. At ¥15,000 per month, that is ¥15,000 gone with no appeal mechanism.

The payment date does not count from your child's birth. It counts from your **application date**. This is a hard administrative rule with no exceptions based on standard practice across Japanese municipalities.

The counter you want is called **子育て支援課** (kosodate shien ka) or **児童福祉課** (jidou fukushi ka). In smaller municipalities it may be combined into a general citizen services counter. Ask for 児童手当の申請 (jidou teate no shinsei).

> **Step-by-Step Checklist: Documents for Foreign Applicants** > > 1. **Zairyu card** (yours, the applicant) > 2. **Juminhyo** showing your child's name and your address (issued within the last 3 months) > 3. **Health insurance certificate** (kenko hoken card or certificate of enrollment) > 4. **Bank account details** in the applicant's name (not the child's name) > 5. **Employer certificate** (kinmu jokyo todoke) if you are a salaried employee, your HR or payroll department issues this > 6. **Child's birth certificate** or mother-child handbook (boshi techo) if juminhyo does not yet reflect the birth > 7. **My Number** for all household members (you do not need the physical card, the number is sufficient)

Processing time before first payment is typically **1 to 2 months** after submission. You will receive a decision notice by mail. If your Japanese reading is limited, the [Document Decoder](https://mykyoten.com/tools/document-decoder) can help you parse what the notice actually says.

Honest on-the-ground reality: based on reports from foreign residents, some city hall counters are unfamiliar with applying the rules to non-Japanese families and may initially hesitate or ask for documents not actually required by law. Bring the items on the list above. Politely insist. You have the legal right to apply.

Every June, your municipality sends a **現況届 (genkyou todoke)**, sometimes called a 額確認書. This is an annual confirmation that your circumstances have not changed. You fill it out and return it by the deadline stated on the form, typically late June.

**If you do not return it, payments are suspended.** They are not automatically cancelled, but you will not receive the August payment or beyond until the form is processed. In most cases you can restore payments by submitting late, but the suspended months may not be retroactively paid. Do not ignore this envelope.

> **Reality Check:** The genkyou todoke is usually sent to your registered address. If you moved and did not update your juminhyo, you will not receive it. You will also not receive your payment. Updating your address at city hall is not optional and the 14-day reporting rule applies. See the full explanation in the [missing 14-day address rule guide](https://mykyoten.com/guides/microguide-15-missing-14-day-address-rule).

### When Your Situation Changes Mid-Year

Report these events to city hall **within 15 days**:

- Job change (your employer certificate on file becomes outdated) - Income change above a certain threshold (less critical post-2024 reform, but municipalities still track this) - Child leaves Japan for more than 60 consecutive days - You leave Japan and deregister - Your visa status changes

Switching jobs matters because your employer certificate was issued by your previous company. Your new employer must issue a fresh one. If you need help reading your payslip to confirm social insurance enrollment status at the new job, use the [Payslip Decoder](https://mykyoten.com/tools/payslip-decoder) to verify what is actually being deducted.

This is a two-step process and most families get it wrong. You must:

1. **File a withdrawal notice** (受給事由消滅届) at your old municipality when you deregister 2. **File a new application** at your new municipality within 15 days of registering your new address

The catch is that the allowance does not automatically transfer. It stops at the old municipality and must be freshly applied for at the new one. The 15-day window starts from the date you register at your new city hall, not the date you physically moved.

Your next step: Go to your city hall's 子育て支援課 counter this week with your zairyu card, your child's juminhyo, and your health insurance card. Ask for the 児童手当申請書. Fill it out on the spot. If you have already missed the 15-day window after a birth or move, apply immediately anyway. You cannot recover lost months, but you stop the bleeding from today.

**Can I apply if my child was born overseas and has not yet arrived in Japan?** No. The child must be registered in your juminhyo in Japan before you can apply. Once the child arrives and you update your resident registration, the 15-day application window opens. Apply within those 15 days from the juminhyo registration date, not the child's birth date.

**What if I am self-employed rather than salaried?** The employer certificate requirement does not apply to self-employed applicants. You will instead need your most recent tax return (kakutei shinkoku) or a tax certificate from your municipal tax office. The rest of the document list is the same. Processing may take slightly longer.

**Does receiving 児童手当 affect my permanent residency application?** Receiving jidou teate is a legal entitlement and does not negatively affect your PR application. In fact, being properly registered and receiving benefits on record demonstrates you are living correctly under the system. Review what actually affects PR eligibility at the [permanent residency guide](https://mykyoten.com/guides/permanent-residency-japan).
