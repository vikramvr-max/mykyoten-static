Leaving Japan without settling your tax obligations can result in the Japanese government pursuing you internationally for unpaid taxes, and may block future visa applications if you ever want to return.

Most foreigners pack their bags, cancel their lease, and assume that leaving is the end of the story. It is not. Japan's tax system has a long memory, and the paperwork you skip on the way out will find you eventually.

Who Actually Needs to Appoint a Tax Agent Before Leaving?

Here is the part nobody tells you: the legal requirement to appoint a **zeimu dairi-nin** (tax representative) depends entirely on whether you have outstanding or anticipated tax obligations after your departure date.

**Salaised employees** whose employer handles year-end adjustment (nenmatsu chosei) and who have no side income, no real estate, and no deductions to claim may not need to file a final return at all. If your employer completes nenmatsu chosei before you leave and there is nothing else outstanding, you are technically clear. This matters: if your departure happens mid-year and your employer cannot complete adjustment before you go, you must file.

**Freelancers and self-employed workers** must file a final tax return (kakutei shinkoku) regardless. No exceptions. If you earned income in Japan under a business owner or freelance structure during any part of the departure year, you are required to either file before you leave or appoint a tax representative who will file on your behalf after departure.

**Business owners** with registered entities face the most complex situation. Corporate tax obligations, consumption tax filings, and personal income tax can all remain open simultaneously. These individuals almost always need a tax representative and, in many cases, a certified tax accountant (zeirishi).

The legal basis is Article 117 of the Income Tax Act. If you leave Japan while tax obligations remain open or anticipated, failure to appoint a representative is a violation, not just an inconvenience.

How to Register a Tax Representative Before You Leave

The zeimu dairi-nin can be anyone: a spouse, a friend still living in Japan, a professional accountant. What matters is that they have a Japanese address and are willing to accept legal responsibility for communications from the tax office on your behalf.

**Step 1:** Choose your representative and confirm they agree in writing.

**Step 2:** Fill out the "Notification of Tax Representative" form (zeimu dairi-nin no todokede). This is available at your local tax office (zeimusho) or downloadable from the NTA (National Tax Agency) website.

**Step 3:** Submit the form to your local tax office before your departure date. Bring your residence card, My Number documentation, and a copy of your representative's ID.

**Step 4:** Your representative receives all future notices, refund correspondence, and any audit inquiries at their Japanese address.

The catch is that your representative can receive and file on your behalf, but they cannot make financial decisions without your explicit authorization. Refunds, for example, go to whatever Japanese bank account you designate. Your representative cannot redirect funds without a separate power of attorney.

**If you are expecting a refund:** Arrange to keep your Japanese bank account open long enough to receive it, or formally designate an account your representative can monitor. Banks in Japan will often close accounts of non-residents after 1-2 years based on reports from foreign residents, so timing matters.

> **Warning Box:** If you leave without appointing a representative and the tax office cannot reach you, they will send notices to your last registered Japanese address. When those go unanswered, the account moves to collections. Collection agencies in Japan can and do contact foreign embassies and coordinate with immigration systems for significant unpaid balances.

Getting Your Tax Payment Certificate (Nouzei Shomeisho)

The **nouzei shomeisho** is issued by your municipal office (shiyakusho or kuyakusho), not the national tax office. This document proves you have paid your resident tax (juuminzei) and is sometimes required by employers, landlords, or as part of departure documentation.

**Required documents at the counter:** - Residence card or passport - My Number card or notification slip - Application form (provided at the counter) - ¥400 per certificate (cash, paid at the counter)

Processing is usually same-day if you go in person. Postal applications take 5-10 business days. Online applications, where available, vary by municipality.

**Resident tax is assessed on the prior year's income and billed in the current year.** This is the infamous second-year shock that catches many foreigners completely off guard. If you arrived in Japan in 2024 and leave in mid-2026, you may owe 2026 resident tax based on your 2025 income, even though you are no longer living there. That bill does not disappear when you board the plane. Read more about this timing trap in the [resident tax second year shock guide](https://mykyoten.com/guides/microguide-20-resident-tax-second-year-shock).

Filing a Final Tax Return Before You Leave

If you are required to file, you do not wait until the standard March 15 deadline. You file **before your departure date**. This is called a **pre-departure filing** (shinkoku mae no kokoku), and the tax office is legally obligated to accept it.

Japan taxes **tax residents** on worldwide income. If you held a long-term visa (Engineer, Humanities, Instructor, Business Manager, etc.) and were registered at a Japanese address, you are a tax resident. That means overseas rental income, foreign dividends, freelance income paid to a foreign account: all of it is reportable.

**The filing window:** You can submit your final return at any point before departure. Walk into your local tax office (zeimusho), explain you are departing permanently, and request a pre-departure filing packet. Staff at major urban offices generally have English-language support or materials, based on common experience at offices in Tokyo, Osaka, and Nagoya.

A Brazilian English teacher on a Humanities/International Services visa departing in late March 2026 with annual income of ¥3,200,000 and ¥48,000 in medical expense deductions would file using Form B, declare all Japan-sourced income, claim eligible deductions, and potentially receive a refund of ¥15,000-¥40,000 depending on withholding amounts. That refund needs a valid Japanese bank account to land in. If the account is scheduled for closure, coordinate the timing carefully.

If you are unsure what is on your payslips or withholding documentation, use the [Gensen Withholding Slip Decoder](https://mykyoten.com/tools/decode/gensen-withholding-slip) to understand exactly what was withheld before you file.

> **Reality Check:** Many foreigners assume their employer "handled everything." In practice, nenmatsu chosei only covers employment income from that employer. Freelance income, a second job, stock gains, or overseas income are never covered. If any of those apply to you, you have an unfiled obligation. Full stop.

What Happens If You Leave Without Completing Tax Clearance

This is where people mess up most. They assume the Japanese government will just write off a few months of unpaid resident tax. They are wrong.

**Unpaid resident tax (juuminzei):** Municipalities will first send collection notices to your last registered address. When those go unanswered, they pass the account to a tax collection unit. Interest accrues at approximately 8.7% annually on overdue resident tax (NTA published rates, subject to annual revision). Municipalities can and do engage debt collection processes, including wage garnishment against assets held in Japan.

**The immigration link:** Based on immigration reporting compliance requirements, outstanding tax debt (particularly unresolved balances tied to a My Number record) can appear in administrative databases reviewed during visa applications. While Japan does not operate a simple "tax debt = visa denied" automatic system, substantial unpaid tax is documented as a negative compliance factor and has, based on reports from foreign residents and immigration attorneys, contributed to denied re-entry or PR applications.

For anyone who plans to return to Japan, even years later, this matters enormously. Unresolved tax records do not expire quickly. You can check your overall compliance exposure with the [Immigration Risk Checker](https://mykyoten.com/tools/immigration-risk-checker) before you make departure plans.

**Resolving debt from overseas:** It is possible. Contact your former municipal office directly by phone or written correspondence (Japanese preferred). Payment can sometimes be made via international wire to a designated municipal account, though procedures vary. Appointing a representative retroactively (even after departure) to negotiate or pay on your behalf is legally permitted.

**What if your employer never gave you your withholding slip?** This is more common than it should be. Without the gensen choshu hyo, you cannot accurately file. Contact the employer first in writing. If they are unresponsive, the NTA can retrieve withheld amounts on record through your My Number. Do not skip filing just because the paperwork is incomplete.

**What if you already left and realize you missed something?** File or pay as soon as you realize it. Voluntary resolution before formal collection proceedings is always treated more favorably than waiting for the municipality to initiate enforcement.

**Do I need to pay resident tax if I leave Japan in January?** Yes. Resident tax for the prior year is still owed even if you depart early in the billing year. The January 1 rule means whoever is registered at a Japanese address on January 1 owes that year's resident tax on prior-year income. Leaving in January does not erase that liability. Coordinate payment through a tax representative if you cannot settle before departure.

**Can my Japanese spouse be my tax representative?** Yes, a spouse with a valid Japanese address can serve as your zeimu dairi-nin. Submit the formal notification form to your local tax office before departure. Your spouse can receive tax notices and file returns on your behalf, but cannot authorize fund transfers or make legal commitments beyond what the form explicitly covers without additional documentation.

**Will unpaid resident tax actually affect a future visa application?** Honestly, it depends on the amount and the visa category. Small balances may not trigger automatic flags, but significant unpaid tax tied to your My Number record is visible to administrative reviewers. For PR applications especially, tax compliance history is reviewed carefully. Resolving any outstanding balance before applying is strongly advisable.
