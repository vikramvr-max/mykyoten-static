The money comes off the top before income tax is calculated. That is the appeal. But buried inside the rules is a lock-in mechanism that most foreigners discover too late: depending on when you enrolled and how long you hold the account, you may not be able to touch that money until age 75. If you leave Japan before hitting certain thresholds, your exit options narrow significantly. This guide explains exactly how it works, who qualifies, and what the exit really looks like. ---

Who Can Actually Enroll: The Visa and Pension Rules Most Guides Ignore

The baseline eligibility requirement is straightforward: you must be enrolled in Japan's National Pension system (Kokumin Nenkin) or the Employees' Pension Insurance (Kosei Nenkin). You must also be under age 65 at the time of enrollment. That second requirement is where foreigners on certain visa categories get tripped up. **Company employees on work visas** -- Engineer/Specialist in Humanities/International Services, Skilled Labor, Intracompany Transferee, and similar -- are enrolled in Kosei Nenkin automatically through their employer. They qualify for iDeCo. **Self-employed individuals** on Business Manager or other independent-category visas are enrolled in Kokumin Nenkin. They also qualify. **Spouse visa holders** who are not employed and who have voluntarily enrolled in Kokumin Nenkin as Category 1 insured persons qualify. However, if a spouse visa holder is not enrolled in any public pension scheme -- which can happen if they are listed as a dependent under their spouse's Kosei Nenkin -- they fall into Category 3 insured status. Category 3 persons were prohibited from new iDeCo enrollment starting May 2022. If you are on a spouse visa and not working, verify your pension category before assuming you can enroll. See the [pension and social insurance guide](https://mykyoten.com/guides/japanese-pension-social-insurance-foreigners) for how Category 1, 2, and 3 status works in practice. **Student visa holders** are generally not eligible. Students are often exempt from National Pension contributions via a special deferral (Gakusei Tokurei). Being on a deferral plan means you are not actively contributing to the pension system, and iDeCo enrollment requires active participation or enrollment. **What if you are on a Working Holiday visa?** Working Holiday visa holders who are employed and paying into Kosei Nenkin through their employer can enroll. Working Holiday holders paying Kokumin Nenkin independently can also enroll. The visa category itself is not disqualifying. Pension enrollment status is. ---

How to Actually Enroll: The Steps From Start to First Deduction

Enrollment is paper-heavy and slower than most people expect. > **[Step-by-Step Checklist]** > 1. Obtain your My Number (you need this on the application form -- see the [My Number Card guide](https://mykyoten.com/guides/my-number-card-japan)) > 3. Choose a financial institution (called an "unjo kikan" -- a record-keeping institution registered with the National Pension Fund Association) > 4. Download or request the application packet from your chosen institution > 5. Complete the application form in Japanese -- most institutions require a Japanese address, a Japanese bank account, and your My Number > 6. Submit via post (most institutions do not accept walk-in enrollment) > 7. Wait for the National Pension Fund Association to process and issue your iDeCo account number > 8. Return to your financial institution to select your funds and set your contribution amount > 9. First deduction typically occurs two to three months after the application is approved Realistic timeline: **three to five months** from submitting the application to your first actual investment. October enrollment applications often do not process until January or February of the following year due to year-end backlogs. The My Number linkage is mandatory. If your My Number information has any discrepancy with your juminhyo (residence certificate), the application will be returned. This happens more often with foreign residents because name romanization on residence certificates sometimes differs from the name on a bank account or alien registration history. ---

The Tax Savings in Actual Yen: Three Salary Examples

Every yen you contribute to iDeCo is deducted from your taxable income. It is classified as a "small enterprise mutual aid premium deduction" (shoukibo kigyou kyousai tou kakekomikin koujyo), which means the full contribution amount is subtracted before your income tax and resident tax are calculated. Here is how that plays out at three salary levels, assuming a company employee with no other special deductions and a standard income tax rate: **Annual salary 4,000,000 yen (contribution: 23,000 yen/month = 276,000 yen/year)** Income tax rate at this bracket: approximately 10%. Annual income tax saving: approximately 27,600 yen. Resident tax saving (flat 10%): approximately 27,600 yen. Total annual saving: approximately 55,200 yen. **Annual salary 6,000,000 yen (contribution: 23,000 yen/month = 276,000 yen/year)** Income tax rate: approximately 20%. Annual income tax saving: approximately 55,200 yen. Resident tax saving: approximately 27,600 yen. Total annual saving: approximately 82,800 yen. **Annual salary 9,000,000 yen (contribution: 23,000 yen/month = 276,000 yen/year)** Income tax rate: approximately 23%. Annual income tax saving: approximately 63,480 yen. Resident tax saving: approximately 27,600 yen. Total annual saving: approximately 91,080 yen. These reductions appear on your **gensen withholding slip** (gensen choshuhyo) at year-end. The iDeCo deduction is listed in the small enterprise mutual aid section. If you see an unfamiliar line item on your payslip or gensen slip, the [Gensen Withholding Slip Decoder](https://mykyoten.com/tools/decode/gensen-withholding-slip) can identify what each field means. > **[Reality Check]** > The tax saving is real and consistent, but it only benefits you while you are a tax resident in Japan paying Japanese income tax. If your effective income tax rate is already near zero because of deductions elsewhere, iDeCo's tax benefit shrinks accordingly. The resident tax portion (10%) applies regardless of income tax bracket, which is why even moderate earners see meaningful savings. ---

The cap you face depends entirely on your employment situation and whether your employer runs a corporate pension scheme. | Employment Category | Monthly Cap | Annual Cap | |---|---|---| | Self-employed (Category 1 insured) | 68,000 yen | 816,000 yen | | Company employee, no corporate pension | 23,000 yen | 276,000 yen | | Company employee, with defined contribution corporate pension (DC) | 20,000 yen | 240,000 yen | | Company employee, with defined benefit corporate pension (DB) only | 12,000 yen | 144,000 yen | | Company employee, with both DB and DC corporate pension | 12,000 yen | 144,000 yen | | Spouse visa / Category 3 insured (enrolled before May 2022) | 23,000 yen | 276,000 yen | **If you contribute above your applicable cap**, the National Pension Fund Association will reject the excess. It will not simply cap your contribution automatically. You would need to correct it manually, which involves paperwork back through your financial institution. Ask your HR department specifically whether your company runs a DB plan, DC plan, both, or neither. Many foreign employees assume they have no corporate pension when they actually do -- and contribute at the wrong cap as a result. ---

What Happens to Your iDeCo Money When You Leave Japan

This is the section that matters most for most foreign residents, and it is where the system has serious friction. **The basic rule:** iDeCo funds cannot be withdrawn as a lump sum before age 60. That is the floor. But the actual accessible age depends on how long you have been contributing. | Total contribution period | Earliest withdrawal age | |---|---| | 10 years or more | Age 60 | | 8 years to under 10 years | Age 61 | | 6 years to under 8 years | Age 62 | | 4 years to under 6 years | Age 63 | | 2 years to under 4 years | Age 64 | | Under 2 years | Age 65 | | Under 1 month | Age 75 (effectively inaccessible) | **The age 75 scenario is not hypothetical.** If you enroll but fail to make even one valid contribution -- due to a bank account issue, a rejection during setup, or a processing error -- and your account technically exists for years without qualifying contribution periods accumulating, you can end up with an account that cannot be accessed until age 75. This has been reported by foreign residents who enrolled, had early payment processing failures, and did not notice for months. Take a concrete example: **A Brazilian software engineer on an Engineer visa** enrolls in iDeCo at age 32 with a company that has no corporate pension. She contributes 23,000 yen per month for 14 months, then accepts a position at a company in Germany and leaves Japan. She has accumulated 14 months of contributions -- under two years. Her earliest withdrawal age is 64. She cannot access the funds before then regardless of where she lives. Her options at that point: 1. **Leave the account open.** She can maintain the iDeCo account from overseas as a "preserved" account (unyo shirasete). She stops contributing but the funds remain invested. She collects at age 64 or later. 2. **Transfer to a Japanese defined contribution pension if eligible.** Generally only applicable if she joins a company with a matching DC plan, which requires working in Japan again. 3. **Request a special lump-sum payment if she qualifies.** There is a narrow exception: if your total iDeCo account balance is 25,000 yen or below, a special small-balance cash-out is permitted regardless of age. This is almost never applicable after real contribution periods. > **[Warning Box]** > Leaving Japan does not unlock your iDeCo account. Leaving Japan does not trigger any early withdrawal right. The lock-in is based purely on contribution period and age. There is no "expat exit" clause. If you leave before your contribution period reaches two years, your funds are locked until age 65 at minimum. **The interaction with the pension lump-sum withdrawal (Dattai Icchi-kin):** When most foreigners leave Japan, they claim a refund of their national pension contributions through the Lump-Sum Withdrawal Payment system. This is a separate payment from Kokumin Nenkin or Kosei Nenkin -- it is not iDeCo. Do not confuse them. Your iDeCo balance is independent of the Lump-Sum Withdrawal Payment. Claiming that pension refund does not affect or release your iDeCo balance in any way. For a full breakdown of how the pension lump-sum refund works and how it is taxed, see the [pension refund tax guide](https://mykyoten.com/guides/pension-refund-tax-20-percent-japan). **What if you lose your Japanese bank account after leaving?** iDeCo institutions require a Japanese bank account for contribution collection and eventually for payout. If you close your Japanese bank account after leaving, you need to notify your iDeCo institution and arrange an alternative. Some institutions will allow a foreign bank transfer at withdrawal. Others require you to maintain a Japanese bank account. This varies by institution and should be confirmed before you close any accounts. Your next step: Before enrolling, calculate exactly how many years you realistically expect to remain in Japan. If that number is under two years, map out the specific age at which your funds would become accessible using the contribution-period table above, and factor that into your decision about whether to enroll at all. If you are already enrolled and approaching a departure, check your contribution period total with your financial institution now -- not after you have handed in your resignation. ---

**Can I enroll in iDeCo if I only have one year left on my visa?** Yes, visa duration does not determine iDeCo eligibility. Pension enrollment status does. However, if you contribute for under two years before leaving Japan, your funds will be inaccessible until age 65 at minimum. Short remaining time in Japan is a strong practical reason to evaluate iDeCo carefully before enrolling. **Do I need to file a Japanese tax return to get the iDeCo deduction?** If you are a company employee and your employer handles year-end adjustment (nenmatsu chosei), the iDeCo deduction is processed through that adjustment using your contribution certificate. You generally do not need to file a separate return. Self-employed persons filing their own kakutei shinkoku claim it directly on that return. **What happens to my iDeCo account if I switch from a company employee to self-employed status in Japan?** Your account continues. You must notify your iDeCo institution and update your contribution category. Your contribution cap will change -- from up to 23,000 yen per month as an employee to up to 68,000 yen per month as self-employed. Failing to update can result in incorrect contribution amounts that require correction. --- If you want to understand exactly what is being deducted from your monthly pay before and after iDeCo kicks in, the [Payslip Decoder](https://mykyoten.com/tools/payslip-decoder) lets you enter your Japanese payslip line by line and identifies what each deduction is.
