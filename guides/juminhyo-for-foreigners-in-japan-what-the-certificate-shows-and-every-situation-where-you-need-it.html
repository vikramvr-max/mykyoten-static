Without a valid juminhyo you cannot open a bank account, apply for a phone contract, or prove your address to any Japanese institution. And if your residence card address is out of date, the certificate you obtain will be rejected on the spot. That is not a hypothetical. It happens constantly, and it derails real applications with real deadlines.

What Exactly Gets Printed on a Foreigner's Juminhyo

The juminhyo (住民票) for a foreign resident is not the same document as the one issued to Japanese nationals. Yours contains additional fields specific to your immigration status.

Here is what appears on the certificate:

**Full name** printed in both romaji and katakana. The romaji matches your residence card exactly. This matters because banks and phone carriers use this to verify identity against your passport.

**Current registered address.** This is the address on file at your ward or city office. Not your actual address. Not where you sleep. The registered one.

**Date of birth and nationality.** Your nationality field will show your country of citizenship as recorded by immigration. If you are a naturalized citizen of another country, confirm which nationality was registered.

**Visa status (在留資格).** This shows your current visa category, for example Engineer/Specialist in Humanities/International Services, or Specified Skilled Worker. Institutions requesting this are checking that your visa permits the activities you are engaging in. A bank seeing an expired visa category will pause the application immediately.

**Residence card number.** This allows the requesting institution to cross-reference your card. Any mismatch between the juminhyo and your physical card is a red flag.

**My Number.** This is the 12-digit individual number assigned under Japan's social security and tax system. It does not appear by default. You must actively request the version that includes it.

Here is the part nobody tells you: the My Number field being printed or omitted is a choice you make at the counter or kiosk. Most institutions do not need your My Number on the juminhyo. Submitting it unnecessarily creates a paper trail and a privacy exposure that cannot be undone once the document leaves your hands.

**Option 1: City hall counter**

Bring your residence card. No appointment needed at most offices. Fill in the request slip at the counter, specify how many copies, and confirm whether you want My Number included (the default at most offices is excluded). Cost: ¥200 to ¥300 per copy. Processing time: same day, typically 10 to 20 minutes if the queue is short.

**Option 2: Convenience store kiosk**

You need your My Number Card (not just the number card notice). Go to a Seven-Eleven, FamilyMart, or Lawson. Use the Loppi or multifunction terminal, select the certificate printing option, insert your card, enter your PIN, and select juminhyo. Cost: ¥200 to ¥300. Available 24 hours, including weekends and holidays. This is genuinely useful. The catch is that the kiosk version cannot always print household-level certificates, and some offices require counter-issued copies for specific procedures. If you are unsure, check your specific municipality's rules. See the [My Number Card guide](https://mykyoten.com/guides/my-number-card-japan) for how to get and activate the card for kiosk use.

Some municipalities accept written requests by post. You send a completed form, a copy of your residence card, and a self-addressed return envelope with a postal money order. Turnaround: typically 5 to 10 business days. Useful only if you cannot physically visit and the convenience store option is unavailable.

> **Warning Box** > If you recently moved and have not yet registered your new address at city hall, the juminhyo you print at the kiosk will show your old address. That certificate will be rejected by banks and carriers doing address verification. Register your new address within 14 days of moving. This is a legal requirement, not a suggestion. This is the complete list of common scenarios requiring a juminhyo for foreign residents:

**Bank account opening.** Almost every bank in Japan requires a juminhyo as proof of address. A residence card alone is usually not accepted as the sole document.

**Mobile phone contract.** Carriers require address verification. Based on common experience reported by foreign residents, a juminhyo issued within the past 3 months is the standard expectation. See the [SIM card ID verification guide](https://mykyoten.com/guides/microguide-11-sim-card-id-verification) for what else carriers check.

**Car purchase and registration.** Vehicle registration requires proof of address linked to your name. A juminhyo is the standard supporting document.

**School enrollment.** Public schools require proof of residence in the catchment area.

**Japanese driving licence application.** The licence centre requires a juminhyo to confirm your registered address in Japan.

**Visa renewal support documents.** While the Immigration Bureau does not always list it as mandatory, many applicants submit a juminhyo to support address consistency. If your address on past tax records differs from your current residence card, a juminhyo bridges the gap. For more on renewal documents, see the [visa renewal vs change of status guide](https://mykyoten.com/guides/visa-renewal-vs-change-status-japan).

The My Number Inclusion Question Nobody Explains Clearly

| Version | Includes My Number | Use Case | |---|---|---| | Standard juminhyo | No | Bank, phone, school, car, licence | | My Number included version | Yes | Tax filing, pension enrollment, specific government applications | | Household juminhyo | Depends on request | Family-based applications, dependent visa support |

Basically, the standard version without My Number is the right choice for 80 to 90 percent of daily use situations. The only time you need the My Number printed is when a government body or employer explicitly requests it for tax or social security registration purposes.

This is where people mess up. They request the My Number version by default because it sounds more official. Then they submit it to a bank or phone carrier. That institution now has your My Number on file without any legal necessity for it. Japan's My Number Act restricts which entities can collect and use this number. Submitting it carelessly does not trigger an immediate penalty for you, but it is poor practice and unnecessary exposure.

What If Your Juminhyo Shows Wrong Information

This happens. Based on reports from foreign residents, the most common errors are: address not updated after a move, nationality field blank or incorrect, and name romanization that does not match the passport.

**Step 1: Get the juminhyo and read every field carefully before submitting it anywhere.**

**Step 2: Identify the specific error.** Is it an address issue (you moved and did not re-register) or a data entry error by the city hall (they input something incorrectly when you registered)?

**Step 3: Go to the Residents' Division (市民課 or 住民登録係) at your ward or city office.** Bring your residence card, passport, and the incorrect juminhyo. If the error is an address you failed to register, you complete a change of address form on the spot. If the error is a city hall input mistake, you request a correction and they issue an amended record.

**Correction timeline:** Same-day for address registration errors if done in person. For data corrections involving nationality or name fields, some municipalities process within 1 to 3 business days, though this can vary.

> **Reality Check** > Here is what nobody prepares you for: if you are in the middle of a bank account application with a 7-day document validity window, a 3-day correction timeline cuts your margin significantly. Submit a new juminhyo request the same day the correction is confirmed, not after. The corrected record and a fresh certificate are two separate steps.

A concrete case: A Filipino nurse on a Specified Skilled Worker (介護) visa moved from Osaka to Saitama in March, registered her new address at city hall on day 12 (within the 14-day window), and then applied for a new phone contract three days later. The carrier's system flagged her because her old SIM was registered to her Osaka address. She obtained a fresh juminhyo showing her Saitama address and resubmitted. The contract was approved on day 17. Total additional cost: ¥300 for the new certificate. Total delay: 4 business days.

**Edge case 1:** Your nationality field is blank because the city hall clerk at initial registration did not complete the field. This is a city hall error. They correct it at no charge. Bring your passport as the source document.

**Edge case 2:** You have a common use name (通称名) registered alongside your official name. If the juminhyo shows the tsushome name but the bank requires your official passport name, clarify with the bank before submitting. Some institutions accept the combined record; others require the official name version only. **Can I use a juminhyo from three months ago for a bank application?**

Most banks require a juminhyo issued within 3 months of the application date. Some specify 1 month for stricter procedures. Check the specific bank's requirements before visiting. An expired certificate wastes everyone's time and delays your application by however long it takes to get a new one.

**Do I need a juminhyo if I have a My Number Card?**

Not automatically interchangeable. Some institutions accept the My Number Card as address proof on its own. Others require the juminhyo specifically. The card proves identity; the juminhyo proves registered address with full status details. When in doubt, bring both.

**What happens if I submit a juminhyo with an old address by mistake?**

The institution will reject the document or flag the discrepancy. If it reaches a formal decision stage with incorrect data, the application may be invalidated. You will need to update your registered address, obtain a new juminhyo, and resubmit from the beginning. No fine is issued for submitting an outdated certificate, but the delay is entirely yours to absorb.
