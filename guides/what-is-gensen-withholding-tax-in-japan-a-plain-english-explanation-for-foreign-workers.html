Japan's tax system does not trust you to save money and pay tax yourself at year-end. Basically, Article 183 of the Income Tax Act requires your employer to calculate an estimated income tax amount each month, deduct it from your salary before the money hits your bank account, and send it directly to the National Tax Agency on your behalf. That deduction is called **gensen chōshū** (源泉徴収), which translates literally to "collecting from the source." This system exists because Japan's government wanted guaranteed, predictable tax revenue without chasing millions of individual workers for payment. It also reduces the risk of non-payment. For employers, it is a legal obligation. Missing it carries penalties on the employer side, not just yours. Look at your payslip right now. There is a line that reads 所得税 or 源泉所得税. That number is your monthly withholding amount. If you have never paid attention to it, you may have been silently overpaying or underpaying income tax every month. How Does Japan Actually Calculate Your Monthly Deduction?

Your employer does not guess. They use the **withholding tax table** (源泉徴収税額表), a government-issued chart published by the NTA. The table has two versions: monthly salary and daily salary. Your gross salary amount is cross-referenced against your **declared number of dependents** to produce a monthly withholding figure. Here is where foreigners diverge from Japanese colleagues. A Japanese employee earning ¥350,000 gross per month with one dependent might have ¥8,420 withheld. A foreign colleague earning the same gross salary but with **zero declared dependents** because their spouse is overseas and not registered in Japan could have ¥12,580 withheld. Same salary. Different deduction. Entirely legal, but it costs you money every month. The catch is, dependents you have not formally declared do not exist in the system. If your spouse lives in Vietnam and you never submitted the dependent declaration form (扶養控除等申告書) to HR, you are paying tax as a single person with no dependents. Every single month. --- > **[Warning Box]** > If you work for a foreign company that has no registered legal entity in Japan, that employer has **no withholding obligation under Japanese law**. You are entirely responsible for calculating and paying your own income tax. Missing this makes you legally liable for underpayment, plus surcharges. This situation is increasingly common with remote-work arrangements and foreign tech employers. ---

Why Does Your Monthly Withholding Almost Never Equal Your Real Tax Bill?

The monthly table is an approximation. It cannot account for mid-year salary changes, bonuses, deductions you claimed late, insurance premiums, or medical costs. The result is that almost every salaried worker in Japan either overpays or underpays by some margin across the year. This is corrected through two mechanisms: **Year-end adjustment (年末調整)** is handled by your employer, typically in November or December. They recalculate your actual annual tax liability based on the real numbers, compare it to what was withheld month by month, and either add the difference to your December salary or take a small additional deduction. Most employees with straightforward situations never file anything themselves because year-end adjustment handles it. **Final tax return (確定申告)** is a self-filed return submitted to your local tax office between February 16 and March 15. This is not optional for everyone. You are legally required to file if you earn over ¥20 million annually, have income from multiple sources, earned over ¥200,000 in side income, or your employer did not perform year-end adjustment. For example: A Brazilian software developer on an Engineer/Specialist visa earns ¥500,000 per month. His employer withheld ¥147,000 in total across January to December. But after applying his life insurance premium deduction and an overseas dependent deduction he forgot to declare mid-year, his actual liability was ¥118,000. He is owed a refund of ¥29,000. He never filed. He never received it. --- > **[Comparison Table]** > > | Situation | Year-End Adjustment | Final Tax Return Required | > |---|---|---| > | Single employer, simple salary | Yes, employer handles | No | > | Multiple employers in one year | Partial only | Yes | > | Freelance or side income over ¥200,000 | No | Yes | > | Foreign employer, no Japan entity | No | Yes | > | Overseas dependents not declared | Limited | Yes, to claim refund | > | Left Japan mid-year | No | Yes, before departure | ---

Where Does This Break Down for Foreigners Specifically?

Three situations cause the most problems, based on common experience reported by foreign residents across Japan. **First: overseas dependents.** If your spouse or parents live abroad and financially depend on you, they can qualify as dependents under Japanese tax law. But you must submit documentation, typically foreign government certificates translated into Japanese, to your employer during the year-end adjustment period. Many foreign workers either do not know this is possible or miss the HR submission window. **Second: tax treaty benefits not applied.** Japan has bilateral tax treaties with over 70 countries. Some treaties reduce or eliminate withholding on specific income types. A US citizen, for example, may have treaty protections that apply to certain scholarship income or pension distributions. If your employer or the paying institution does not apply the treaty correctly, you are over-withheld. You must file a return to claim it back. This does not happen automatically. **Third: working for a non-Japanese employer.** This is where people mess up badly. If you are employed by a company incorporated outside Japan with no permanent establishment here, they are not required by Japanese law to withhold income tax from your pay. You receive your full gross salary, which feels great until you realize you owe a full year of income tax on March 15. Here is the part nobody tells you: the penalty for late payment is a surcharge of 7.3% per annum on unpaid tax, plus a non-filing penalty of 15% to 20% of the tax due depending on circumstances. You have to ask, and you have to know the right questions. The system is not designed to catch foreign-specific situations automatically. ---

Step one: Check your 源泉徴収票 (gensen chōshū-hyo), the withholding slip your employer issues every January for the prior year. This document shows your total annual salary, the total tax withheld, and any deductions applied. Step two: Confirm whether your employer performed year-end adjustment. Ask HR directly. If they did, and your situation is simple, you may not need to file anything. Step three: If you had overseas dependents, side income, multiple employers, a foreign employer, or claimed any treaty benefit, you need to file a final return yourself. The filing window is February 16 to March 15 each year at your local tax office (税務署). You can also file online via e-Tax. Step four: If you missed the March 15 deadline, file immediately. A late voluntary filing is treated more leniently than one filed after the tax office contacts you. The non-filing penalty (無申告加算税) starts at 15% of unpaid tax but rises to 20% for amounts over ¥500,000 in underpayment. Interest surcharges accrue daily. **What if you already left Japan?** You were required to appoint a tax agent (納税管理人) before departure and file a departure return before you left. If you did not, the obligation does not disappear. Five years is the window. Most people do not know this and walk away from money they were legally owed. **Q: My company does year-end adjustment. Do I still need to file a tax return?** If your only income is from one employer, your total annual salary is under ¥20 million, and you have no unclaimed deductions like overseas dependents or medical expenses over ¥100,000, then no. Your employer's year-end adjustment is sufficient. But if any of those conditions do not apply, you must file yourself by March 15. **Q: Can I claim my overseas spouse as a dependent for Japanese income tax purposes?** Yes, in many cases. You need documentation proving the relationship and financial dependency, typically a foreign family register or equivalent, plus remittance records. These must usually be submitted to HR before the year-end adjustment deadline in November or December, or included in your own final return filing. **Q: What happens if my company never issued me a 源泉徴収票?** Your employer is legally required to issue it by January 31 of the following year. If they fail to do so, you can request it in writing. If they still do not provide it, you can report this to the National Tax Agency. You can still file a tax return using salary records, but the missing slip complicates the process significantly. ---
