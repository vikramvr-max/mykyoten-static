Why Does Your Monthly Withholding Almost Never Equal Your Real Tax Bill?

The monthly table is an approximation. It cannot account for mid-year salary changes, bonuses, deductions you claimed late, insurance premiums, or medical costs. The result is that almost every salaried worker in Japan either overpays or underpays by some margin across the year. This is corrected through two mechanisms: **Year-end adjustment (年末調整)** is handled by your employer, typically in November or December. They recalculate your actual annual tax liability based on the real numbers, compare it to what was withheld month by month, and either add the difference to your December salary or take a small additional deduction. Most employees with straightforward situations never file anything themselves because year-end adjustment handles it. **Final tax return (確定申告)** is a self-filed return submitted to your local tax office between February 16 and March 15. This is not optional for everyone. You are legally required to file if you earn over ¥20 million annually, have income from multiple sources, earned over ¥200,000 in side income, or your employer did not perform year-end adjustment. For example: A Brazilian software developer on an Engineer/Specialist visa earns ¥500,000 per month. His employer withheld ¥147,000 in total across January to December. Where Does This Break Down for Foreigners Specifically?

Three situations cause the most problems, based on common experience reported by foreign residents across Japan. **First: overseas dependents.** If your spouse or parents live abroad and financially depend on you, they can qualify as dependents under Japanese tax law. But you must submit documentation, typically foreign government certificates translated into Japanese, to your employer during the year-end adjustment period. Many foreign workers either do not know this is possible or miss the HR submission window. **Second: tax treaty benefits not applied.** Japan has bilateral tax treaties with over 70 countries. Some treaties reduce or eliminate withholding on specific income types. A US citizen, for example, may have treaty protections that apply to certain scholarship income or pension distributions. If your employer or the paying institution does not apply the treaty correctly, you are over-withheld. You must file a return to claim it back. This does not happen automatically. **Third: working for a non-Japanese employer.** This is where people mess up badly. If you are employed by a company incorporated outside Japan with no permanent establishment here, they are not required by Japanese law to withhold income tax from your pay. You receive your full gross salary, which feels great until you realize you owe a full year of income tax on March 15.
