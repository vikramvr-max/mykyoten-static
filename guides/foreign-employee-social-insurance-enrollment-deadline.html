Japan Shakai Hoken: Coverage Starts Day 1, Even If Card Not Arrived

Social insurance coverage in Japan begins on your first day of employment, not when your card arrives. What your employer must do within 5 days and what to do if enrollment is delayed.

> **Last verified: 2026.** This guide is based on Immigration Bureau regulations, Ministry of Health Labour and Welfare guidelines, and standard Japanese administrative practice. It is informational only and does not constitute legal advice. Consult a licensed immigration attorney or official government sources for case-specific guidance.

Missing the 5-day social insurance enrollment deadline can trigger immigration red flags and impact your future permanent residency application. **The consequences are real and they follow you.**

The Japanese government requires your employer to enroll you in Employee Health Insurance (EHI) and Employee Pension Insurance within 5 days of your first day of work. Not 5 business days. **Five calendar days.** This includes weekends and holidays.

Why does Japan enforce this 5-day rule so strictly?

The Ministry of Health Labour and Welfare tracks social insurance enrollment dates to verify legitimate employment relationships. **Immigration uses this data during visa renewals and permanent residency applications.**

Late enrollment signals to both agencies that either your employer doesn't understand their legal obligations, or worse, that your employment arrangement might not be genuine full-time work.

A Canadian software engineer on an Engineer visa learned this the hard way. His startup employer missed the enrollment deadline by 10 days, claiming they were "still setting up HR systems." During his visa renewal 18 months later, immigration questioned the delayed enrollment and requested additional employment verification documents that took 3 months to resolve.

What documents do you need for EHI enrollment?

Your employer handles the actual enrollment process, but you need to provide specific documents immediately. **Don't wait for HR to ask.**

[Step-by-Step Checklist] - [ ] Copy of your residence card (both sides) - [ ] Copy of your passport (photo page and latest entry stamp) - [ ] Employment contract or offer letter with start date - [ ] My Number notification card or My Number card - [ ] Bank account information for salary deposits - [ ] Previous employment certificate (if applicable) - [ ] Dependent information forms for spouse/children (if applicable)

The catch is that some employers don't know they need your My Number for the enrollment process. **Bring it proactively on your first day.** If you don't have a My Number yet, your employer can still start the enrollment process, but they'll need to update it within 14 days of when you receive your My Number notification.

How do employer and employee responsibilities actually work?

**Your employer must submit Form 3 (健康保険・厚生年金保険被保険者資格取得届) to the Japan Pension Service within 5 days.** This is their legal obligation, not yours, but the consequences affect you directly.

Here's what happens in practice: HR completes the form using your documents, submits it to their local Japan Pension Service office or online through e-Gov, and receives confirmation of your enrollment. You should receive your health insurance card within 2-3 weeks.

Your only direct responsibility is providing accurate documents on time. **But smart foreigners follow up.** Ask your HR department for the submission confirmation receipt. If they seem confused about this request, that's a red flag that they might not have submitted it yet.

[Reality Check] Many smaller companies, especially startups and foreign-owned businesses, genuinely don't understand the 5-day rule. They think it's like other HR paperwork that can wait until the next batch processing day. This ignorance doesn't protect you from the consequences.

What if your employer misses the deadline?

**Penalties hit both you and your employer, but differently.**

Your employer faces administrative penalties from the Japan Pension Service ranging from warnings to fines up to ¥300,000 per violation. More seriously, repeated violations can trigger labor inspection investigations.

For you, late enrollment creates [immigration reporting compliance issues](https://mykyoten.com/guides/immigration-reporting-compliance-rules-japan) that can surface during visa renewals or permanent residency applications. Immigration may question whether your employment was genuine from day one.

**The retroactive enrollment still happens.** Your coverage and contribution obligations start from your actual first day of work, regardless of when the paperwork gets submitted. But you'll be without a health insurance card until the late processing completes.

If your employer refuses enrollment entirely, this violates Japanese labor law. Any company with one or more employees must enroll eligible workers in social insurance. **You need to escalate this immediately** to your local Labor Standards Inspection Office (労働基準監督署).

How much will social insurance actually cost you?

Monthly Employee Health Insurance premiums vary by prefecture and salary, but typically run 4.95% to 5.15% of your gross salary. Employee Pension Insurance adds exactly 9.15% of your gross salary. **Your employer pays matching amounts.**

For example, if you earn ¥400,000 per month in Tokyo: - Health Insurance: ¥19,800 per month (¥400,000 × 4.95%) - Pension Insurance: ¥36,600 per month (¥400,000 × 9.15%) - **Total deductions: ¥56,400 per month**

These amounts get deducted from your salary starting with your first paycheck that includes a full month of work. Pro-rated deductions apply if you start mid-month. [Warning Box] **What if you're already enrolled in National Health Insurance?** You cannot have both. Your employer's enrollment in Employee Health Insurance automatically terminates your National Health Insurance. But there's a timing gap where you might receive bills for both systems. Keep all documentation and contact the National Health Insurance office in your city to confirm the termination date.

What happens if you discover enrollment problems later?

Honestly, this happens more than it should. You might discover months later that your employer never enrolled you, or enrolled you incorrectly with wrong visa status or dependent information.

**Immediate damage control:** Contact your employer's HR department and request they submit a correction or late enrollment form immediately. Document this request in writing (email works). If they refuse or delay, you have grounds to contact the Labor Standards Inspection Office.

**Immigration implications:** Late discovery of enrollment problems can complicate [permanent residency applications](https://mykyoten.com/guides/permanent-residency-japan). Immigration expects consistent social insurance enrollment throughout your employment history. Gaps or corrections may require additional explanation documentation.

**Financial implications:** You remain liable for retroactive contributions even if your employer failed to enroll you properly. The Japan Pension Service can demand back payments covering the entire period you should have been enrolled.

**What if I start work on a Friday and the 5-day deadline falls on a weekend?** The deadline still applies. Your employer can submit the enrollment form online through e-Gov on weekends, or submit it the next business day but mark the acquisition date as within the 5-day window. Most employers submit online to avoid weekend deadline issues.

**Can I enroll in Employee Health Insurance if I work part-time?** Yes, if you meet the eligibility thresholds: working at least 20 hours per week, earning at least ¥88,000 per month, and employed at a company with 51+ employees. Companies with 50 or fewer employees may voluntarily enroll part-time workers but aren't required to.

**What if my employer claims they don't need to enroll foreign employees?** This is completely wrong. Social insurance enrollment requirements apply to all employees regardless of nationality or visa status. Any employer making this claim either doesn't understand Japanese labor law or is trying to avoid their legal obligations.
