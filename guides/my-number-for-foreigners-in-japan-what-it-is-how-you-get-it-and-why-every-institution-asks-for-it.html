So What Actually Is My Number, and How Do You Get It?

My Number (マイナンバー) is a 12-digit individual number assigned by the Japanese government to every person registered in the resident registry, including foreign nationals with mid-to-long-term residency status.

The trigger is simple. You complete your 転入届 (residence registration) at city hall after arriving in Japan. The system generates your number automatically. Approximately 2 to 3 weeks later, a sealed envelope arrives at the address you registered. Inside is your 通知カード (notification card), a plain paper or thin card document printed with your name, address, date of birth, and your 12-digit number.

**The number itself never changes.** Even if you move, renew your visa, change your name, or switch visa categories, your My Number stays the same for life.

Here is the part nobody tells you: the envelope is sent by registered mail requiring a signature, and if you are not home when delivery is attempted, it goes back to city hall. If the card is returned undelivered, you need to visit city hall in person with your residence card and request reissuance. Some offices hold returned cards for 3 months before treating them as abandoned. Do not assume the post office will hold it indefinitely.

Notification Card vs. My Number Card: They Are Not the Same Thing

This is where people mess up constantly.

The **通知カード (notification card)** is a paper document. It proves your number exists, but it cannot be used as photo ID. As of 2020, notification cards are no longer issued for new registrations and are technically no longer valid as proof of My Number for most official purposes under updated administrative guidelines. Many employers and financial institutions still accept it combined with a separate photo ID, but this varies.

The **マイナンバーカード (My Number card)** is a physical IC chip card with your photo. It functions as official photo ID, proof of your My Number, and increasingly as your health insurance certificate. It is the one card that does everything.

How to Actually Apply for the My Number Card

**Step 1:** Locate your 通知カード. On the back of the card (or the notification letter), find the QR code.

**Step 2:** Use a smartphone to scan the QR code. This takes you to the online application portal (マイナンバーカード総合サイト). Create an account using your email, then upload a photo of your face. The photo must be plain white background, no glasses, taken within 6 months.

**Step 3:** Submit the application. You will receive a confirmation email. Processing takes approximately 4 to 6 weeks currently, based on reports from foreign residents in major cities.

**Step 4:** City hall sends you a postcard notification when your card is ready. You must bring this postcard, your notification card, your residence card, and any other requested ID to pick up the card in person. Some city halls now require a prior appointment. What Happens When You Move or Change Visa Status

Moving address is the step where foreign residents routinely let My Number administration fall apart.

When you move, you have 14 days to register your new address via 転入届 at city hall. That same visit is when you update your My Number card address. The address on the card is printed on a sticker that city hall staff attach to the back. Do not skip this.

**If you do not update within 14 days,** your My Number card becomes technically invalid as an ID document. Institutions that scan the IC chip will detect an address mismatch and may refuse to accept it. More critically, if your pension or tax records are still linked to the old address, correspondence and notices go to the wrong place. See the [address change reporting guide](https://mykyoten.com/guides/address-change-reporting) and the [missing 14-day address rule guide](https://mykyoten.com/guides/microguide-15-missing-14-day-address-rule) for exactly what the consequences look like in practice.

**Changing visa status does not change your My Number.** A Brazilian data analyst who changes from Engineer/Specialist in Humanities to Highly Skilled Professional visa keeps the same 12-digit number. However, you should confirm your resident registration was updated correctly after the status change, because errors at this step have been known to create duplicate or suspended records in local government systems, based on common experience reported by foreign residents.

**What if you leave Japan temporarily for more than a year?** If you deregister from the resident registry (転出届), your My Number record is suspended. When you return and re-register, the same number is typically reactivated. Do not assume it disappears permanently.

**What if your notification card envelope was delivered to your old address because you moved before it arrived?** Go to city hall in person immediately with your residence card. Explain the situation. They can trace whether the card was returned and reissue documentation from the counter.
