Here's the deal: Tsushome is a system where permanent residents (especially ethnic Koreans and Chinese born in Japan) can register an alternative name at city hall that appears on official documents like the juminhyo (resident record). This was created decades ago to let people with non-Japanese names use Japanese-sounding names in daily life to avoid discrimination.

As of January 2026, tsushome still exists but is increasingly irrelevant because of the Residence Card (在留カード) system. The card only displays your legal name (the one on your passport). There's no field for a tsushome anymore. So even if you register one at city hall, it won't appear on your ID card-it only shows up on your juminhyo if you specifically request the "alias included" version (別名記載).

For most foreigners on work visas, student visas, or spouse visas, you cannot register a tsushome at all. The city hall will reject your request. This option is locked to permanent residence status.

When you sign up for electricity (TEPCO, Kansai Electric, etc.), the online form asks for your name. Just type your nickname. The system doesn't verify it against government databases. The only time it matters is if there's a billing dispute and they ask for ID-then you'd need to explain the mismatch. But honestly? Utility companies in Japan don't care. They just want the bill paid.

Same for package deliveries. Your Amazon account can be "Alex" even if your Residence Card (在留カード) says "Alexander." The delivery driver will yell "Alex-san!" into the intercom and hand you the box. Nobody's checking passports.

The bank's compliance team will flag the mismatch later, freeze your account, and force you to come back with ID to fix it. This wastes hours.

What If My Legal Name Is Impossible to Pronounce in Japanese?

Look, this is common. Names like "Brzęczyszczykiewicz" or "Nguyễn" or "Tchaikovsky" break Japanese clerks' brains. Here's what actually works:

For daily life (coffee orders, restaurant reservations): Use whatever katakana approximation is easiest. If your name is "Siobhan," tell people it's "Shiobān" (シオバーン) or even just "Shio" (シオ). Nobody will check.

For official documents: You're stuck with however your passport romanizes your name. Banks and city hall will input it exactly as written, even if it's 20 letters long and unpronounceable. The computer doesn't care.

The one exception: Some city halls let you add a katakana reading (フリガナ) to your juminhyo that's different from the strict romanization. This doesn't change your legal name-it just helps clerks pronounce it. Ask at the counter: "Furigana wa jiyū ni kakeru desu ka?" (Can I write the furigana freely?). About 60% of wards will say yes.

Reality check: "Japan's name system is designed for 99.9% Japanese citizens who have kanji names, furigana readings, and family registries-foreigners are an afterthought bolted onto a 1950s bureaucracy."

When you get a credit card in Japan, the bank will romanize your name based on how you wrote it in katakana on the application. This creates hilarious mismatches.

Example: Your passport says "Smith," you write スミス (sumisu) on the credit card application, and the card arrives printed as "SUMISU." Now your card name doesn't match your passport, which can cause issues when booking international flights or hotels.

The fix: When filling out credit card applications, check if there's a field for "romaji name" (ローマ字名). If so, write your passport spelling there. If there's only a katakana field, write a note in the margin: "Romaji: Smith" - some banks will honor it.

If you're one of the rare people who can register a tsushome (permanent residents), the city hall will ask if you want it printed on your juminhyo. This costs the same ¥300 as a regular juminhyo, but most places don't accept tsushome documents anyway.

Banks? Nope, they want your legal name. Employers? Nope, they need the name matching your visa. Immigration? Definitely not.

The only time a tsushome juminhyo is useful is for school enrollment (so your kid's classmates don't butcher their foreign name) or community center registrations (so the elderly volunteers can actually read your name tag). For everything else, it's a waste of ¥300.

Immigration will issue a new Residence Card (在留カード) with your new legal name. This takes 2-4 weeks. During that time, you'll have mismatched documents-your old card with the old name, your new passport with the new name. Banks and employers will freak out. Bring the marriage certificate to smooth things over.

Don't try to use your old name for some things and your new name for others. Pick one and update everything. The mismatch will eventually lock you out of online banking or tax filing.
