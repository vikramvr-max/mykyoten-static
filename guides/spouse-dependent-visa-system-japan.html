Spouse & Dependent Visa in Japan: Complete Guide (2026)

These two visa categories sound similar but work very differently. Understanding which one applies to you is the first thing to get right.

A spouse visa is for people legally married to a Japanese national or a permanent resident. It gives you unrestricted work rights. You can work any job, any hours, in any industry, with no permission required. Your visa status itself authorises all activity.

A dependent visa is for family members of someone who holds a working visa in Japan. If your spouse is on an Engineer/Specialist in Humanities visa, you enter as their dependent. Your work rights are not automatic - you need permission to work at all, and even with that permission you are capped at 28 hours per week across all jobs combined.

The distinction matters enormously for day-to-day life, for your PR timeline, and for what happens if your circumstances change.

What Happens If You Divorce or Separate", anchorId:

If you divorce, you must notify immigration within 14 days. Submit a notification of change in marital status to the Immigration Services Agency. Failure to notify is a compliance violation and is factored into any future application evaluations.

Your spouse visa does not expire on the day of divorce. However, once immigration becomes aware - and they will through municipal records even if you do not report it - revocation proceedings can begin. In practice, you have roughly 6 months before revocation becomes likely, but this is not a guaranteed grace period.

Your options after divorce: change to a work visa if you have a qualifying job; apply for a Long-Term Resident visa especially if you have Japanese children; or leave Japan.

If you have a child who is a Japanese national and you have been the primary caregiver, you have a credible case for a Long-Term Resident visa. Immigration weighs the best interests of the child. Clean tax records and stable income significantly strengthen this application.

Start the process early. Do not wait for personal circumstances to settle before engaging immigration.
