Jumin Zei: The Tax That Appears in Your Second Year

If income tax is the deduction you expect, resident tax is the one that blindsides you. Jumin zei does not appear on your payslip during your first year in Japan. Then in June of your second year, a new line item shows up and your take-home pay drops by another ¥20,000-40,000 per month. Here is how it works. When Your Deductions Change During the Year

Your deductions are not static. Several shifts happen at predictable points and knowing when they occur helps you plan. June is when new resident tax amounts kick in. If you earned more last year, your June deduction will be higher. This is the single largest mid-year surprise most employees face. September is when social insurance premiums are typically revised. Your employer reports your average monthly earnings from April through June, and the resulting standard monthly remuneration determines your pension and health insurance premiums for the next twelve months. A spring bonus or high overtime during those three months can bump your premiums. December brings nenmatsu chosei, the year-end tax adjustment. Your employer reconciles your actual income tax liability against what was withheld. For most employees this results in a small refund appearing in the December or January paycheck. January is when new withholding tax tables take effect if the government has adjusted brackets. Rarely dramatic, but worth noting if your January payslip looks slightly different from December.
