Quitting your job in Japan does not cancel your resident tax bill. he moment your employment ends, a payment obligation that was quietly handled by your employer becomes your personal responsibility. The notice that arrives in the mail weeks later is not optional reading.

What Actually Happens to Your Resident Tax the Second You Leave a Company

While you were employed, your company handled 住民税 (resident tax) through a system called **special collection** (特別徴収). Every month, they deducted a portion from your salary and sent it directly to your ward or city office. You probably never thought about it.

When you quit, that system stops. Your municipality switches you to **ordinary collection** (普通徴収). Instead of 12 monthly deductions spread across the year, you now owe the remaining balance directly. The local government has the legal right to demand the entire remaining amount in one lump sum.

Here is the part nobody tells you: this switch happens automatically. You do not need to apply for it, and you do not receive a warning that it is coming. One day a payment notice simply arrives at your registered address.

The Lump-Sum Notice: How Much You Will Owe and When It Arrives

Resident tax is calculated based on your **prior year's income**, not your current income. This is the structural reality that catches most foreigners completely off guard.

The formula is roughly: **(Prior Year Income - Deductions) x 10%** (combining the prefectural rate of 4% and the municipal rate of 6%), plus a flat per-capita levy that typically ranges from ¥3,000 to ¥6,000 depending on your municipality.

**A concrete example.** A Brazilian software engineer on an Engineer/Specialist in Humanities/International Services visa earned ¥5,500,000 in 2024 and resigned in March 2025. After standard deductions of approximately ¥1,560,000, her taxable income is roughly ¥3,940,000. Her resident tax bill is approximately **¥394,000 for the full year**. By March, she had already paid three months through salary deduction - about ¥98,500. The remaining **¥295,500** is now due via ordinary collection, potentially as a single demand.

That notice typically arrives between **June and August**, depending on your municipality and when your employer reported your resignation. The payment deadline printed on the notice is usually **30 days from the issue date**. Missing that deadline triggers late penalties (延滞金) starting at roughly 2.4% annually for the first two months, rising to 8.7% afterward under current rates.

What to Do Within 30 Days of Quitting: The Actual Steps

Most foreigners wait for something to happen. That is the wrong approach.

**Step 1.** Go to your ward or city office's tax division (税務課) within two weeks of your resignation date. Bring your residence card, your resignation notice or a document confirming your last day, and your My Number.

**Step 2.** Ask them to confirm how much resident tax remains unpaid for the current fiscal year and what collection method will apply to you now.

**Step 3.** If the remaining balance is more than you can pay in one shot, ask explicitly about **installment payment** (分割払い). Based on common experience reported by foreign residents, most ward offices will negotiate a split of two to four payments if you request it proactively and in person. This is not guaranteed and is not a legal entitlement, but asking before the notice arrives is far more effective than asking after a deadline has passed.

**Step 4.** Request a **tax payment certificate** (納税証明書) that shows your current payment status. You will need this document later. Do not leave the office without it.

The Deadlines Foreigners Consistently Miss

The **January 1 rule** is the one that produces the most shock. Resident tax liability for a given year is determined by where you are registered on January 1 of that year. If you were living in Japan on January 1, 2025, you owe resident tax for all of 2025 - even if you quit your job in February and left the country in March.

The **June billing cycle** means that even if you resign in May, your annual bill for the previous year's income is just arriving. You might owe a full year's tax that your employer never collected, because you left before the June cycle completed.

**Resigning in December versus June produces meaningfully different outcomes.**

If you resign in June, you have already had six months of withholding deducted. The remaining balance is roughly half the annual bill. If you resign in December, you may have had eleven months withheld and owe very little directly. But if you resign in January or February - right after the new fiscal year begins - you could owe nearly a full year's bill with almost nothing pre-paid.

**What if you leave Japan before the notice arrives?** The tax debt does not disappear. If you have a Japanese bank account, the municipality can pursue collection through it. If you return to Japan on a new visa, unpaid resident tax can surface during immigration checks and complicate your status. Appointing a tax agent (納税管理人) before departure is the legally correct move.

**What if you are on a fixed-term visa that expires while you have unpaid tax?** Unpaid resident tax is not an automatic visa bar, but it is considered during discretionary renewal assessments. Honestly, immigration officers have access to municipal tax records. > **Reality Check** > The tax system and the immigration system are separate bureaucracies - but they share data. Municipalities report unpaid tax to prefectural authorities, and immigration applications require you to submit a tax certificate. If your certificate shows a delinquency, you will need to explain it in writing. For permanent residency, the bar is even higher: the PR assessment requires five years of clean tax payment history as a baseline. What Unpaid Tax Means for Your Visa, PR Application, and Re-Entry

This is where people mess up most badly, and the consequences are not theoretical.

For **visa renewal**, you must submit a tax certificate showing no delinquency. An unpaid balance from a post-resignation lump sum - especially one that went to collections - will appear on that certificate. You can still renew if you pay in full before submitting your application, but you cannot hide it if you do not.

For **permanent residency**, the standard requires that you have paid all resident taxes on time, every year, for the entire qualifying period. One missed payment or one late payment without a formal installment arrangement can result in rejection or a required re-application after further years of clean history. The [permanent residency guide](https://mykyoten.com/guides/permanent-residency-japan) covers this in detail.

For **re-entry**, Japan does not bar you at the airport for unpaid resident tax alone under normal circumstances - but if you have a pending asset seizure order or your address was not updated and the municipality has escalated the case, the paper trail exists and creates friction.

**The one document you must obtain before doing anything else** is the 納税証明書 from your ward office tax division. Get it in person. Confirm that it reflects your most recent payment status. If there is a discrepancy between what you paid and what is recorded, fix it at the counter before you leave.

Your next step: Go to your ward office tax division this week, bring your residence card and My Number, ask for a current tax balance statement and your 納税証明書, and if the balance is large, request a written installment agreement before any notice becomes overdue.

**Can I just pay my resident tax when I renew my visa, or does it need to be paid sooner?** Technically you can pay before submitting your renewal application, but waiting creates risk. If your payment is not yet recorded in the system when the tax office prints your certificate, your application may be delayed or returned. Pay well before your renewal date and allow at least two weeks for the record to update.

**If I leave Japan permanently, do I still owe resident tax?** Yes, if you were registered on January 1 of that year. Before leaving, appoint a 納税管理人 (tax agent) who can receive and pay notices on your behalf. Failing to do this does not cancel the debt, and it can affect any future visa applications if you return to Japan.

**Does my employer notify the city office when I quit?** Yes. Employers are required to notify the municipality and switch you from special to ordinary collection. However, timing varies, which is why your lump-sum notice may arrive weeks after your last day. Do not wait for the notice - contact the ward office yourself to get ahead of it.
