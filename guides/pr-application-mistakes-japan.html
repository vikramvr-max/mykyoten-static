The application fee alone is approximately ¥8,000, but the real cost of rejection is the months of waiting only to receive a denial, followed by more months of waiting before you can reapply.

Many rejections are entirely preventable. By understanding the most common mistakes, you can ensure your application is as strong as possible. Here are the errors we see most frequently - and how to avoid them.

Mistake #1: Late Payments on Taxes or Social Insurance

This is the single most common reason for PR rejection in Japan. Immigration takes a zero-tolerance approach to late payments of:

National Health Insurance (国民健康保険) or employer health insurance

National Pension (国民年金) or employee pension

Critical: The compliance window is typically 24 months (2 years). If you have even one late payment during this period - even by a single day - your application is likely to be rejected.

Set up automatic payments (口座振替) for all taxes and insurance

If you've had late payments, wait until you have a clean 24-month record before applying

Request payment history certificates from your ward office and pension office to verify your record

If you find discrepancies, address them immediately with the relevant office

Mistake #2: Excessive Time Spent Outside Japan

PR requires "continuous residence" in Japan. While occasional travel is fine, immigration scrutinizes applicants who spend extended periods abroad. Red flags include:

Single trips exceeding 3 months

Multiple extended trips in a single year

Total time abroad exceeding 20% of your residence period

Patterns suggesting you live primarily outside Japan

Extended absences from Japan can raise questions about your commitment to living here How to Avoid This Mistake

Keep detailed records of your travel, including entry/exit stamps and flight records

If you must travel for extended periods (e.g., family emergency, work assignment), gather documentation explaining the necessity

Consider how your travel pattern appears from immigration's perspective - they're looking for people who genuinely live in Japan

Mistake #3: Incomplete or Outdated Documentation

Many PR applications are returned or delayed because of documentation issues. Common problems include:

Certificates issued more than 3 months before application

Missing required documents (especially pension payment records)

Photographs that don't meet specifications

Unsigned forms or missing information

Create a checklist of all required documents and verify each one

Obtain dated certificates (tax, residence, pension) as close to your application date as possible

Use official immigration office photo specifications - not just any passport photo

Have someone else review your application package before submission

Mistake #4: Weak Reason Statement

The 理由書 (reason statement) is your opportunity to explain why you're applying for PR and why you should be granted it. Many applicants treat this as a formality and submit generic statements. This is a missed opportunity.

Immigration officers read these statements. A thoughtful, specific reason statement demonstrates:

Your genuine connection to Japan

Your understanding of the commitment PR represents

Your plans for continued contribution to Japanese society

Write specifically about your circumstances - don't use a template

Mention concrete ties: employment, family, property, community involvement

If you have Japanese language ability, consider writing at least part in Japanese

Be honest and sincere, not formulaic

Mistake #5: Underestimating Traffic Violations

Many applicants are surprised to learn that traffic violations can affect PR applications. While minor violations (parking tickets) typically don't cause rejections, immigration considers:

Moving violations (speeding, running red lights)

Patterns of violations suggesting disregard for rules

Serious violations (DUI, reckless driving - these are almost certain rejections)

Drive carefully, especially in the years leading up to your application

If you have violations, be prepared to explain them and show they were isolated incidents

Consider waiting if you have recent violations to demonstrate improved behavior

Some applicants rush to apply as soon as they technically qualify, without ensuring they're truly ready. This leads to:

Insufficient income history at the required level

Weaker overall applications that get rejected

Use the months before you qualify to strengthen your application

Ensure your income has been stable at an adequate level for at least 2-3 years

Verify your compliance history is clean for the full review period

Consider that a strong application submitted 6 months later is better than a weak application submitted immediately

Mistake #7: Not Using Available Resources

Many applicants don't realize that immigration offices offer guidance, and there are legal and professional resources available to help. Mistakes that could be avoided include:

Misunderstanding requirements because you relied on outdated online information

Not taking advantage of pre-application consultations

Trying to handle complex situations (sponsorship issues, income complications) without professional help

Visit your local immigration office for guidance before applying

Consider consulting with a gyoseishoshi (administrative scrivener) who specializes in immigration

Use reliable, up-to-date resources to understand current requirements

PR application in Japan is a marathon, not a sprint. The best applicants start preparing years in advance - maintaining clean payment records, limiting extended travel, building stable employment, and gathering documentation. By avoiding these common mistakes, you significantly increase your chances of approval on your first attempt.
