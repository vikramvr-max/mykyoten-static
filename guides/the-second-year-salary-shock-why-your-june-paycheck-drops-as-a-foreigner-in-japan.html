Why Does Your Paycheck Drop in Year Two and Not Year One?

Japan's resident tax operates on a **one-year lag**. Your income from January to December of your first year is assessed the following year, and deductions begin in June of that assessment year. This is not a quirk or an oversight. It is the structural design of the *jūminzei* system.

Here is what that looks like in practice. You arrive in Japan in 2024. You work and earn income throughout 2024. In early 2025, your employer files your income details with your local municipality. The municipality calculates what you owe, sends a tax determination notice to your employer, and from **June 2025**, your employer starts deducting that annual bill in 12 equal monthly installments through May 2026.

This is why your first year feels financially lighter than it actually is. You are not paying resident tax yet. The bill is being calculated behind the scenes.

The WHY behind this system: municipalities need finalized annual income figures before they can calculate what you owe. Since the Japanese tax year runs January to December and final filing happens in spring, the earliest the system can produce accurate numbers is May or June. So June becomes the universal start date for payroll deductions across the entire country.

Here is the part nobody tells you: **most foreign workers are never warned about this in advance**, not by their employer, not by their recruiter, and not at city hall. Reports from foreign residents consistently describe the June drop as completely unexpected, even among those who did thorough research before arriving.

How Do You Calculate Exactly How Much Your Paycheck Will Drop?

The standard resident tax rate in Japan is **10% of your prior year taxable income** (this breaks down as 6% prefectural and 4% municipal in most areas, though exact splits vary slightly by location). On top of that, there is a flat per-capita levy (*均等割, kintōwari*) that typically adds around ¥5,000 to ¥6,000 per year depending on your municipality.

The calculation runs like this:

1. Take your gross income from the prior year. 2. Subtract the basic deduction (¥480,000 as of 2026 for most earners) and any other applicable deductions (employment income deduction, dependent deductions, etc.). 3. Apply 10% to the resulting taxable income figure. 4. Add the per-capita levy. 5. Divide the total by 12. That is your new monthly deduction starting June.

The employment income deduction alone is substantial and scales with your salary, so your actual taxable base is meaningfully lower than your gross. Do not make the mistake of applying 10% directly to your gross and assuming that is the final number. It will be higher than reality.

> **[Warning Box]** > If you skip accounting for deductions and mentally budget 10% of your gross income as your resident tax hit, you will overestimate your bill. But if you forget about the per-capita levy or assume your municipality applies the exact same rates as Tokyo, you may underestimate slightly. Always confirm the final number on your tax determination notice (住民税決定通知書) or ask HR for the exact monthly amount.
