What Is a Tax Representative and Who Can Actually Do It?

A 納税管理人 (nōzei kanrinin), or tax representative, is a person physically residing in Japan who agrees to act on your behalf for resident tax matters after you leave. They receive notices, make payments, and communicate with the municipality in your name. Basically, this is your legal proxy for tax purposes. The representative does not pay your taxes with their own money. They manage the administrative relationship using funds you arrange to leave with them. **Who qualifies:** Any adult resident of Japan. A friend, a spouse staying behind, a family member, a former colleague, or a licensed tax accountant (税理士). The person must reside in Japan and be contactable by your municipality. There is no official certification required for individuals, but they must sign the notification form and accept legal responsibility for receiving notices. Here is the part nobody tells you: if you choose a friend or family member, you are placing a real administrative burden on them. They will receive official mail. They may need to visit city hall. They need to be trustworthy enough to handle your tax payments without incident. Choose carefully. Professional tax accountants can serve as representatives for a fee, typically ranging from 10,000 to 30,000 yen per year depending on the complexity of the arrangement and the municipality involved. For a single-year wind-down with a straightforward tax bill, a flat fee engagement is usually sufficient. ---
