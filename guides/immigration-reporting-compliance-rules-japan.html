Japan's immigration reporting system serves three control functions that foreign residents often misunderstand.

Status Control Logic: Your visa status comes with specific conditions. Work visa holders must actually work. Spouse visa holders must maintain genuine marriages. Immigration requires active reporting to verify you're living within your status parameters.

Address Control: Immigration maintains real-time knowledge of where foreign residents live. When you move, even within the same city, you have 14 days to report your new address.

Employment Tracking: Work visa holders generate tax revenue and social insurance contributions. Immigration tracks employment changes to ensure visa holders remain productive contributors.

Critical Reality Check: Immigration reporting isn't optional compliance you can delay until convenient. Every missed deadline creates documentation that immigration officers will review during future applications.

Japan's core immigration compliance requirement: report major life changes within 14 days. What constitutes a "major life change" catches many foreign residents off guard.

Job Change Reporting: When you change employers, you have 14 days to report both your departure from the old company and your start at the new company. This applies even if you're staying in the same industry with the same visa type. Learn more about job change reporting requirements and the specific deadlines.

If your new job doesn't match your visa category, simply reporting the change isn't enough. You need to obtain permission for additional activities or work outside the scope of your visa authorization before you start working.

Company Name Change: Your employer changed their corporate name but you're doing the same job? You still need to report within 14 days. Immigration tracks employers by registered company names. Including when your employer changes their company name.

Moving requires two separate reporting processes that foreign residents frequently confuse.

First, report your address change to your municipal office within 14 days. This updates your residence registration. See our city hall paperwork guide to avoid common rejection issues.

Second, update your address with immigration within 14 days using a different form at a different office. Many residents assume the municipal notification automatically updates immigration records. It doesn't. The full address change process involves both city hall and immigration reporting.

✓ Report to municipal office (住民票 update) ✓ Report to immigration office (residence card update) ✓ Update address with employer or school if applicable ✓ Update national health insurance registration ✓ Update national pension registration if self-enrolled

Case Study: Mark moved from Tokyo to Osaka for a job transfer. He reported his address change to Osaka city hall but forgot to update his residence card address with immigration. Three months later, immigration sent renewal documents to his old Tokyo address. Mark missed the renewal deadline and had to pay penalty fees while explaining why his residence card showed outdated information.

Marriage reporting depends on your visa status. If you're on a work visa and marry a Japanese national, you don't need to report the marriage to immigration unless you plan to change your status to spouse visa.

But if you're already on a spouse visa and get divorced, you have 14 days to report the divorce to immigration. Divorce potentially invalidates your basis for spouse status.

Sponsor Change: Student visa holders must report if their sponsoring educational institution changes, merges, or closes. Dependent visa holders must report if their sponsoring family member's status changes significantly.

Your residence card isn't just identification. It's proof of your legal status.

Mandatory Carrying Requirement: Foreign residents must carry their residence card at all times. Not a copy. Not a photo on your phone. The actual card.

Police officers can request to see your residence card during routine interactions. Failing to produce it can result in fines up to 200,000 yen and potential detention.

Replacing Lost Cards: Lost or stolen residence cards must be reported immediately to police and replaced within 14 days at your regional immigration office. The replacement process requires filing a police report, providing photos, and paying a 1,600 yen fee.

Losing your job while on a work visa creates a compliance obligation that many foreign residents don't understand until it's too late.

You have 14 days from your last day of work to report job loss to immigration. Not 14 days from when you find your next job. Fourteen days from employment termination.

How Immigration Evaluates Unemployment: Immigration doesn't immediately revoke work visas when you report job loss. They evaluate whether you're actively seeking appropriate employment. Residents who report job loss promptly and demonstrate active job seeking typically avoid status complications. See what specifically to report when you change or lose a job for the full walkthrough.

Extended unemployment creates status risk that escalates over time:

0-3 months: Normal transition period. Continue job searching and maintain compliance reporting. 3-6 months: Immigration may request interviews to assess your situation and job search progress. 6+ months: Risk of status revocation increases significantly.

Divorce fundamentally changes the basis for spouse visa status.

Immediate Reporting Obligation: Spouse visa holders must report divorce to immigration within 14 days of divorce finalization. The report doesn't automatically cancel your visa, but it triggers immigration review of your continued eligibility. Spouse visa holders should understand dependent visa regulations before any status changes.

Six-Month Action Window: Immigration typically allows six months after divorce reporting for status adjustment. During this period, you should either secure employment and apply for work visa status change, or find other qualifying status that matches your situation.

Reality Check: "I thought my spouse visa was valid for three years, so I had three years to figure things out after divorce. I was wrong. Immigration started status review immediately after I reported the divorce."

Standard Re-entry vs Special Re-entry: Most foreign residents can leave Japan for up to one year and return using the standard re-entry permission that's automatically included with residence cards. No special application required.

If you plan to stay outside Japan for more than one year, you need special re-entry permission applied for in advance. This permission allows up to five years absence for permanent residents.

Absence Duration Limits: More than one year: Immigration may question your residential ties during re-entry. More than two years: Significant risk of residence status complications. More than three years: Strong presumption that you've abandoned Japan residence.

Administrative Penalties: Late reporting results in warnings for first-time violations, potential fines for repeat violations. Non-reporting: Fines up to 200,000 yen. Learn about when immigration can revoke your visa.

Visa Renewal Impact: Single late reporting usually has minimal renewal impact with an otherwise clean record. Multiple missed deadlines may result in shorter renewal periods. Serious non-compliance risks renewal denial.

Permanent Residency Implications: Minor violations may require explanation letters but don't automatically disqualify PR applications. A pattern of violations seriously undermines PR eligibility. Major violations can disqualify PR applications entirely.

The Address Change Assumption: Most foreign residents correctly report address changes to city hall but assume this automatically updates immigration records. It doesn't.

The Employer Reporting Assumption: Many work visa holders believe their employer handles employment reporting with immigration. Most employers don't. Immigration reporting remains your personal responsibility.

The Marriage Status Confusion: Foreign residents on work visas frequently think they need to report marriage to immigration immediately. They usually don't, unless changing to spouse visa status. But spouse visa holders frequently think they don't need to report divorce immediately because their visa is still valid. They're wrong.

Case Study: Sarah lost her job in March but was too stressed to deal with immigration paperwork. She found new employment in June and assumed everything was fine. During her visa renewal in December, immigration discovered the unreported three-month employment gap. Simple job loss reporting in March would have avoided these complications entirely.

Immigration officers evaluating PR applications examine your entire compliance history for patterns that indicate how seriously you take legal obligations.

Your compliance pattern includes immigration reporting, tax filing, pension enrollment, traffic violations, and other interactions with Japanese authorities. Each area reinforces or undermines your overall reliability profile. Understanding resident tax obligations is critical for visa renewals.

Building Positive Compliance Narrative: Strong PR applications demonstrate proactive compliance management. This means consistent early reporting, complete documentation, and proactive communication when situations are unclear.

The Compliance Recovery: If you have past compliance violations, start building a clean record immediately. Two years of perfect compliance can rehabilitate minor past violations, but major violations may require longer recovery periods.

Personal Compliance Folder: Maintain a folder containing your current documents (residence card, employment contract, lease agreement), reporting history (copies of all immigration reports submitted with dates), and contact information for your local immigration office.

Deadline Tracking System: Set calendar alerts for immediate triggers (within 24 hours of major life changes), renewal alerts (3-6 months before visa expiration), and annual compliance reviews.

What changes do you need to report to Japanese immigration?

You must report job changes, address changes, company name changes, marriage or divorce if on a spouse visa, and job loss. Each event has a 14-day reporting deadline from the date the change occurs.

How do you report a job change to immigration in Japan?

Submit a notification of change in organizational affiliation at any regional immigration office or online through the Immigration Services Agency portal at isa.go.jp. You need your residence card and the details of your former and new employer.

What is Article 19-16 of the Immigration Control Act?

Article 19-16 requires mid-to-long-term residents on work visas to report changes in organizational affiliation within 14 days. This covers joining a new employer, leaving an employer, and company name or merger changes.
