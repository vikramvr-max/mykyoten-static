Permanent Residency (PR), known as 永住権 (eijūken) in Japanese, is a residence status that allows you to live and work in Japan indefinitely without the need for visa renewals. Unlike other visa statuses that require periodic renewal (typically every 1, 3, or 5 years), PR holders have no expiration date on their right to reside in Japan.

PR provides the stability to truly put down roots in Japan PR is not citizenship - you remain a citizen of your home country and retain your original passport. However, it provides significant stability and freedom, including the ability to work in any field, change employers freely, and access certain financial products that require long-term residence status.

Japan's Immigration Services Agency (ISA) evaluates PR applications based on several criteria. The core requirements include:

The standard path requires 10 years of continuous residence in Japan, with at least 5 of those years holding a work or residence-based status (not a student visa). However, there are important exceptions:

Spouse of Japanese National: 3 years of marriage and 1 year of continuous residence in Japan

Highly Skilled Professional (70+ points): 3 years of continuous residence

Highly Skilled Professional (80+ points): 1 year of continuous residence

Long-term Resident: 5 years of continuous residence

4. Tax and Social Insurance Compliance

This is where many applicants stumble. Immigration strictly checks that you have:

Paid all resident taxes (住民税) on time

Enrolled in and paid National Health Insurance (国民健康保険) or employer health insurance

Enrolled in and paid National Pension (国民年金) or employee pension

Permanent residency eligibility depends on pension and social insurance compliance .

You should also review your Japan tax responsibilities before applying.

Critical: The compliance window is typically 24 months. Any late payments within this period - even by a single day - can result in rejection or delay.

PR applications are submitted at your local immigration office. The process involves:

Gathering documents: Certificate of employment, tax payment certificates, resident tax certificates, pension payment records, residence card, passport, photographs, and a written statement of reasons for applying.

Submitting the application: This can be done in person or through a legal representative.

Waiting for a decision: Processing times vary significantly - anywhere from 4 months to over 12 months. The average is around 6-8 months.

Receiving the result: If approved, you'll receive a new residence card with "Permanent Resident" status. If denied, you'll receive a written explanation and can reapply after addressing the issues.

What 2026 Changes Mean for Applicants

Recent guidelines from the Immigration Services Agency have placed increased emphasis on:

Japanese language ability: While not officially required, demonstrating Japanese proficiency (through JLPT certification or work history) is increasingly viewed as a positive factor in applications.

Community integration: Evidence of long-term commitment to living in Japan, such as property ownership, children in school, or community involvement, can strengthen your case.

Remote work considerations: For applicants whose employers are overseas, additional documentation may be required to prove stable income and tax compliance in Japan.

Long-term residency requires compliance with immigration reporting rules .

Understanding why applications fail can help you avoid the same mistakes:

Late tax or pension payments within the 24-month window

Gaps in health insurance coverage

Insufficient income relative to dependents

Incomplete or inconsistent documentation

Extended time spent outside Japan (generally, absences exceeding 3 months at a time or totaling more than 20% of your residence period can raise concerns)

If you're considering PR, start preparing well in advance:

Audit your compliance: Check your tax, pension, and health insurance payment history now. Any issues need to be resolved and a clean 24-month record established.

Maintain stable employment: Avoid job-hopping in the year before applying if possible.

Limit extended travel: Stay in Japan as much as possible, especially in the year leading up to your application.

Gather documents early: Some documents (like tax certificates) need to be issued within 3 months of application, so plan accordingly.
