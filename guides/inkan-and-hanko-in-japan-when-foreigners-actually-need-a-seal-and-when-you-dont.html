You do not need to own a seal to live in Japan. You do not need a registered seal to rent an apartment, open a personal bank account, or register your address at city hall. Most foreigners buy one anyway, out of anxiety, because someone online told them they needed it, or because a real estate agent mentioned it without context.

Here is the part nobody tells you: getting this wrong in the other direction is the actual problem. Showing up to a property purchase or a car purchase without a **jitsuin** and its certificate can freeze the transaction entirely. If you are working against a signing deadline, that delay has a direct financial cost.

This guide explains exactly which seal you need, when, and what happens if you show up with the wrong one.

What Are the Three Types of Inkan and Which One Do You Actually Need?

**Jitsuin** is a registered seal. You physically register it at your municipal office, and it gets recorded against your name in the resident registry. When you use it for a high-stakes legal transaction, you also bring an **inkansho** certificate proving the seal on file is yours. This certificate costs ¥300 per copy and is valid for three months for most transaction types. Without the certificate, the seal alone proves nothing.

**Mitomein** is an unregistered personal stamp. It carries no official backing. It is used to acknowledge documents in daily life: workplace attendance sheets, internal office forms, delivery receipts. Most apartment leases, based on common experience reported by foreign residents, accept a mitomein or a handwritten signature with no complaint.

**Signature-only** procedures are increasingly common and cover a wide range of city hall registrations, standard employment contracts at foreign-affiliated companies, and major bank account openings for personal use.

How Does a Foreigner Actually Register a Jitsuin?

Go to the **juminhyo** counter at your local municipal office, not a satellite branch. Bring your residence card and your seal.

The name on your seal must match the name on your resident registry. For most foreigners that means katakana or romaji. Kanji is only relevant if your name is formally registered in kanji characters, which applies primarily to some Chinese and Korean residents. Romaji names carved in full legal name format are accepted for registration, based on standard municipal practice across major cities.

1. Carve or purchase your seal with your name as it appears on your residence card 2. Visit the juminhyo / inkan registration counter at city hall 3. Present your residence card and the seal 4. Complete a one-page registration form 5. Receive confirmation of registration (same day in most municipalities) 6. To obtain an inkansho certificate, return to the same counter or use a convenience store terminal linked to your My Number Card and pay ¥300 per copy

Cost to register: **¥0 to ¥500**, depending on the municipality. Most charge nothing. The inkansho certificate itself is ¥300 per copy every time you need one.

> **Reality Check:** Some foreigners arrive at city hall with a seal carved in a nickname or a shortened name that does not match their residence card. The registration will be rejected. The seal is useless until it matches your official registered name exactly. Do not carve a casual version of your name.

Which Procedures Still Require a Registered Seal in 2024-2025?

The short list of procedures where a jitsuin with inkansho certificate remains the standard requirement:

**Real estate purchases.** The purchase contract and registration paperwork at the judicial scrivener's office require a jitsuin. If you are buying property and miss this step, the notarial process stalls. The seller's agent will not wait indefinitely.

**Vehicle registration.** The transport bureau requires a jitsuin and inkansho for private vehicle registration. Some dealers will handle the paperwork themselves, but they will ask for your seal and certificate.

**Business bank accounts.** Based on common experience from foreign residents operating as sole proprietors or small business owners, major Japanese banks typically require a jitsuin for business account registration. Personal accounts at the same banks often do not.

**Some immigration-adjacent procedures** involving power of attorney or notarized declarations may require a registered seal depending on the specific document type. Confirm with the office requesting the document before you go.

Take a concrete scenario. A Brazilian architect on an Engineer/Specialist in Humanities/International Services visa decides to purchase a condominium in Osaka. The signing date is set. He arrives without his inkansho certificate because he forgot it expires every three months and his copy had expired. The judicial scrivener cannot proceed. He has to leave, travel to city hall, pay ¥300, get a fresh certificate, and return. That cost him four hours and, in this case, a rescheduled signing that delayed the property handover by one week.

Where Are Foreigners Wasting Time Getting a Seal They Don't Need?

This is where people mess up: they over-prepare.

**Standard apartment leases** in Japan, including those handled through major agencies, typically accept either a mitomein or a handwritten signature. You do not need a jitsuin to rent an apartment. If a leasing agent tells you otherwise, ask them to show you the specific clause requiring registration.

**Personal bank accounts** at major banks including Japan Post Bank, MUFG, SMBC, and Mizuho have moved to signature-based account opening for individual foreign residents. The requirement for a stamp at these institutions, based on reports from foreign residents, has been largely phased out for personal accounts.

**City hall registrations** including address changes, resident certificate requests, and My Number Card applications do not require a seal. Your residence card and signature are sufficient. If you are unsure what documents you need for city hall visits, the [documents forget city hall guide](https://mykyoten.com/guides/documents-forget-city-hall) covers this in detail.

**Standard employment onboarding forms** at most Japanese companies, and nearly all foreign-affiliated companies, accept a signature or a mitomein. A jitsuin is not standard for HR paperwork.

> **Warning Box:** Do not assume a jitsuin requirement has been dropped just because a procedure was digitized. Some digitized procedures still require a seal at the physical confirmation stage. Verify with the specific office before assuming you can skip the seal entirely.

**What if you are changing address and need new documents quickly?** You do not need to re-register your seal unless your name changed. The jitsuin registration is linked to your name record, not your address. Address changes require only a residence card and standard notification, not seal re-registration. See the [changing address guide](https://mykyoten.com/guides/changing-address-japan) for the full sequence.

How Do You Actually Buy a Seal and What Should Be on It?

**Mitomein from a 100-yen shop:** Pre-made stamps with common Japanese names are sold for ¥100. These work for most foreigners' daily needs but require your name to match one of the pre-carved options, which is almost never the case for non-Japanese names. Basically useless for most foreign residents.

**Custom carved mitomein:** A small stamp shop or online carver will make a custom mitomein for ¥500 to ¥2,000. Turnaround at a physical shop is often same-day or next-day. Online carvers take two to five business days typically.

**Jitsuin-grade custom seal:** Carved from harder materials (buffalo horn, titanite resin, ebony) for durability. Cost range: ¥3,000 to ¥15,000 or more depending on material and carver. This is the seal you register. It should last years.

**What name to use:** Use your full legal name as it appears on your residence card. Shortening it or carving only a surname is legally acceptable for registration purposes, but your name on the seal must be reproducible from the characters registered. When in doubt, use your full name.

**Storing your jitsuin:** Keep it separate from your inkansho certificate. If someone has both your seal and your certificate simultaneously, they can theoretically authorize transactions in your name. Do not store them in the same wallet or folder.

**If your seal is lost or stolen:** Go to city hall immediately. Deregister the seal at the inkan registration counter that same day. Bring your residence card. The deregistration is free and immediate. Once deregistered, any document signed with that seal after the deregistration date is not valid. The risk window is the period between the theft and your deregistration, which is why speed matters.

> **What if you lost your seal before a scheduled signing?** Contact the counterparty immediately and explain. For property transactions, the judicial scrivener can advise on a short-notice path to registration of a new seal. Carve a replacement, register it at city hall, obtain a fresh inkansho certificate, and reschedule. The whole process can be completed in one to two business days if you move quickly.

**What if your name is extremely long and won't fit on a standard seal?** Carvers accommodate this regularly. A surname-only seal is legal for registration. Confirm with your specific municipality if using only a portion of your name, as practices based on reports from foreign residents suggest most offices accept surname-only registration without issue.

Your next step: identify which specific procedure is triggering your seal question. If it is a property purchase or car registration, start the jitsuin registration process this week. If it is an apartment lease or personal bank account, a mitomein or your signature is almost certainly enough.

**Can I use my seal in romaji letters on a Japanese official document?** Yes, if your name is registered in romaji on your juminhyo resident record and your seal matches exactly. The key requirement is consistency between your seal and your registered name format. Confirm the name format on your residence card and carve accordingly.

**What happens if I use the wrong seal on a high-stakes document?** The document can be invalidated or returned for correction. In a real estate transaction, this can trigger a delay in the legal transfer of ownership. Depending on contract terms, a missed signing deadline could result in penalty clauses. This is not a paperwork formality.

**Do I need to bring my jitsuin to every appointment related to a property purchase?** Yes, typically for the formal contract signing and the registration appointment with the judicial scrivener. Confirm the full list of required appearances with your scrivener in advance. Each appointment requiring the seal also requires a fresh inkansho certificate if the previous one has expired.
