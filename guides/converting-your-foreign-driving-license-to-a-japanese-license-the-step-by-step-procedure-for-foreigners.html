Which Countries Get the Easy Path, and How Do You Know Where You Stand?

Japan divides foreign license holders into two categories. **Equivalent countries** (which include Germany, Switzerland, France, Australia, Canada, and others) go through a simplified conversion with no written knowledge test and no practical driving test. Everyone else, including holders from the United States, China, the Philippines, Brazil, and many others, must pass both a written knowledge test and a practical driving examination.

The official list is maintained by the National Police Agency and is updated periodically. The catch is, the list is not always easy to find in English. Your first step before anything else is to call or visit your **local Driver's License Center (運転免許センター)** and confirm which category your home country falls into. Bring your foreign license. They will tell you on the spot.

Here is the part nobody tells you: even within the "equivalent" category, some prefectures have processed these applications differently based on reports from foreign residents. Always confirm locally. Do not rely on forum posts from three years ago.

Here is the complete checklist:

1. **Your original foreign driving license** (valid, not expired) 2. **Certified Japanese translation** of your foreign license from JAF (Japan Automobile Federation) or a designated translation agency. JAF charges approximately ¥3,000 per translation and turnaround is typically 2 to 4 weeks if done by mail. Walk-in service at JAF offices is faster but not available everywhere. 3. **Your passport** with entry and exit stamps showing presence in Japan and, critically, presence in your home country after obtaining your license 4. **Residence card (在留カード)** 5. **Juminhyo (住民票)**, your official residence certificate from your city hall, issued within the last 3 months. If you are not sure what checkboxes to request on this document, the [juminhyo checkboxes explained guide](https://mykyoten.com/guides/microguide-16-juminhyo-checkboxes-explained) walks through exactly what to tick. 6. **One passport-style photo** (3cm x 2.4cm, white background, taken within the last 6 months) 7. **Application fee**: approximately ¥2,050 to ¥2,550 for the conversion fee plus ¥1,750 for the license issuance fee, totaling roughly ¥3,800 to ¥5,000 depending on prefecture and whether a practical test applies

The Entry Stamp Rule That Catches People Off Guard

Japan requires proof that you were physically present in your home country for **at least 3 months after the date your foreign license was issued**. The logic behind this rule is straightforward: Japan does not want to recognize licenses obtained opportunistically in a foreign country without actual driving experience there.

This is where people mess up. If your passport does not show clear entry and exit stamps from your home country during that 3-month window (because you used an e-gate, because the country does not stamp passports, or because you got a new passport since then), you need substitute documentation.

Acceptable alternatives based on common experience reported by foreign residents include:

- An older passport showing the relevant stamps - A letter from your home country's embassy confirming residency during that period - Flight records or boarding pass copies combined with bank statements showing in-country activity - Foreign residence registration documents covering the relevant period

**Concrete example:** A German engineer on an Engineer/Specialist in Humanities visa arrived in Japan in March 2023. His German license was issued in January 2023. His current passport only shows entry into Japan. He needs to retrieve his previous passport or request a letter from the German embassy confirming he was resident in Germany through at least April 2023. Without this, the application is rejected at the counter.

What Happens at the Driver's License Center, Step by Step

1. **Arrive early.** Most Driver's License Centers open at 8:30 AM. Lines for foreign license conversions form quickly, and if you arrive after 9:00 AM at a busy center like Fuchu (Tokyo) or Osaka's Kadoma, expect to wait 2 to 3 hours before your documents are even reviewed. 2. **Submit your documents at the reception counter** designated for foreign license conversions (外国免許切り替え). Staff will review everything and stamp your application. 3. **Eye test.** Standard visual acuity check. You need at least 0.7 in each eye (corrected is fine). Color perception is also checked for certain license classes. 4. **Written knowledge quiz (for non-equivalent countries).** This is a 10-question multiple choice test in Japanese (some centers provide English). You need 7 out of 10 to pass. Study the official question bank beforehand. It is not hard if you prepare. 5. **Practical driving test (for non-equivalent countries).** This is the part that trips people up. The test is conducted on a closed course, not public roads. Judges assess specific techniques including the **S-curve**, **crank course**, and **starting and stopping precision**. Based on reports from foreign residents, most people fail at least once. Booking a lesson at the center's driving school beforehand costs roughly ¥5,000 to ¥8,000 per session and is genuinely worth it. 6. **Photo taken and license printed.** If you pass everything, your new Japanese license is issued the same day.

Failing is not disqualifying. You can retake it. Each retake costs an additional fee of approximately ¥1,750 to ¥2,200. There is no cap on the number of attempts, but each attempt requires a new appointment, which in busy prefectures means waiting 2 to 4 weeks.

**Edge case scenario 1:** A Filipino nurse on a Specified Skilled Worker visa fails the practical test twice. Her foreign license is set to expire in 6 weeks. She needs to immediately contact her home country's licensing authority for an extension or renewal, because Japan requires your foreign license to be valid at the time of conversion. If it expires before she passes, she may need to restart the translation and document process with the renewed license. This also means she cannot legally drive in Japan during this period at all.

**Edge case scenario 2:** A Canadian software engineer discovers at the counter that Canada is not on the equivalent country list for his specific province (because some provinces have bilateral arrangements and others do not). He came prepared for a document-only conversion and now faces a practical test he did not prepare for. The solution is to reschedule, take a practice lesson at the center's adjacent driving school, and return.

> **Reality Check:** The practical test course layout is the same at most centers across Japan. You are not being tested on real traffic situations. You are being tested on course memorization and precise technique. Foreign residents who treat it like a real driving test fail. Those who treat it like a choreography exercise pass faster.

How Long Is Your Converted License Valid, and What Happens Next

Your converted Japanese license is issued with a **validity period tied to your age and driving record**, not your visa status. Most first-time converted licenses are valid for **3 years** (gold license holders from equivalent countries may receive a 5-year license based on common experience reported by residents, though this varies).

Renewing your Japanese license as a foreigner works the same as for Japanese nationals. You receive a renewal notice by mail to your registered address roughly 2 months before expiry. You attend a renewal seminar (講習) at a Driver's License Center, pay the renewal fee (approximately ¥2,500 to ¥3,300 depending on your record), and your license is reissued. Your **residence card address must be current** for the renewal notice to reach you. If you have moved and not updated your address, you may miss this notice entirely. The [address change reporting guide](https://mykyoten.com/guides/address-change-reporting) explains the reporting obligations that feed into this.

Honestly, missing your license renewal has no direct immigration consequence on its own. But driving on an expired license is a separate traffic violation, and any traffic violation goes into your record, which is reviewed during visa renewal and permanent residency applications. If you are building a case for PR, a clean traffic record is part of the picture. What If Your Foreign License Expires Before You Finish the Conversion

This is a real trap. If your foreign license expires at any point during the conversion process, including while you are waiting for a retake appointment, **the conversion application is voided**. Japan requires a valid foreign license to complete the transfer.

Your options are limited but real:

- Contact your home country's licensing authority immediately and request an extension or renewal by post. Many countries allow this for overseas residents. - If your license cannot be renewed remotely, you will need to travel home to renew it before returning to restart the conversion. This is costly but it is the only path. - Do not attempt to drive while this is in progress. No valid foreign license plus no valid Japanese license means you are driving illegally. Full stop.

**Your next step:** Call your local Driver's License Center this week, confirm which category your home country falls into, and order your JAF translation immediately since the 2 to 4 week processing window is often the longest bottleneck in this entire process.

**Can I drive on my home country license while waiting for my conversion appointment?** Legally, you may drive on a valid International Driving Permit (IDP) for up to 1 year from your date of entry into Japan. After that, or if you are a resident rather than a tourist, the IDP is not valid. If your foreign license is valid but you do not have an IDP, you cannot legally drive in Japan before your conversion is complete.

**Does the converted license show my nationality or visa type?** No. A Japanese driving license shows your name, address, date of birth, and license class. It does not indicate your nationality or visa status. However, your registered address on the license must match your current residence card address, so address changes need to be reflected promptly.

**Does a traffic violation in Japan affect my visa renewal or PR application?** Minor violations (parking tickets paid promptly) are generally not flagged. Serious violations, unpaid fines, or violations resulting in accidents create a record that immigration officers can and do review.
