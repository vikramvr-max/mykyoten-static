What Is Actually Printed on Your Residence Card and What Does It Mean?

Your residence card (在留カード, *zairyu card*) is the primary document the Japanese government uses to verify your legal status. Every field on it carries legal weight. **Status of Residence** (在留資格): This tells you what you are permitted to do in Japan. Engineer/Specialist in Humanities/International Services, Specified Skilled Worker, Spouse of Japanese National, these are not just labels. They define what work you can legally perform, whether you need activity permission, and what renewal conditions apply. If your card says "Engineer" but your job duties have shifted to something outside that category, you may already be in a compliance gap. The [Visa Work Checker](https://mykyoten.com/tools/visa-work-checker) can help you cross-reference your current role against your permitted activities. **Period of Stay** (在留期間): The duration granted at your last entry or renewal. Common values are 1 year, 3 years, or 5 years. This is not the expiry date. It is the duration type. **Date of Expiry** (在留期間の満了日): The actual calendar date your status ends. This is the date that matters operationally. Renewal applications must typically be submitted before this date, and you should aim to apply at least 3 months in advance. **Work Permission Indicators**: On the back of the card, look for the section titled "就労制限の有無" (Employment Restrictions). If it says 就労不可 (no work permitted), working at all is illegal. If it lists specific conditions, those conditions are binding. If it says 在留資格の範囲内で就労活動に制限なし (no restriction within scope of status), you have standard work rights for your visa category. Here is the part nobody tells you: the back of the card is where most people stop reading. The front shows the pretty version. The back contains the enforcement layer. ---

How Do You Actually Use the Immigration Online Status System?

The Immigration Services Agency operates an online inquiry system that lets you check the processing status of pending applications. This is not a general "check your visa" portal. It is specifically for confirming where an in-progress renewal or status change application stands. **What you need:** Your application receipt number (受付番号), printed on the paper receipt handed to you at the immigration counter when you submitted your application. **Step-by-step:** 1. Go to the Immigration Services Agency status inquiry page (search: 出入国在留管理庁 手続状況照会). 2. Select your application type from the dropdown. 3. Enter your receipt number exactly as printed, including any hyphens. 4. Enter the CAPTCHA characters and submit. **Result codes you may see:** - 審査中: Under review. No action needed yet. - 交付準備中: Card is being prepared. You should receive a notice to collect within roughly 2 weeks. - 許可: Approved. Collect your new card at the immigration office. - 不許可: Rejected. You will receive a written notice explaining next steps. The catch is this system only tells you about pending applications. It does not give you a real-time status of your current residence card. For current status, you need the physical card itself plus the city hall records. > **Warning Box** > If you submit a renewal application and then lose your receipt slip, you cannot easily retrieve the application number remotely. Keep a photo of the receipt in cloud storage the moment you receive it. Immigration counters do not have a simple lookup by name. Losing the receipt number means visiting the office in person, bringing your passport and residence card, and requesting confirmation manually. This can cost you half a day and significant stress near an expiry deadline. ---

What Does City Hall Actually Tell You About Your Immigration Status?

Your juminhyo (住民票, resident registration certificate) is issued by your municipal city hall and is a separate record from immigration's database. However, since 2012, the two systems have been linked. Your juminhyo now includes immigration-related fields. When you request your juminhyo, ask specifically for the version that includes 国籍・地域 (nationality/region) and 在留資格 (residence status) fields. Not every clerk will include these by default unless you ask. The document typically costs 300 yen per copy. Fields to check on your juminhyo: - 国籍・地域: Your registered nationality - 在留資格: Should match your residence card exactly - 在留期間: Should match the period type on your card - 在留期限: The expiry date as recorded by city hall **If the juminhyo and your residence card show different information**, that is a flag. It can happen when immigration updates their system after a status change but city hall has not yet synced, or when a clerical error occurred at the point of registration. See the [juminhyo checkboxes guide](https://mykyoten.com/guides/microguide-16-juminhyo-checkboxes-explained) for a full breakdown of what each field on that document means. Requesting a juminhyo requires your residence card and the standard 300 yen fee. Processing is immediate at the counter. You can also use convenience store kiosks (7-Eleven, FamilyMart, Lawson) with a My Number Card, which takes under 5 minutes. ---

When Can Your Status Change Without You Knowing?

This is where people mess up. They assume that because they did not go to immigration themselves, nothing changed. That is wrong. **Employer-sponsored transfers:** If your employer applied for a status change or renewal on your behalf, as is common with large corporations and technical intern programs, your new card details may differ from what you expected. The employer handles the paperwork, you get a new card handed to you, and you never fully review what was actually approved. Months later, you discover your period of stay was shortened, or your permitted activity scope was narrowed. **Marriage to a Japanese national:** Marrying a Japanese citizen does not automatically convert your status to Spouse of Japanese National. You must apply for the change yourself. However, divorce does trigger consequences. If your status was Spouse of Japanese National and you divorce, you are required to report the change to immigration within 14 days. Failure to do so can affect renewal eligibility, and immigration monitors family register (koseki) data. Read the [spouse and dependent visa guide](https://mykyoten.com/guides/spouse-dependent-visa-system-japan) for the full picture on what changes are required and when. **Scenario:** A Brazilian IT professional on an Engineer/Specialist visa gets married to a Japanese national in March 2025. His company simultaneously processes a renewal on his behalf in April 2025. The renewal is approved under the same Engineer category with a 3-year period, which is fine. But he now also has the option to change to Spouse of Japanese National status, which could offer more flexible work permissions. He never reviews the new card carefully and assumes his status updated automatically after the marriage registration. It did not. Two years later, a side job opportunity arises. He checks his card, sees "Engineer" with its activity restrictions, and realizes he needs a separate [side work permission](https://mykyoten.com/guides/side-work-permission) application he was never aware of. > **Reality Check** > Immigration status does not update because of life events. Marriage, divorce, job changes, company mergers: none of these trigger automatic card updates. Every status change requires a formal application. The card in your wallet is always the legal record, regardless of what has changed in your personal life. ---

Act immediately. Do not wait, do not assume it will resolve itself. **Step 1:** Compare your residence card against your juminhyo line by line. Note every field that differs. **Step 2:** If the discrepancy is on the juminhyo only (city hall record), visit your city hall with your residence card and passport. Explain you need to confirm your immigration registration. They can cross-check against the national immigration database. Bring the specific fields you believe are incorrect in writing. **Step 3:** If the discrepancy is on the residence card itself (for example, a wrong expiry date or wrong status category), contact the Immigration Services Agency directly. The main contact line is 0570-013904, available Monday through Friday during business hours. Explain that you believe there is a data entry error on your issued card. **Step 4:** Request a written record of every communication. If you are told an error exists and a correction is underway, ask for a written acknowledgment. This protects you legally while the correction is processed. Immigration officers cannot detain or penalize you for a documented administrative error that you proactively flagged. **Step 5:** If the situation involves a potential overstay or unauthorized activity, do not attempt to self-resolve without legal guidance. Contact a registered administrative scrivener (行政書士) or immigration attorney before taking any further action. Overstay penalties start at fines and can escalate to detention and deportation bans of 1 to 10 years depending on duration and circumstances. Honest reality: corrections at immigration offices can take 2 to 4 weeks. **Can I check my visa status online without a pending application number?** There is no general public portal that lets foreigners look up their current residence card status using only a name or My Number. The online inquiry system only covers in-progress applications. For current status, your residence card and a juminhyo are the authoritative sources. If you have concerns about data accuracy, visiting your city hall in person is the most reliable method. **What happens if my residence card expires while a renewal is pending?** If you submitted your renewal application before the expiry date, you are legally permitted to remain in Japan and continue activities under your previous status for up to 2 months while the application is processed. A stamp or notation on your passport or a separate paper certificate from immigration confirms this. Do not travel internationally during this window without confirming re-entry permissions first. **Does my My Number card show my visa status?** No. Your My Number card does not display visa category, period of stay, or expiry date. It shows your name, address, date of birth, and My Number. Since 2025, My Number Cards can function as health insurance certificates, but they do not replace residence cards for immigration status verification.
