When exactly should I submit my change of status application?

**Submit your application at least 3 months before your graduation date.** Immigration officers need time to verify your job offer, check your university status, and process the paperwork. The official processing time is 2-8 weeks, but delays happen constantly. Your current student visa expires when you graduate, creating a hard deadline that Immigration won't extend just because they're behind on paperwork. Here's the catch: Immigration requires your employer to have all internal approvals finished before you apply. Most companies take 4-6 weeks to prepare the certificate of employment and salary documentation after making you an offer. A Canadian computer science student I know got his job offer in October, submitted his change of status application in December, but graduated in March. His application was still pending when graduation came. **He had to leave Japan and reapply from Canada**, losing 3 months of salary and nearly losing his position entirely. The math is brutal but simple: **job offer + 6 weeks company paperwork + 3 months Immigration processing = start this process 4-5 months before graduation.** **Reality Check** *Immigration doesn't care about your graduation ceremony date. They care about when your university officially ends your enrollment status. Check your academic calendar carefully - some universities end enrollment status weeks before the ceremony.*

What documents do I need from my employer and university?

Your employer must provide a **Certificate of Employment** (在職証明書) showing your exact job title, salary, and start date. This isn't just an offer letter. It's a formal document confirming they will actually employ you with specific terms. **Required employer documents:** - Certificate of Employment with official company seal - Company registration certificate (登記事項証明書) - dated within 3 months - Financial statements showing the company can pay your salary - Detailed job description matching your visa category Your university must provide: - **Academic transcript** showing you're on track to graduate - **Expected graduation certificate** (卒業見込み証明書) - **Attendance record** proving you've been a legitimate student The university documents cost around ¥300-500 each. Get extras because Immigration sometimes requests additional copies during processing. **Warning: Your university's international office closes for weeks during graduation season.** Get your documents in January or February, not March when everyone else is panicking.

What are the fees and what happens if I graduate first?

The application fee is **¥4,000** paid in revenue stamps at Immigration. But here's what nobody tells you: **if your graduation date passes while your application is pending, you enter a legal gray zone.** Technically your student status ends, but your new work status hasn't started. Immigration calls this "transitional status" and you're supposed to wait quietly without working. **You cannot start your job** even if your company is ready. You cannot earn income. You just wait. If the application gets denied after graduation, you have **30 days to leave Japan** from the denial date. No exceptions. No appeals that let you stay. **Step-by-Step Checklist** *□ Calculate graduation date minus 4 months = latest job search deadline* *□ Get job offer with specific start date* *□ Request all university documents immediately* *□ Wait for employer to prepare Certificate of Employment (6 weeks)* *□ Submit application minimum 3 months before graduation* *□ Prepare backup plan if still pending at graduation*

Why do applications get rejected and how do I avoid it?

**Salary mismatches** kill more applications than anything else. Your salary must match typical pay for someone with your education and job type. Immigration has internal salary databases they check against. If you're applying for an Engineer visa but your salary is ¥200,000 per month, that's suspicious. Fresh engineering graduates typically earn ¥220,000-280,000 monthly. [Check if your work matches your visa category](https://mykyoten.com/tools/visa-work-checker) before applying. **Academic performance matters more than people think.** Immigration reviews your attendance record and grades. If you failed courses or had attendance below 80%, expect extra scrutiny. They want to see you were a serious student, not just someone using student status to stay in Japan. **Company legitimacy issues** create problems. Immigration investigates whether your employer is real, financially stable, and actually needs foreign workers. Startup companies or companies with recent financial troubles face harder questions. A Filipino engineering student got rejected because his hiring company had only 3 employees and revenue of ¥15 million annually. Immigration questioned whether they really needed a full-time foreign engineer. His second application succeeded after joining a larger company with clear international business needs. **Common rejection reasons based on Immigration Bureau feedback:** - Salary doesn't match job responsibilities or education level - Company cannot demonstrate legitimate business need for foreign worker - Job duties don't clearly fit the requested visa category - Missing or incomplete financial documentation from employer - Poor academic performance or attendance issues

What are my backup options if the application fails?

**Option 1: Temporary Visitor status conversion.** If your application is denied but you have legitimate reasons to stay (like ongoing job searches), you can sometimes get a 90-day Temporary Visitor status. This doesn't allow work, but keeps you legal while you find alternatives. **Option 2: Leave and reapply from your home country.** This is expensive and time-consuming, but sometimes necessary. Your employer can sponsor a Certificate of Eligibility application while you're abroad. Processing takes 1-3 months from overseas. **Option 3: Find a new sponsor quickly.** If your original employer can't support the application, other companies might. But you need time for this - another reason to start early. **What if scenarios:** - **What if my graduation gets delayed by a semester?** Your change of status application remains valid, but inform Immigration about the status change. This actually helps because it gives more processing time. - **What if my job offer gets withdrawn during application processing?** You must notify Immigration immediately. The application becomes invalid and you need a new sponsor or must prepare to leave Japan. The worst mistake is waiting until the last minute. Your next step: Calculate your exact graduation date, subtract 4 months, and mark that date as your absolute latest deadline to have a solid job offer in hand with a company ready to sponsor your visa change.

**Can I work part-time while my change of status application is pending?** Yes, as long as you're still enrolled as a student and have valid part-time work permission. Once you graduate, all work must stop until the new visa is approved. **What happens to my student loan or scholarship if I change status?** This depends on your loan terms or scholarship agreement. Some require you to return to your home country after graduation. Check your contracts before applying for status change. **Can I apply for change of status if my grades are poor?** Yes, but poor grades increase scrutiny. Immigration wants evidence you were a legitimate student.
