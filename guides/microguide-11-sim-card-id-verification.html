As of January 2026, Japan merged the old residence card and My Number card into the Residence Card (在留カード) (特定在留カード). This is your new all-in-one ID. The old pink health insurance cards are being phased out-your Residence Card (在留カード) now doubles as your health insurance proof if you register it properly.

Here's the deal: Mobile carriers will not activate a SIM without this physical card. Period. No exceptions for "I'm waiting for it in the mail" or "I have a photocopy." The 2016 anti-fraud telecom laws are still in full effect, and shops are terrified of fines. They'll check your card number, your face, and your address. If anything's off by one character, you're walking out empty-handed.

Day 1: You land, clear immigration, get your Residence Card (在留カード) stamped right there at the airport (Narita and Haneda both do this now).

Day 2-3: Go to your local ward office (kuyakusho) with your card, passport, and lease. Register your address. The clerk will give you a slip confirming your address is now "official." This is the magic paper. Without it, no phone shop will touch you.

Day 3-4: Hit a carrier. Big three (Docomo, AU, Softbank) are pricier but faster. Budget carriers (Rakuten Mobile, IIJmio) are cheaper but the staff might not speak English. Bring:

A credit card or cash (some shops still don't take foreign cards for the deposit)

The clerk will photocopy everything, ask you to fill out a form in katakana (practice writing your name beforehand), and activate your SIM. Takes 30-60 minutes. Do not leave without testing the SIM in the store. I've seen people walk out, get home, and realize the APN settings are wrong.

What If I Miss the 14-Day Deadline?

Look, this happens. You get jet-lagged, your landlord is slow with the lease, whatever. If you register your address after 14 days, you're technically subject to a fine (up to ¥200,000, though they rarely enforce it for first-timers). The real penalty? Your My Number notification gets sent to your registered address, and if you're not there to receive it, it goes back to the ward office. Then you have to go back, show ID, and pick it up in person. Adds a week to everything.

[WARNING BOX] If your Residence Card (在留カード) shows your old address (because you moved after getting it), the phone shop will reject you even if you have the registration slip. You need to go back to the ward office and get the back of the card updated with a new address sticker. This mistake costs people an extra day and a lot of frustration.

Without a Japanese number, you're flying blind. Postal tracking exists, but it's clunky and requires a JP Post account (which... also asks for a phone number). Basically, you're in a loop.

Don't pay for a "My Number Card photo" at a convenience store (¥200-300). Use your phone, print it at home or a conbini printer for ¥30, and cut it yourself. The ward office doesn't care if the edges are slightly rough.

Avoid airport SIM cards marketed to tourists. They're data-only (no phone number) and cost 3-4x what a proper contract does. You'll just have to buy another SIM later.

Rakuten Mobile's "free for 3GB" plan is dead (ended 2023), but their cheapest plan is still ¥1,078/month. If you're on a budget, that beats Docomo's ¥3,000+ plans.

Honestly, this is tough. Phone shops want a lease or utility bill in your name. If you're crashing with someone, you have two options:

Get a "letter of residence" (居住証明書) from your friend. They go to the ward office, show their lease, and sign a paper saying you live there. Costs ¥300. Not all wards do this-call ahead.

Use a monthly apartment service (like Leopalace or Oakhouse). They give you a proper contract even for short stays, which satisfies the phone shop.

Don't try to fake it. Shops cross-check your address with the ward office database now.

✅ Passport (original, not a copy)

✅ Lease agreement or hotel registration paper

✅ A pen (forms are still paper-based at most ward offices)

✅ ¥5,000-10,000 cash (some phone shops don't take cards for deposits)
