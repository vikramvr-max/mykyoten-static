City hall and immigration are separate systems, even though it's easy to assume they share information automatically. City hall manages your local residency record — your registered address, family status changes, and certificates like the juminhyo. Immigration manages your visa status. Updating one does not update the other.

This matters most around address changes. When you move, you must follow the address change process — a moving-out notice at your old city hall and a moving-in notice at your new one, both within 14 days. This updates your health insurance and pension enrollment along with your official address. Failing to do this on time means official mail, including anything from immigration or the tax office, goes to the wrong place. If a visa renewal notice never reaches you, that is on you, not on the system.

You hand over paperwork you were sure was complete, and it comes back. Without a clear explanation in a language you fully understand, it can feel completely arbitrary. You followed the checklist you found online, so what went wrong? This experience is common for foreign residents in Japan, and it rarely has anything to do with you personally.

Most rejections happen for straightforward, fixable reasons: a missing document, the wrong form for your specific situation, the wrong counter or department, or filing outside the permitted time window. Maybe you brought a copy of your residence card when they needed the original. Maybe you brought your passport but they also needed a certificate of residence issued within the last three months.

Using an outdated form is another frequent problem — city hall forms get updated, and a form downloaded months ago might no longer be current. Going to the wrong counter is easier than you'd think, since address, marital status, and employment changes are often handled by different departments.

If there's a deadline attached to your application, it is not a suggestion. Birth notifications need to be filed within 14 days. Address changes should be reported within 14 days of moving. Outside that window, the application simply can't be accepted, no matter how complete your documents are.

Language barriers compound this. Major cities may offer multilingual support; smaller towns and rural areas typically don't. Busy counters during lunchtime or late afternoon also limit how much explanation staff have time to give.

Documents People Forget Most Often

Your residence card is required for nearly every city hall procedure, yet people sometimes leave it at home assuming their passport is enough. Both are often required together, especially for family registration or moving procedures.

Notification slips from previous procedures get overlooked often. When you move between cities, you need the moving-out certificate from your previous city hall — without it, the moving-in process becomes much more complicated. People forget these because they filed them away weeks earlier.

If a city hall requires a form filled out in advance, showing up without it means completing it on the spot, which is slower and confusing if it's only in Japanese. If you're registering a marriage, birth, or other family status change involving foreign documents, you'll also need an official Japanese translation — it doesn't need to be done professionally, but it must include the translator's name. Many people bring the original but forget the translation entirely.

Health insurance cards, pension books, or My Number cards may also be needed depending on the procedure, particularly if you're moving out of Japan or changing insurance status.

Physical Documents Still Matter

Japan's administrative system still heavily relies on physical paperwork. Even though many records exist digitally, city hall procedures usually require original physical documents — a photo of your residence card on your phone won't work, and a photocopy of your passport usually won't either. This can feel outdated if you're coming from a country where government services moved online, but the physical verification step remains crucial here.

Treat rejection as information rather than failure. When paperwork gets sent back, something specific needs to be adjusted, not something you did wrong personally.

Before your visit, get a complete checklist of required documents — the arrival checklist covers what you need. Look up requirements on the city hall's own website first; many have English-language guides. Print the checklist and bring it with you.

Over-prepare rather than under-prepare — bring both your residence card and passport even if only one is mentioned, and bring your My Number card if you have one. Go during quieter hours if possible; mid-morning on a weekday is usually calmer than lunch hour.

If your application is rejected, ask for clarification before you leave. Even with a language barrier, ask staff to write down what's missing or point to it on a checklist. Return prepared once you know what was needed, and keep copies of everything you submit for future visits.

City hall rejections are frustrating but rarely dangerous on their own. The real risk is repeatedly failing to complete a required procedure — especially address updates, since that can mean missing an immigration or tax notice entirely.
