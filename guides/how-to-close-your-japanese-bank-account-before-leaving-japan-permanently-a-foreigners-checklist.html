Leaving Japan without closing your bank account is one of the most expensive administrative mistakes foreigners make. Dormant account fees can drain your remaining balance over months or years. Recovering that money from overseas is genuinely difficult, and in some cases, practically impossible without a proxy in Japan. This is not a hypothetical risk. Based on common experience reported by foreign residents, it happens regularly. Here is what you actually need to do, in the right order, before you leave. ---

When Should You Actually Start This Process?

The ideal window is **30 to 60 days before your departure date.** That is not an arbitrary number. It takes time to cancel linked automatic payments, wait for pending transactions to clear, transfer funds overseas, and physically visit a branch. Trying to compress all of this into the final week is where people mess up. Japan Post Bank (ゆうちょ銀行) requires you to visit a branch in person for account closure, and branch staff frequently ask for documents that confirm your registered address is still active. If your residence card status has already changed to "departed" in city hall records, some staff will flag this and request additional documentation. **Go before you deregister your address.** MUFG (三菱UFJ銀行) follows a similar in-branch requirement. Based on common experience, attempting to close an MUFG account in the final few days before departure, when your residence card is expiring, can result in the branch requesting additional verification or delaying the closure. Shinsei Bank and Sony Bank have historically allowed account closure by post or online portal, but policies change. Check directly with the bank at least 45 days out. If you wait until the last week, you face a genuine problem: banks will ask for a valid, unexpired residence card. If yours expires before departure, or you have already submitted your move-out (転出届) at city hall and received a move-out certificate instead, the branch may refuse the closure request that day. > **Use the [Leaving Japan Calculator](https://mykyoten.com/tools/leaving-japan-calculator)** to map out your departure timeline and see what administrative tasks need to happen in which order. ---

What Documents Do You Actually Need to Bring?

> ### Warning Box > **Do not assume your passport alone is sufficient.** For account closure as a foreigner, Japanese banks almost universally require the combination of documents that were used when the account was opened. The standard document set for in-branch closure: - **Residence card (在留カード)**: must be valid on the day you visit - **Passport**: must match the name registered on the account - **Bankbook (通帳)**: if issued; some online-only accounts have no physical bankbook - **Cash card (キャッシュカード)** - **Registered hanko (印鑑)** or signature card, depending on what you registered at account opening - **Bank-issued seal certificate or account information sheet**, if requested Here is the part nobody tells you: if your residence card has already been cancelled at the airport exit stamp procedure (when immigration clips or holes your card on departure), **you cannot use that card for in-branch closure.** This is a one-way door. Once you leave through immigration and your card is cancelled, in-branch closure in Japan is no longer possible that day. This is why the sequence matters absolutely. **What if you have already left and your card is cancelled?** A small number of banks allow closure by international post with certified document copies. Typically this requires a notarized copy of your passport, a completed closure form obtained from the bank's overseas inquiry desk, and a designated overseas bank account for fund transfer. Japan Post Bank does not currently offer this service in a standardized way. Expect this process to take 4 to 12 weeks if the bank permits it at all. ---

How Do You Actually Close the Account, Step by Step?

### In-Branch Closure (Recommended) 1. Visit your bank branch during business hours (typically 9:00 to 15:00 weekdays). 2. Take a numbered ticket at the counter and indicate you want to close your account (口座解約, kouza kaiyaku). 3. Present all documents listed above. 4. The teller will verify your identity, confirm your registered information, and process a final balance withdrawal. 5. You receive your remaining funds in cash or by transfer to another account. 6. The teller returns your bankbook with a cancellation stamp, or shreds your card on-site. 7. Get a written confirmation of closure if available. Not all branches issue this automatically. Ask for it (解約証明書). The entire process typically takes 30 to 60 minutes. **If you have a large balance, some branches require advance notice for cash withdrawal above ¥1,000,000.** Call ahead. > ### Step-by-Step Checklist > **Before your branch visit:** > - [ ] Cancel all automatic payments (口座振替) linked to the account > - [ ] Cancel salary direct deposit with your employer's payroll department > - [ ] Redirect any pension refund or resident tax refund recipients (see below) > - [ ] Transfer funds to your overseas destination account > - [ ] Confirm your residence card is still valid > - [ ] Bring bankbook, cash card, residence card, passport, and hanko ### Overseas Closure by Post Only attempt this if you have already left Japan and have no other option. Contact the bank's international inquiry line before sending anything. Required forms are bank-specific and are not publicly downloadable in most cases. Shinsei Bank has historically had English-language support for this process, but verify current policy directly. ---

What Is the Cheapest Way to Move Your Money Out First?

Transfer your remaining funds **before** closing the account, not after. Banks will give you remaining funds in cash at closure, which creates the problem of physically carrying large amounts or opening a temporary account, neither of which is practical. **Wise (formerly TransferWise)** is the most commonly used method among foreign residents. For a transfer of ¥500,000, expect fees of approximately ¥1,500 to ¥3,000 depending on destination currency, with exchange rates close to mid-market. A standard bank international wire from MUFG or Japan Post Bank to an overseas account typically costs ¥2,500 to ¥5,000 per transaction plus potential intermediary bank fees on the receiving end. For transfers above approximately ¥1,000,000, your bank may request documentation explaining the transfer purpose. This is a standard anti-money laundering check, not an accusation. Prepare a simple letter explaining it is a personal relocation transfer. **Tax note:** Transferring your own money overseas is not a taxable event in Japan. However, if you are a long-term resident (generally 10 or more years in Japan) and have unrealized foreign asset gains, Japan's exit tax rules may apply to your total asset position. This is a complex area. For most short-to-medium-term foreign residents, it does not apply, but confirm with a tax professional if your total assets are significant. ---

Which Linked Services Must You Cancel Before the Account Can Close?

This is where the actual delays happen. Banks will sometimes refuse to close an account that still has active automatic payment (口座振替) contracts registered to it. Even if the bank does not technically block closure, leaving these active means payments will fail after closure, which can affect your credit record, utilities, or pension refund routing. > ### Reality Check > A Canadian teacher on a Specialist in Humanities visa discovered three weeks before departure that her Suica card was linked to her Rakuten Bank account for auto-charge. After closing the bank account first, the Suica stopped auto-charging at turnstiles, which was a minor inconvenience. More seriously, her pension lump-sum refund (脱退一時金) was still routed to that account. Cancelling the account before redirecting the pension refund destination meant the payment bounced and required a manual reprocessing request through the Japan Pension Service, adding six weeks to the refund timeline. See the [pension refund tax guide](https://mykyoten.com/guides/pension-refund-tax-20-percent-japan) for how lump-sum withdrawals and the 20% withholding tax interact before you leave. **Cancel or redirect these before closing:** - **Salary direct deposit**: notify your employer's payroll team in writing at least one pay cycle ahead - **Resident tax automatic payment**: contact your ward or city office to arrange final payment by bank transfer before departure, or settle in cash - **National pension and health insurance**: cancel automatic payment at city hall as part of your move-out procedure; see the [pension and social insurance guide](https://mykyoten.com/guides/japanese-pension-social-insurance-foreigners) for details - **Pension lump-sum refund routing**: update the destination account at Japan Pension Service (日本年金機構) to an overseas account before your account closes - **Suica and IC card auto-charge**: unlink from bank account via the transit app or Suica machine; refund your Suica balance at a station service counter - **Investment and securities accounts (証券口座)**: these must be closed separately; they cannot be processed at the bank branch; contact your securities company directly - **Credit cards**: cancel cards using this account as payment source; outstanding balances must be fully settled first - **Utility contracts**: most utilities allow final payment by cash or convenience store; cancel direct debit before closure **What if a payment bounces after you have already left?** The service provider will typically attempt to collect by mail to your registered address. Since your Japanese address no longer exists after move-out, the notice goes nowhere. In practice, unpaid small utilities rarely create cross-border collection issues for most short-term residents, but it is poor practice and can complicate any future return to Japan on a new visa. **Can I close my Japanese bank account after I have already returned to my home country?** Some banks allow overseas closure by international post with notarized documents, but this is not a standardized service and varies significantly by institution. Shinsei Bank has historically supported this, Japan Post Bank generally does not. Expect 4 to 12 weeks. Contact your specific bank's overseas support line before assuming this is possible. **What happens to my money if I never close the account?** After a period of inactivity (typically around 10 years), accounts are classified as dormant under Japanese banking law. Fees may apply during this period. Recovery of dormant funds requires submitting a formal claim in Japan, usually in person or with a Japanese-language notarized power of attorney issued to a proxy. It is recoverable in theory but practically difficult from overseas. **Do I need to close my account before or after the city hall move-out (転出届)?** Before, if possible. Your residence card remains technically valid until expiry or departure, but city hall move-out changes your registered status. Some bank tellers will check whether your address is still active in the system. Closing the account before submitting your move-out notice avoids potential complications at the branch counter. --- If you are working through your departure checklist, the [Leaving Japan Calculator](https://mykyoten.com/tools/leaving-japan-calculator) helps you organize every step by date, including bank closure, pension refund applications, and city hall procedures.
