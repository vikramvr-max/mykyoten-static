PR removes the single biggest source of stress in a foreigner's life in Japan: visa dependency. You are no longer tied to an employer, a spouse, or a specific job category. Your right to live in Japan becomes independent of any external condition.

What You Gain: You gain unrestricted work rights. Any job, any industry, any number of hours. You can start businesses, switch careers, freelance, or stop working entirely without jeopardizing your legal status. Banks treat PR holders differently when it comes to mortgages and large loans. And you gain psychological stability. No more renewal anxiety. The visa renewal process becomes a non-issue once you have PR.

What Does NOT Change: You are still a foreign national. You cannot vote. You still carry a residence card. You still need a re-entry permit for trips longer than one year. You still pay taxes and pension as before. And critically, PR can be revoked. It is a status of residence with no expiration date, but it comes with conditions.

There are several routes to PR, and they have very different timelines. Knowing which one applies to you determines when you should start preparing.

This is the default. You need 10 continuous years of residence (see reporting compliance requirements to ensure continuity) in Japan, with at least 5 of those years on a work visa or qualifying residence status (not student, not trainee). Student years do not count toward the 5-year work requirement even if you held a part-time job.

Continuous means continuous, and immigration is specific about what breaks it. A single trip abroad longer than 3 months breaks continuity outright and resets your clock to zero. Multiple shorter trips totaling more than 150 days in a year also draw scrutiny. Based on common experience, stays under 30 days are rarely questioned, while trips of 60 to 90 days need solid documentation of your reason for travel. Your 5 work years do not need to be consecutive, but they must fall within the 10-year continuous residence window.

If you score 70 points or more on Japan's HSP points system, you can apply for PR after 3 years of residence. Score 80 or more, and the wait drops to just 1 year.

Here is the catch: you do not need to currently hold an HSP visa to use this pathway. If you can demonstrate that you would have qualified for 70 or 80 points at the relevant time in the past (3 years ago or 1 year ago), you can apply under this route even while holding a regular work visa. This is a detail that many applicants miss entirely.

Points come from five categories on the official METI point calculation sheet: academic background, professional career, annual salary, age, and bonus factors. A doctoral degree adds 30 points, a master's adds 20. Age under 30 adds 15 points, under 35 adds 10. Salary bands are tiered by age, so identical salaries score differently depending on how old you are.

The number that trips people up: your points must be verifiable at the time you submit the application, not just at the time your original HSP visa was granted. If your salary has dropped, or your employer lost a certification your bonus points depended on, immigration scores you on what they can confirm from your submitted documents on application day, not on your history. A drop from 80 to 70 points simply moves you to the 3-year track, it is not a rejection, but it changes your timeline.

Married to a Japanese national? You can apply after just 1 year of residence on a spouse visa, provided you have been married for at least 3 years total. This includes time married while living outside Japan. This is the second-fastest pathway after HSP 80 points.

Timing is more strategic than most people realize. Applying the moment you hit the minimum residence requirement is not always the smartest move.

The Risk of Applying Too Early: If you apply with exactly 10 years of residence but your financial records from year 7 show a gap in pension payments, you have a problem. Immigration reviews your entire history, not just the most recent year.

Tax Year Timing Matters: Your most recent tax certificate will be part of your application. This document reflects the previous fiscal year. If you had unusually low income last year, or changed jobs mid-year, consider waiting until the next tax year so you can submit a stronger certificate.

Strengthening tip: If you are within a year of applying, freeze your financial profile. Do not take on new debts, do not change jobs unless necessary, and make sure every pension and tax payment is on time.

This sounds dramatic, but it is accurate. The single most common reason for PR rejection is financial record problems. Not criminal issues. Not insufficient residency. Financial compliance. This includes proper job change reporting and maintaining clean records.

There is no published statutory minimum income for permanent residency. What immigration applies is an administrative standard, and based on official guidance and reports from foreign residents who have navigated this process, ¥3 million per year is the widely recognized floor for a single applicant with no dependants. That figure rises with household size: a spouse who does not work pushes the practical range to ¥3.5 to 4 million, a family of three to roughly ¥4 million, and a family of four to ¥4.5 million or higher, with officers looking more closely at stability the larger the household. These are not official published figures, they are practical thresholds reported consistently across immigration forums and confirmed by licensed administrative scriveners (gyoseishoshi), but they are worth treating as real.

The type of income matters as much as the amount. A salaried employee at a stable Japanese company with full social insurance enrollment presents a very different risk profile than a freelancer reporting the same number. Salary and consistent bonuses count. Rental income counts if declared on your tax return with a paper trail. Spousal income can also count toward the household picture, and is an underused option, even when your spouse is not the primary applicant.

Immigration will request your tax payment certificates going back multiple years. They want to see that you paid the correct amount, on time, for every year. Late payments show up on these certificates. Even payments made one month late can appear as delinquent for the period in which they were due. Understanding resident tax obligations is critical before applying.

Pension compliance has become significantly more important in recent years. Immigration now routinely requests your nenkin records. If you were on national pension at any point and missed payments, those gaps are visible. If you switched between national pension and employees' pension during job transitions and there is an uncovered month in between, that shows up too.

Many foreigners assumed early in their time in Japan that pension did not matter. If you have gaps, you can retroactively pay up to 2 years of missed national pension contributions. Do this before you apply. Learn more about the pension withdrawal system if you're leaving Japan.

Strengthening tip: Request your own nenkin record from the pension office and your nozei shomeisho from the city office before you apply. Review them yourself. Fix any discrepancies before immigration sees them.

Points come from five categories: academic background, professional experience, annual salary, age, and bonus items (Japanese language ability, degree from a Japanese university, patents, etc.). You need 70 for the 3-year fast track or 80 for the 1-year track.

Where People Miscalculate: The most common miscalculation is salary. The points system uses specific salary brackets, and the number immigration cares about is your contracted annual compensation, not your take-home pay. Make sure you have permission for activities outside your visa before pursuing side income.

Strengthening tip: If you are close to 70 or 80 points, take the JLPT. A JLPT N1 certificate adds 15 points. N2 adds 10 points. For many applicants, this single action pushes them over the threshold and shaves years off their waiting time.

A PR application is not just a stack of papers. It is a narrative. Every document either strengthens or weakens the story of a stable, contributing, long-term resident.

The Guarantor: You need a guarantor who is a Japanese national or PR holder. A spouse works. An employer or senior colleague works. A friend with shaky finances does not help your case.

The Employer Letter: Your employer's letter should state your position, your tenure, your salary, and ideally a line about expected continued employment. A vague letter that omits salary or role details is not enough.

What Makes Applications Look Weak: Inconsistencies between documents. If your tax certificate shows a different income than your employer letter, that raises questions. Immigration officers cross-reference. Assume they will find any discrepancy.

Rejections follow clear patterns:

Tax delinquency is the top reason. This includes late payments, underpayments, and years where you failed to file a tax return.

Job instability is the second most common issue. Immigration wants to see that you can sustain yourself indefinitely.

Visa status irregularities matter too. If you overstayed even briefly in the past, or worked outside the conditions of a previous visa, that history is in the system.

Case Study: David, a Canadian software engineer, applied for PR after 10 years in Japan. Strong salary, stable job, Japanese spouse. His application was rejected. The reason: he had changed addresses three times in two years with late notifications, plus one uncovered pension month during a job transition. Always follow the 14-day address change reporting rule. After correcting the pension gap and maintaining a stable address for 14 months, he reapplied and was approved in 4 months.

You can reapply immediately. There is no mandatory waiting period. However, resubmitting the same application without addressing the issues is pointless. Immigration keeps records of previous applications.

How to Repair and Reapply: First, request the reason for rejection. Second, systematically address every issue. If it was financial, get your records clean and wait until the next tax year so you can submit updated certificates.

What if scenario: You are rejected and then change jobs. This can actually help if the new job is higher paying and more stable. Wait until you have been at the new company for at least a year, then reapply.

If you want to keep your original nationality and maintain flexibility to live in other countries, PR is the right choice. If you plan to live in Japan permanently, want voting rights, and are willing to give up your current passport, naturalization may make more sense.

For most foreigners, PR is the practical choice because it does not require renouncing anything.

Yes. PR can be revoked. Most people do not think about this until it becomes relevant.

Long Absences: If you leave Japan for more than 1 year without a re-entry permit, your PR expires automatically. With a re-entry permit, you can stay abroad for up to 5 years, but immigration can still question whether you have genuinely maintained Japan as your primary residence. Understand the visa revocation procedures to avoid risks.

Criminal Issues: A criminal conviction in Japan can lead to PR revocation and deportation. Drug offenses are treated with extreme severity.

Tax Delinquency After Approval: If you stop paying taxes or pension after receiving PR and these delinquencies become severe, revocation is possible.

Before applying, verify each of these items is clean:

Tax: Filed and paid for all years of Japanese residence. No late filings. No underpayments.

Pension: No uncovered months. No late payments. Retroactively paid any gaps within the 2-year window.

Address: Stable address history. All changes reported within 14 days.

Employment: Consistent employment history. If changes exist, documented clearly.

Income: Consistent salary trajectory. Tax certificates match employer letters.

Visa history: No overstays. No unauthorized work. All renewals filed on time.

Documentation: Guarantor confirmed. Employer letter contains role, tenure, salary, and contract status. All documents consistent with each other.
