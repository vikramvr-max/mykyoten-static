You know you're supposed to report your job change to immigration. You've heard that much. But when exactly are you supposed to do it? Within a week? A month? Before you start the new job or after? If you've tried searching online for a clear answer, you've probably found a frustrating mix of advice. Some sources say one thing, others say something completely different, and the official immigration website might use language that feels vague or technical. This inconsistency leads to a common problem: people hesitate, unsure of what's correct, and end up delaying their report longer than they should. The confusion isn't helped by the fact that different visa types sometimes have different rules, and what applies to your colleague might not apply to you. Add in language barriers and the general intimidation of dealing with immigration bureaucracy, and it's easy to see why so many people feel stuck. What Immigration Requires Japanese immigration does set specific timeframes for reporting employment changes, and these deadlines apply regardless of how minor or temporary the change might feel to you. For most visa categories, you're required to report a job change within 14 days of when it happens. This means if you start a new position on March 1st, you should submit your notification to immigration by March 14th at the latest. The key thing to understand is that immigration expects timely updates because they need to maintain accurate records of what everyone on a work visa is actually doing. Your visa was granted based on specific activities and a specific employer or situation. When that changes, they want to know about it. Even if you're moving to a similar role in the same industry, or even within the same company but to a different branch or position, the reporting requirement often still applies. What Happens If You Miss the Deadline Here's something that might ease some of your worry: missing the deadline doesn't automatically result in punishment or visa revocation. Immigration doesn't send enforcement officers to your door on day 15. However, delayed reporting does increase your risk, particularly when it comes time to renew your visa. Immigration tends to look at patterns of behavior rather than isolated incidents. A single late report that you corrected on your own might not raise concerns. But multiple late reports, or a pattern of ignoring requirements, signals that you're not taking your obligations seriously. Why This Comes Up During Renewal The reason timing issues cause problems is that they often remain completely invisible until you apply to renew your visa. Everything seems fine. You're working, paying taxes, going about your life. Then you submit your renewal application, and immigration reviews your complete record. That's when they identify missing notifications or reports that came in weeks or months late. From their perspective, they're seeing a compliance gap. From your perspective, it feels like a problem appearing out of nowhere, but really it's been sitting there in your file the whole time. How to Handle Late Reporting If you've already missed the deadline, don't panic and don't ignore it. Reporting late is almost always better than not reporting at all. Submit the notification as soon as you realize the mistake. You might include a brief explanation about why it's late, but keep it simple and honest. Proactive correction shows good faith. It demonstrates that you're trying to comply with the rules even if you made a mistake. This counts for something during renewal reviews. Immigration generally distinguishes between people who make occasional errors but correct them and people who consistently ignore their obligations. How to Stay Organized The simplest way to avoid these problems is to treat job changes as time-sensitive events, similar to how you'd treat a visa expiration date. When you accept a new position, immediately note the start date and calculate your reporting deadline. Set a reminder on your phone or calendar. Gather the necessary documents right away. Submit your notification well before the deadline if possible. Keep copies of everything you submit, including confirmation receipts if you file online.
