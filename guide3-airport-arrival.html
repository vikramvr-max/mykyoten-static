When This Actually Applies This guide is for people entering Japan on a medium or long-term visa for the first time after approval. If you're arriving on a tourist waiver, none of this applies - you just go through the tourist line and leave. If you're a returning resident re-entering Japan, you already have your residence card and the process is even simpler. What the Airport Actually Does (It's Less Than You Think) Japan uses the airport to verify you're legally allowed to enter under the visa status you were granted. They check your passport, check your visa, issue your residence card, and send you on your way. The airport does not: Register your address Enroll you in health insurance Issue your My Number Verify your job contract in detail Care about your long-term housing plan All of those things are handled by your local city hall after you leave the airport. The airport's only job is to confirm you can legally enter and give you the documents to prove it. See the complete arrival checklist for what comes next. The Immigration Inspection: What Actually Happens After you get off the plane, you follow signs to immigration. There are separate lines for Japanese nationals, re-entry permit holders, and foreign visitors. You want the "Foreign Nationals" line. You'll wait in line. How long depends on how many flights landed at the same time. At Narita during peak hours, this can be 30-60 minutes. At Haneda during off-peak hours, it might be 10 minutes. When you reach the counter, hand the immigration officer your passport and any entry forms they gave you on the plane (some airlines still use paper forms, some have gone digital). The Questions They Ask The officer will ask basic questions. Common ones include: "What is the purpose of your visit?" "How long will you stay?" "Where will you be staying?" "Do you have a job in Japan?" How to answer: Keep it simple and consistent with your visa type. If you're on a work visa: "I'm here for work. I'll be working at [company name]." If you're on a student visa: "I'm here to study. I'll be attending [school name]." If you're on a working holiday visa: "I'm here on working holiday. I'll be staying in [area/guesthouse name initially]." The officer is not interrogating you. They're checking that your answers match your visa paperwork. Don't volunteer extra information. Don't ramble. Short, direct answers are fine. What If You Don't Have a Permanent Address Yet? This is the question that stresses everyone out unnecessarily. If the officer asks where you'll be staying and you don't have a permanent address yet, just tell them where you're staying initially. A hotel name. A guesthouse. A friend's place. Whatever is true. "I'll be staying at [hotel name] in Shinjuku initially" is a perfectly acceptable answer. The officer does not care if this is temporary. They're not checking if you have your entire life figured out. They just want to know you're not homeless the moment you land. "The airport does exactly one thing: confirms your visa is valid and issues your residence card. Everything else happens later at city hall." Getting Your Residence Card: The Two Scenarios At major international airports in Japan, residence cards are issued immediately at immigration. These typically include: Narita Haneda Kansai Chubu (Nagoya) Fukuoka New Chitose (Sapporo) Always check your port of entry in advance if you are unsure. The card has your name, date of birth, nationality, residence status, and period of stay. The back of the card has a space for your address, but it's blank when you receive it. This is normal. Your address gets added later when you register at city hall . The Regional Airport Problem If you land at a smaller regional airport like Fukuoka, Sapporo, or Naha, immigration does not issue the residence card on the spot. Instead, they stamp your passport and give you a notification paper. Landing at a regional airport creates a timing issue. You need to register your address at city hall within 14 days, but you won't have your physical residence card yet. Here's how it works: You go to city hall with your passport (which has the entry stamp and visa) and register your address using that. The city hall staff know this situation and handle it routinely. Your residence card then gets mailed to that registered address. Some landlords get confused by this and insist on seeing a residence card before finalizing a lease. If this happens, explain that you landed at a regional airport and show them the notification paper from immigration. Most landlords are familiar with this, but occasionally you'll encounter someone who isn't. If the landlord still refuses, you have two options: find a different landlord, or register a temporary address (hotel, guesthouse, friend's place) to receive the card, then update your address later when you move into the apartment. This is standard for all foreign nationals entering on long-term visas. You place your index fingers on the scanner, look at the camera, and that's it. The whole process takes 30 seconds. Some people worry this is invasive or unusual. It's not. Japan has done this for over 15 years. The biometric data is tied to your residence card and used for re-entry verification. If you have a physical condition that makes fingerprinting difficult (bandaged fingers, severe scarring, etc.), tell the officer. They have alternative procedures. What Happens After Immigration After you clear immigration and receive your residence card (or notification), you collect your checked luggage and go through customs. Customs in Japan is straightforward. You fill out a customs declaration form (paper or digital) stating what you're bringing into the country. If you have nothing to declare, you go through the green "nothing to declare" lane. If you have items that need declaration (large amounts of cash, certain foods, etc.), you go through the red lane. Most people go straight through the green lane with no issues. Getting From the Airport to the City All major Japanese airports have train connections to the city center. At Narita, you have the Narita Express (N'EX) or Keisei Skyliner. At Haneda, you have the Tokyo Monorail or Keikyu Line. At Kansai, you have the JR Haruka or Nankai Rapi:t. Frugal option: The airport limousine buses are often cheaper than express trains and drop you directly at major hotels or stations. The Narita-Tokyo limousine bus costs around ¥3,200. For Haneda, the regular Keikyu line (not the limited express) costs ¥300-¥500 depending on your destination, significantly cheaper than the monorail. If you have a lot of luggage, the bus is easier because you don't have to navigate train station stairs and turnstiles. IC card tip: You can buy a Suica or Pasmo card at the airport and load it with money. This works for all trains and buses. Saves you from buying individual tickets every time. The Work Permission Stamp (If You're on a Student Visa) If you're entering on a student visa and plan to work part-time, you need to apply for permission to engage in activities outside your status of residence. This is usually called the "work permission" stamp. You can apply for this at the airport right after you receive your residence card, or you can apply later at an immigration office. Applying at the airport is faster and more convenient because you're already there. Look for the "Permission to Engage in Activity Other than That Permitted" counter (usually near the immigration area). Fill out a simple form, hand over your residence card, and they'll stamp your card with the work permission. This takes 5-10 minutes. The catch: Not all airports have this service available 24/7. Narita and Haneda usually have staff available during daytime hours, but if you land late at night, the counter might be closed. In that case, you'll need to go to an immigration office later. Edge Case Scenarios That Stress People Out Scenario 1: What If My Flight Lands After Midnight? Immigration operates 24/7 at all major airports. You will be processed regardless of when you land. The work permission counter might be closed if you land late, but immigration itself never closes. The challenge is getting from the airport to your accommodation. Most trains stop running around midnight. You'll need to take a taxi (expensive - expect ¥15,000-¥25,000 from Narita to central Tokyo) or wait in the airport until trains start running again around 5am. Frugal option: If you know you're landing late, book a hotel near the airport for the first night. Airport hotels are cheaper than city center hotels, and you can take the train into the city the next morning when you're rested. Scenario 2: What If Immigration Asks Detailed Questions About My Job? If you're entering on a work visa, the officer might ask about your job, your employer, or your salary. This usually happens when there's something unclear in your paperwork or if your visa category requires additional verification. Answer honestly and simply. If you have your employment contract or offer letter with you (in your carry-on), you can show it if asked, but this is rarely necessary. If the officer asks a question you don't understand, it's okay to say "I'm sorry, I don't understand. Could you repeat that?" Immigration officers at major airports deal with foreigners all day and are used to language barriers. If there's a serious communication issue, you can ask for an interpreter. Major airports have multilingual staff available. Scenario 3: What If They Spell My Name Wrong on the Residence Card? Check your residence card immediately when you receive it. Make sure your name, date of birth, and visa status are correct. If there's an error, tell the immigration officer right away before you leave the counter. They can reissue the card on the spot. This happens occasionally, especially with names that have unusual spellings or non-Latin characters. If you don't notice the error until later, you'll need to go to an immigration office to get it corrected. This is annoying and time-consuming, so check the card carefully before you walk away. Scenario 4: What If I Lose My Residence Card at the Airport? If you somehow lose your residence card between receiving it and leaving the airport (this is rare but not impossible), go back to the immigration area immediately and report it. They can reissue the card, but you may need to file a lost item report with airport security first. This will delay your departure from the airport by an hour or more. If you lose your residence card after leaving the airport, you need to report it to the police (to get a lost item certificate) and then apply for a reissue at an immigration office. This takes 1-2 weeks and costs ¥1,600. Don't lose your residence card. Keep it in a secure place like your passport wallet. ⚠️ WARNING: Your Entry Date Starts the Clock Your official entry date (the date stamped in your passport) starts several administrative deadlines: 14 days to register your address at city hall 14 days to enroll in National Health Insurance (if applicable) 90 days to report your employer to immigration (if on a work visa) These deadlines do not start when you "get settled" or "find an apartment." They start the moment you pass through immigration. Missing the address registration deadline is technically a violation of immigration law and can result in fines or complications with visa renewal. Don't procrastinate on post-arrival admin tasks. What People Assume vs What Actually Happens People assume: The airport will ask for proof of accommodation, bank statements, return tickets, or long-term plans. What actually happens: Immigration asks 2-3 basic questions, checks your passport matches your visa, and issues your residence card. The whole interaction takes 5 minutes. People assume: They need to speak fluent Japanese to get through immigration. What actually happens: Immigration officers at major airports speak English. At regional airports, English ability varies, but the process is so standardized that language is rarely an issue. People assume: The airport will register them as a resident and issue all necessary documents. What actually happens: The airport only issues your residence card. Everything else (address registration, health insurance, My Number) happens at city hall after you leave the airport. Why the Airport Feels More Stressful Than It Is The airport is the first moment where your move to Japan becomes administratively real. Until this point, everything has been planning and preparation. At the airport, an immigration officer is looking at you and your documents and making a decision about whether you can enter. That creates psychological pressure even though the actual process is routine. Here's the reality: If your visa was approved by the embassy, the airport approval is almost automatic. The embassy already did the hard verification. The airport is just confirming you're the same person who was approved and that nothing has changed. Refusals at the airport are extremely rare and usually only happen when: Your visa has expired since approval (check your dates) Your passport expires within 6 months (some countries) You have a serious criminal record that wasn't disclosed There's a clear discrepancy between your visa paperwork and your answers How Long Does the Whole Process Take? If you landed recently: Go to your local city hall and register your address within 14 days of your entry date.
