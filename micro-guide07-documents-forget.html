Commonly Forgotten Items Your residence card is required for nearly every city hall procedure, yet people sometimes leave it at home, thinking their passport will be enough. While your passport is also often required, especially for procedures involving family registration or moving, your residence card is the primary form of identification and must be presented along with your passport in many situations. Notification slips or certificates from previous procedures get overlooked surprisingly often. When you move from one city to another, you need the moving-out certificate issued by your previous city hall. Without it, the moving-in process becomes much more complicated. People forget these slips because they received them weeks earlier and filed them away somewhere safe. Completed forms are another common issue. Some city halls require you to fill out forms in advance, either downloaded from their website or picked up during a previous visit. Showing up without the completed form means you'll need to fill it out on the spot, which takes extra time and can be confusing if the form is only in Japanese. Translation documents catch many people off guard. If you're registering a marriage, birth, or other family status change that involves foreign documents, you'll need official Japanese translations of certificates. The translation doesn't necessarily need to be done by a professional. You can translate it yourself, but it must include the translator's name. Many people bring the original foreign document but forget the translation entirely. Health insurance cards, pension books, or My Number cards might be needed depending on what you're doing. If you're moving out of Japan or changing your insurance status, these documents become essential. Physical Documents Still Matter Japan's administrative system still heavily relies on physical paperwork. Even though many records exist digitally in government databases, city hall procedures often require you to present original physical documents. A photo of your residence card on your phone won't work. A photocopy of your passport usually won't work either. They need the actual physical items. This can feel outdated, especially if you're coming from a country where most government services have moved entirely online. But in Japan, that physical verification step remains crucial. How to Prepare Properly Over-prepare rather than under-prepare. Before every city hall visit, look up the specific requirements on the city hall's official website. Many municipalities have English-language guides that list exactly what you need to bring. If the website isn't clear or only available in Japanese, call ahead and ask. Print out the instruction page or checklist and bring it with you. Having the requirements in writing helps you double-check before you leave home and gives you something to show the staff if there's any confusion. Bring extra identification beyond what's explicitly required. Having both your residence card and passport, even if only one is mentioned, gives you backup options if questions arise. If you have a My Number card, bring that too. Assume each visit is unique and don't rely solely on what you needed last time. City hall procedures change, requirements get updated, and what worked for your address registration might not apply to your pension enrollment or marriage notification. Keep important documents organized in a dedicated folder at home. When you receive certificates, notification slips, or other paperwork from city hall, file them immediately in a place you'll remember.
