When This Actually Applies This guide applies after you've registered your address at city hall . How Japan Structures Health Insurance (Two Main Paths) Japan has universal health coverage, but it's not a single unified system. Instead, there are two main insurance schemes, and which one applies to you depends on your employment situation. Path 1: Employee Health Insurance (健康保険 - Kenkō Hoken) - If you work for a company that enrolls you in employee health insurance, your employer handles everything. This applies to most full-time employees and some part-time workers who meet certain hour thresholds. Path 2: National Health Insurance (国民健康保険 - Kokumin Kenkō Hoken) - If you're not covered by employer insurance, you're automatically enrolled in National Health Insurance (NHI) through your city hall. This includes students, freelancers, unemployed people, and part-time workers whose employers don't provide insurance. You don't get to choose between these two. Your employment situation determines which system you're in. Employer-Provided Health Insurance: How It Works If your employer provides health insurance, they enroll you through the company's payroll system. This usually happens within your first month of employment. How you know you're enrolled: Your employer deducts health insurance premiums from your paycheck You receive a health insurance card (保険証 - hokenshō) in the mail within 2-3 weeks The card shows your employer's insurance provider name (like Kyokai Kenpo, company-specific mutual aid associations, etc.) What you pay: Premiums are based on your salary and split between you and your employer (roughly 50/50). The exact percentage varies slightly by insurance provider but averages around 10% of your salary total, with you paying 5%. For example, if your monthly salary is ¥300,000, your health insurance deduction is roughly ¥15,000 per month. When it starts: Your enrollment starts from your address registration date, not from when you receive the card. If you register your address on April 15, your coverage starts April 15, even if your card doesn't arrive until May. What Health Insurance Actually Covers Japanese health insurance covers 70% of most medical costs. You pay the remaining 30% at the point of care. What's covered: Doctor visits and consultations Diagnostic tests (blood tests, X-rays, MRIs, etc.) Prescription medications Hospital stays Surgery Most dental work (cleanings, fillings, basic procedures) Maternity care What's usually NOT covered: Cosmetic procedures Elective plastic surgery Some advanced dental work (implants, whitening) Certain experimental treatments Glasses and contact lenses (though eye exams are covered) Normal pregnancy and childbirth (but complications are covered, and there are separate childbirth subsidies) Cost examples with insurance: Regular doctor visit: ¥1,500-¥3,000 (you pay 30% of ¥5,000-¥10,000 total) Prescription medication: ¥500-¥2,000 depending on what's prescribed X-ray: ¥1,000-¥2,000 MRI: ¥3,000-¥6,000 Hospital stay (per day): ¥3,000-¥9,000 depending on room type Without insurance, these costs are 3-4x higher. "Your enrollment date is your address registration date. You owe premiums from that day forward, whether you received the card or not." What If You Don't Have Your Card Yet? If you need medical care before your insurance card arrives, you have two options: Option 1: Pay full price (100%) at the clinic, keep the receipt, and file for reimbursement later at your city hall or insurance provider. Bring your insurance card once you receive it plus the receipts. They'll refund you 70% of what you paid. Option 2: Tell the clinic you're waiting for your insurance card. Many clinics can look up your enrollment in the national database if you provide your name, date of birth, and address. They'll charge you the 30% co-pay and bill the insurance system directly. Option 2 is easier but not all clinics do this. Smaller clinics might insist on option 1. Edge Case Scenarios That Confuse People What If I Have a Job With Insurance But There's a Gap Before I Start? If you arrive in Japan on March 1, register your address, but your job doesn't start until April 1, you're automatically enrolled in NHI for the month of March. Once your employment starts April 1, your employer enrolls you in employee health insurance and you're removed from NHI. You'll receive an NHI premium bill for March only. Pay it. Don't ignore it thinking "but I have a job now." The systems track this and will keep billing you until you pay. What If I Switch Jobs? When you leave a job that provided health insurance, your coverage ends on your last day of employment. If there's any gap before your new job starts, you're automatically re-enrolled in NHI for that gap period. Your old employer should give you a "certificate of loss of qualification" (資格喪失証明書 - shikaku sōshitsu shōmeisho). Take this to city hall to register for NHI if there's a gap. If you start your new job within a few days and the new employer provides insurance, you might skip NHI entirely. If you switch jobs with no gap (last day Friday, start new job Monday), your new employer should handle the transition directly. What If I'm a Student Working Part-Time? If you're a student, you're on NHI unless your part-time job meets the thresholds for employer insurance enrollment (usually 20+ hours per week, earning over ¥88,000 per month, and meeting other conditions). Most student part-time jobs don't meet these thresholds, so students stay on NHI and pay the low student rate (around ¥2,000-¥3,000 per month). If your part-time employer does enroll you in their insurance, great. Your NHI automatically ends and you switch to employer insurance. ⚠️ WARNING: The Backdated Premium Trap If you registered your address months ago but never received your insurance card or premium bill (maybe because you moved addresses, or mail got lost, or you registered a temporary address), you still owe premiums from your original registration date. When the city hall finally catches up with you, they'll send a bill for all the months you weren't paying. This can be ¥20,000-¥100,000+ depending on how long it's been. You can sometimes negotiate a payment plan, but you cannot escape the debt. "I didn't know I was enrolled" is not a valid excuse in Japan's system. Register your address properly, update it when you move, and make sure you're receiving mail from city hall. How to Pay Your NHI Premiums (And Save Money) NHI premium bills arrive by mail every 2-3 months. Each bill covers multiple months (usually 2-4 months at a time). Payment methods: Convenience store: Take the bill to any convenience store and pay cash. No fee. Bank transfer: Pay at any bank. Small fee (¥100-¥200) depending on the bank. Automatic withdrawal: Set up automatic withdrawal from your Japanese bank account. No fee, and some municipalities offer a small discount (¥50-¥100 per bill) for using this method. Credit card: Some municipalities accept credit card payments online. Check your city website. Frugal tip: Setting up automatic withdrawal saves you the hassle of remembering to pay and might get you a small discount. Go to your city hall with your bankbook and seal to set it up. If You Can't Afford the Premiums If your income is very low or you've lost your job, you can apply for a premium reduction or exemption at city hall. Bring proof of your financial situation (income statements, unemployment paperwork, bank statements). The reduction can be 20%-70% off depending on your circumstances. Students, especially international students, often qualify for significant reductions. Ask at city hall. They don't advertise this, but it exists. When You Leave Japan Permanently If you're leaving Japan for good, go to city hall before you leave and submit a move-out notification (転出届 - tenshutsu todoke). This ends your health insurance enrollment and stops future premiums. If you don't do this and just leave the country, premiums keep accumulating and you'll owe money even though you're not in Japan anymore. Common Mistakes People Make Mistake 1: Thinking they can skip health insurance because they're young and healthy. You can't. It's legally required. You'll get billed eventually, and the longer you wait, the bigger the backdated bill. Mistake 2: Assuming travel insurance counts as Japanese health insurance. It doesn't. Travel insurance is supplementary. You still need Japanese health insurance by law. Mistake 3: Not updating their address when they move, then wondering why they're not receiving their insurance card or bills. Your insurance card and bills get mailed to your registered address. If you move and don't update your address at city hall, the mail won't reach you, but you're still enrolled and premiums still accumulate. Mistake 4: Ignoring premium bills thinking they'll go away. They don't. The city will keep billing you, add late fees, and eventually it can affect your visa renewal if you have large unpaid debts to the government. What to Do Right Now If you just registered your address: Wait 1-3 weeks for your insurance card to arrive. If it doesn't arrive within a month, go to city hall and ask about it. If you have a job starting soon: Ask your employer when your health insurance enrollment will be processed. If there's a gap between your arrival and your employment start, you're on NHI for that gap. If you're on NHI and received a premium bill: Pay it at a convenience store or set up automatic withdrawal to avoid forgetting. If you haven't received any insurance card or bills despite registering your address months ago: Go to city hall immediately. You're probably enrolled but not receiving mail.
