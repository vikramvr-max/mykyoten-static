Your work visa in Japan isn't tied to your job title. It's tied to the actual activities you perform. You might have "Marketing Manager" printed on your business card, but what immigration cares about is what you actually do each day. Are you creating marketing strategies? Or are you spending most of your time doing administrative work, manual tasks, or duties that fall outside the scope of your visa category? Each status of residence has its own eligibility requirements and permitted job types, and work outside the authorized scope of activities is prohibited unless a Permission to Engage in Activities Other Than Permitted is obtained. Your visa was approved based on specific types of professional activities, not whatever your employer decides to assign you. Why Roles Drift You get hired as a software engineer. Your visa was approved for that engineering work. But over time, your responsibilities start shifting. Maybe your team is small and everyone pitches in wherever needed. You start spending more time on customer support, basic administrative tasks, or working the sales floor during busy periods. Each change feels minor and reasonable in the moment. Job responsibilities often evolve slowly without anyone reviewing whether they still match your visa. Six months later, you might be doing work significantly different from what was described in your original application. From your perspective, you're being a helpful team player. From immigration's perspective, you might be working outside your permitted scope. How Immigration Evaluates Work Immigration evaluates actual duties performed, not job titles or intentions. When you apply for visa renewal, they want to understand what you actually do day to day. They might request detailed documentation about your work activities or question inconsistencies between your stated role and actual duties. The most common reason for denial is that the duties do not fall under a work-eligible status. This becomes problematic when companies use generalist hiring practices. Assigning new graduates to retail stores or factory rotations often triggers problems, even if the company intends for them to move into professional roles later. Work visas only cover work that requires high level professional knowledge or skills. Simple labor, manual work, or tasks that don't require specialized knowledge generally don't qualify. When Duties Expand Beyond Your Original Scope If your job responsibilities expand beyond what was originally approved, clarify whether additional permission is required. If the additional duties fall within the same scope already permitted under your visa, no additional permission is necessary. For example, if you were hired as an interpreter and occasionally take part-time interpreting work on the side, that stays within your approved scope. But if the additional duties fall outside your visa category, you need Permission to Engage in Activities Other Than Permitted. The permission requirements include that the side job must not interfere with the primary role, primary employment must be maintained, and the side job must not involve unskilled labor. What counts as staying within scope versus going outside it isn't always obvious. If you're on an Engineer/Specialist in Humanities/International Services visa and your role as a designer gradually expands to include some marketing work, that might still fall within the permitted range. But if you start spending significant time doing retail sales, warehouse work, or other manual tasks, that's likely outside your scope. How to Protect Yourself If you're uncertain whether your current duties match your visa, address it proactively. Talk to your employer's HR department about whether your actual work aligns with what was described in your visa application. If there's been drift or expansion beyond the original scope, discuss whether you need to apply for permission to engage in other activities. Don't wait until renewal to discover problems. If your job has changed significantly since your visa was granted, clarifying the situation now gives you time to fix it properly. Immigration values transparency and good-faith efforts to comply. Keep your own records of what you actually do at work. If questions arise during renewal, being able to clearly describe your duties and show they align with your visa category makes the process smoother.
